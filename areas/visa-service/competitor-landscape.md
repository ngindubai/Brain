# Competitor landscape

The space is layered, funded, and the one-click vault is already standard.

## The stack

- **VFS Global** - the giant. Operates in 166 countries, 528m+ transactions since 2001. Built around government contracts and physical application centres. Not your direct competitor, but it owns the heavy compliance logistics layer.
- **Atlys** - the funded consumer-app challenger. Founded 2021, over $76m raised (Series C $36m, March 2026, led by Susquehanna Asia VC, MakeMyTrip joined as investor). Around 700,000 visas a year, 150+ destinations. Acquired UK firm Artionis in February 2025. UAE, US, UK and Australia are now nearly half its business. Core features: reusable stored documents, bulk applications, HR-system integration, AI photo and passport checks, a support bot handling 38% of contacts. Revenue is a service charge per transaction.
- **iVisa** - the established digital intermediary. Operating since 2013, 2.6m+ travellers. Large Trustpilot presence. Green branding deliberately distinct from government blue, states openly that it is not a government site. Building toward an "ecosystem" platform: visas, health permits and insurance in one interface, with vaulted documents raising switching costs.
- **VisaHQ** - older, human review, registered with many embassies, positioned for business travellers and complex cases.
- **Sherpa (joinsherpa)** - infrastructure and ancillary play. ML flags application errors before submission to government portals, sells into travel platforms via API.
- **SimpleVisa** - the one that matters most for the SEO thesis. Sells a white-label visa hub as a product, API or no-code, with the exact programmatic route-page architecture.
- **Long tail** - Visa2Fly, StampThePassport, Akbar Travels, and offline agents.

## The one-click vault is standard, not novel

Both leaders already do upload-once-apply-anywhere. Atlys explicitly markets reusable stored documents and bulk apply. iVisa's stated investment thesis is that once a user's documents are vaulted, switching cost is high and lifetime value rises. The feature you saw as the wedge is the incumbents' retention moat. Why this matters: [[feasibility-verdict]].

## The SEO route-page model is already productised

SimpleVisa publicly sells the playbook you described. Their recommended hub structure:

- /visa/ hub
- /visa/{destination}/ destination overview ("Do I need a visa for X")
- /visa/{destination}/{nationality}/ passport-specific page, which they call the highest-intent, highest-converting page
- supporting /fees/, /processing-time/, /documents/ templates

They also name the exact traps: thin templated pages across many combinations, index bloat, duplicate URLs. This is a documented, sold service, not a gap in the market. iVisa has ranked these terms for over a decade.
