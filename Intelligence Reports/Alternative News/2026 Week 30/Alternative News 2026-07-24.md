## Story 1: Prosecutor Investigating US Boat Strikes and Torture Claims Assassinated in Ecuador
**Source:** Drop Site News | https://www.dropsitenews.com/p/prosecutor-investigating-ecuador-boat-strikes-murdered
**Author:** Not confirmed (direct fetch blocked, see sourcing note) | **Published:** 24 July 2026 | **Read time:** ~6 min

Gloria Alexandra Bravo Cedeño, a prosecutor in Manta, Ecuador, was shot dead on 14 June 2026 as she left a café with her sister, who was also killed. A gunman on a motorcycle opened fire; officers arriving at the scene found five shell casings, Bravo's injured driver, and no sign of the police protection detail that had reportedly been assigned to her. Ecuador's Attorney General's Office has attributed the killing to organised crime "in retaliation" for prosecutorial work generally, without naming a specific case.

Bravo was the prosecutor assigned to investigate US military strikes on three Ecuadorian fishing vessels off the Galápagos between January and April 2026: La Fiorella, whose eight crew have not been found; and two further boats whose 36 survivors allege they were captured, hooded, held without medical care, and taken as far as El Salvador before being returned to Ecuador. Human Rights Watch had begun working with Bravo on the drone strikes investigation before her death and had interviewed her in May, when she reportedly told a researcher "the danger is always present." She is the third prosecutor killed in Manta since 2022; Ecuador's Observatory of Rights and Justice has recorded 26 killings of judges, prosecutors and justice-system personnel nationally since 2020, against a national homicide rate of 51 per 100,000.

Drop Site's report, per the search summary of it, places the killing the day after the US Congress formally demanded answers from Washington on the strikes — a detail that sharpens the "in retaliation for what work" question the Ecuadorian government's organised-crime framing leaves open. The wider campaign this feeds into is the Trump administration's declared campaign against drug-trafficking vessels in the Pacific and Caribbean, which rights monitors have tied to an estimated 180+ deaths and which Amnesty International and HRW have both said may constitute violations of international law. Drop Site and The Guardian jointly broke the survivors' torture allegations in April; this piece is the first to connect that torture reporting to the killing of the official who was meant to investigate it.

No named perpetrator, no confirmed link between the assassination and the boat-strikes case specifically, and Ecuadorian authorities have not indicted anyone. The strength of the story is the pattern it draws (timing, absent protection detail, HRW's independent confirmation that she was actively working the case) rather than a smoking-gun document.

## Declassified UK — first live content since the blockage (dated 23 July, not today)

Per the granularity rule this is not written up as a full story, since it is dated yesterday, but it is the first substantive Declassified UK content this routine has been able to locate in over a month and is worth logging in full here rather than as a passing mention. The Metropolitan Police has reversed an April 2026 decision not to investigate ten British nationals (four of them identifiable) accused of war crimes and crimes against humanity while serving in the IDF in Gaza, per a 240-page referral the Public Interest Law Centre and the Palestinian Centre for Human Rights submitted in August 2025. The Met told PILC last week it will now conduct "a fresh scoping exercise" on the four identified individuals. The reversal follows a threatened judicial review by British-Palestinian campaigner Lubna Speitan, represented by PILC. Morning Star and Middle East Monitor both republished/cited Declassified UK as the source; Declassified UK's own domain remains unreachable by direct fetch.
