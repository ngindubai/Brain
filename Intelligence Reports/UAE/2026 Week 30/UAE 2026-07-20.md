## Story 1: UAE non-oil trade hits a record Dh1.937 trillion in H1 2026, up 13.1% year-on-year

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/uae-non-oil-trade-nears-dh2tn-in-record-first-half-of-2026-1.500612911
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

The UAE's non-oil foreign trade reached Dh1.937 trillion in the first half of 2026, up 13.1% on the Dh1.728 trillion recorded in H1 2025. Non-oil exports specifically hit a record Dh452.8 billion, growing 23.9% year-on-year and now making up 23.4% of total trade, up from 21.3% a year earlier — exports growing meaningfully faster than trade overall, which is the healthier version of a trade-growth story. Trade with CEPA (Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement) partner countries alone totalled Dh304.3 billion. Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum called the figures "historic," framing them as evidence of "the world's confidence in the UAE."

This is a genuinely strong number rather than routine government messaging dressed up as one — a 13% trade-growth rate against a global backdrop that includes an active Strait of Hormuz crisis and elevated shipping-insurance costs is a real signal that the UAE's trade diversification strategy (CEPAs with a widening set of partners, non-oil export push) is holding up under stress rather than just performing well in calm conditions. For the wider portfolio this is background rather than a direct hit on any single vertical, but it's a useful macro anchor: whatever happens with Hormuz specifically, the broader non-oil economy the comparison network and company-formation-adjacent sites sit inside is still expanding at a healthy clip.

## Story 2: Dubai Customs' liquidity support package shows real uptake — Dh79m to 428 companies, 6,613 more given extended terms

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/markets/dubai-customs-dh79-million-in-liquidity-aid-for-private-sector-from-march-june-1.500613147
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Dubai Customs disbursed over Dh79 million in liquidity assistance to 428 companies between March and June, with a further 6,613 businesses benefiting from extended suspension periods on duty cases. The mechanism has three parts: an extra 120 days to settle suspended customs duties on re-export, temporary-admission and transit goods; installment plans instead of lump-sum duty payments; and an 80% reduction in penalties for eligible cases. The package also prioritised customs clearance for food and medicine and opened alternative trade routes through Khorfakkan, Fujairah and the Hatta Border Crossing.

The uptake numbers matter more than the headline figure here — 428 companies actually drawing on direct liquidity support and over 6,600 more using extended terms is a meaningfully large slice of Dubai's trading SME base, and it's a concrete data point that Dubai Customs is treating cash-flow pressure on private businesses as a real, current problem worth actively intervening on rather than a hypothetical one. Read alongside Story 1's export growth, it suggests the trade boom isn't universally easy money — enough firms needed liquidity support that the government built and publicised a formal package for it. Relevant background for the company-formation vertical: a regulator that visibly eases pressure on SMEs during a trade boom is a mildly positive signal for new formation activity, though not one to overstate.

## Story 3: TDRA updates spectrum rules for air taxis and delivery drones, laying groundwork for Advanced Air Mobility

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/aviation/uae-updates-rules-on-radio-frequencies-used-by-air-taxis-and-delivery-drones-1.500613071

The Telecommunications and Digital Government Regulatory Authority (TDRA) has approved updated spectrum frameworks — version 4.0 for aeronautical systems, version 3.0 for unmanned aircraft — defining dedicated radio frequency bands (including 5030-5091 MHz and satellite bands) for command-and-control links used by air taxis, delivery drones and electric aircraft. The update also sets out separate authorisation routes for short-range equipment versus full unmanned-aircraft control systems, and defines ground-infrastructure rules for vertiports and ground-control stations. It's explicitly a spectrum framework only — it doesn't itself authorise commercial services, which still need separate civil aviation approval.

This is plumbing, not a headline, but it's the kind of plumbing that has to exist before Dubai's stated ambition of commercial air-taxi services can move from demonstration flights to a licensed industry. No direct portfolio hit today, but worth a mental flag: any future logistics- or delivery-adjacent venture thinking about the UAE market now has a slightly clearer regulatory picture of what "commercial drone delivery" will require once the aviation-side approvals catch up to this spectrum groundwork.

## Story 4: Global central banks keep shifting reserves from dollars to gold — 27% of reserves now gold, up from 20% a year ago

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/markets/us-dollar-loosens-grip-on-central-bank-reserves-worldwide-as-gold-takes-over-1.500613257

ECB data through end-2025 shows gold now makes up 27% of global official reserves, up from 20% a year earlier, while US Treasuries fell to 22% from 25% over the same period — helped by a 64% gold price surge through 2025 as well as genuine buying. Dollar-denominated assets overall still account for 57% of global reserves, and the dollar remains dominant in trade invoicing (40-54% of global exports) and cross-border banking, so this is portfolio diversification rather than a dollar-abandonment story. No UAE-specific reserve breakdown was given in the piece.

This is global macro context rather than a UAE-specific story, but it's relevant background for anyone watching Gulf sovereign wealth and central bank positioning generally — the direction of travel (steady, multi-year reserve diversification into gold) is consistent with what's been reported out of several Gulf central banks and sovereign funds over the past two years. No action item, just useful context for the macro picture the Gulf economy sits inside.

## Story 5: Weaker rupee is redirecting Gulf NRI property money toward India's growth corridors, not just prestige addresses

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/property/a-weaker-rupee-is-stretching-gulf-nri-property-budgets-these-indian-markets-are-winning-1.500610911
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

A weaker Indian rupee is stretching the purchasing power of Gulf-based NRIs buying property in India, and the money is increasingly chasing infrastructure-linked growth corridors — Navi Mumbai, Panvel and Kharghar rather than central Mumbai, plus Bengaluru's Whitefield and Hyderabad's Financial District — rather than prestige postcodes. Premium apartments above ₹1 crore made up 62% of residential sales across India's seven largest cities in H1 2025, up from 51% a year earlier, and industry sources frame the currency and yield-gap dynamics as an active driver of the cross-border capital flow, not just a side effect of it.

GCC-based buyers remain among the most active NRI cohorts in this market. No direct read-through to any current Orwell property or comparison-network vertical, but it's a useful reminder that Gulf-resident wealth continues to flow outward into property as much as it flows into local UAE real estate — worth keeping in mind as background context for how much of the region's disposable capital is actually staying local versus being redeployed elsewhere.
