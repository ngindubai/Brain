## Story 1: Eleventh night of US strikes on Iran, ceasefire push falters in Pakistan, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia come under renewed fire

**Source:** Al Jazeera, Washington Post, CNBC | https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/21/us-launches-tenth-consecutive-night-of-attacks-on-iran ; https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2026/07/21/iran-us-hormuz-strait-war-july-20-2026/92883244-84d8-11f1-9cec-0fb26676f07e_story.html ; https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/21/us-iran-war-trump-hormuz-houthis.html
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

US Central Command completed its eleventh consecutive night of strikes on Iran in the early hours of today, hitting military operations centres, maritime attack capability, aircraft hangars, drone storage and logistics infrastructure, all framed as degrading Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. Regional mediators have put a 10-day ceasefire proposal to Washington and Tehran in talks running in Pakistan, an attempt to resurrect the memorandum of understanding that collapsed this week, but US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said publicly that Iran is "not serious about talks." President Trump has gone the other way rhetorically, threatening to expand strikes onto Iranian power infrastructure: "next week comes the power plants."

The fallout has spread wider than Iran itself. Kuwait's air defences engaged hostile drones early today, and Saudi Arabia intercepted a ballistic missile near Prince Sultan Air Base, which houses US personnel — both a continuation of the pattern flagged in yesterday's report (Iran striking Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, Jordan and Syria as the conflict widens beyond a straight US-Iran exchange) rather than a new front, but a reminder the geographic spread is not narrowing. US defence secretary Pete Hegseth told lawmakers the war has cost $37.5 billion so far and requested a further $67.1 billion in emergency funding, underlining that Washington is treating this as an extended commitment rather than a punitive strike campaign nearing its end.

Brent crude is trading around $91-92/bbl, a five-week high, up roughly 2% on the day; WTI is near $84.63. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the US Navy is prepared to provide "safe passage" escort for tankers through Hormuz "when it is appropriate and should it be needed" — the first concrete US statement on a naval escort option rather than pure strike posture, worth watching if it materialises given the direct read-through to marine war-risk pricing (Story 5).

## Story 2: Murban outpaces Brent as Abu Dhabi's own benchmark reflects Gulf-specific supply fear

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/energy/oil-tops-91-again-brent-hits-five-week-high-as-us-iran-conflict-houthi-threats-jolt-global-markets-1.500615853
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Murban crude — the ADNOC-produced benchmark that prices most Abu Dhabi exports and much of the Asian Middle East import market — jumped 4.9% to $85.77 today, comfortably outpacing Brent's 2% gain and WTI's 0.34% move. That gap matters: Murban outperforming the international benchmarks specifically reflects traders pricing in Gulf-origin supply risk rather than a generic global oil story, which is exactly the commodity UAE state revenue is most directly exposed to. Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthis have separately declared a maritime embargo against Saudi shipping, forcing reroutes and adding a second geographic pressure point (Red Sea/Bab-el-Mandeb) on top of Hormuz itself.

This sits against continued ADNOC commercial activity through the disruption rather than a halt: the company has sold roughly 16 million barrels of Emirati crude at wider discounts in its fifth spot tender since June, a sign Abu Dhabi is keeping volume moving into the market opportunistically even as headline prices climb, taking the discount rather than holding barrels back. Oil remains well below the peaks seen in the acute phase of the conflict, when Brent briefly cleared $120, so today's move is a re-pricing of risk rather than a fresh supply shock on that scale. US crude inventory data due later today was flagged by traders as the next near-term catalyst.

## Story 3: Gold clears $4,070 as safe-haven demand takes over from oil as the conflict's other trade

**Source:** Mitrade, FXStreet, VT Markets | https://www.mitrade.com/au/insights/news/live-news/article-2-1922427-20260722 ; https://www.fxstreet.com/news/gold-rebounds-above-4-050-as-safe-haven-demand-intensified-on-persistent-us-iran-tensions-202607212318
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Gold rallied through $4,070-4,071/oz today, up more than 1.5%, as the eleventh night of strikes and the stalled ceasefire push sent capital back into the metal after a brief retreat to the $4,000 level in the prior session. The move is being read as classic safe-haven flow rather than an inflation or dollar-weakness trade — it's tracking the conflict headlines almost tick for tick, with traders explicitly citing the Rubio "not serious" comment and the widened Houthi threat to Saudi shipping as the day's triggers.

Dubai's gold trade is not a side detail here: gold was the single largest commodity in UAE non-oil trade in H1 2026, and Dubai's retail and wholesale gold market (DMCC, Gold Souk, Dubai Gold & Commodities Exchange) sits directly on international spot pricing, so a sustained rally of this size feeds through quickly to walk-in demand, jewellery retailer margins and DGCX futures volumes. No portfolio angle here directly, but it's a market worth flagging as background if any Orwell venture touches gold trading, remittance, or high-net-worth retail in Dubai — today's move is real money changing behaviour, not noise.

## Story 4: Aviation suspensions deepen into autumn — and the binding constraint is now insurance, not government advisories

**Source:** Bloomberg, Khaleej Times | https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/insurance-woes-amid-us-iran-war-stall-airlines-return-to-dubai ; https://www.khaleejtimes.com/business/aviation/dubai-flights-suspension-air-france-other-airlines-update-iran-us-war
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

The picture that was still framed as short suspension "windows" in yesterday's report has hardened considerably. Air France's Beirut suspension now runs to 2 August, Riyadh to 24-25 July and Dubai to 27-28 July — but the wider carrier list has moved onto a different timescale entirely: Aegean, Airbaltic, Air Canada, ITA Airways and Singapore Airlines are now suspended into Dubai until 24 October, under an EASA Gulf-airspace warning, while British Airways, Lufthansa and SWISS are out until 13 September and WizzAir until mid-September. That is roughly 13 international carriers off Dubai routes for two to three months, not two to three weeks.

The more important detail is why. Dubai Airports CEO Paul Griffiths told Bloomberg directly that it is difficulty securing insurance, not UAE airspace restrictions or even government travel advisories on their own, that is now the practical barrier stopping some European carriers returning — insurers are unwilling or expensively unwilling to underwrite Gulf operations while the conflict is live. That reframes yesterday's open question (are these windows extending or lifting?) with a clearer answer: they are extending, and the mechanism is the insurance market rather than a UAE-side capacity problem. Emirates continues running close to 90% of its normal schedule and DXB remains near full capacity on the roughly 50 carriers still operating, so the domestic hub story is unchanged; the erosion is entirely on the foreign-carrier side.

## Story 5: Marine war-risk premiums fragment as the worst-exposed routes push toward 10% of hull value

**Source:** AGBI | https://www.agbi.com/analysis/shipping/2026/07/gulf-shipping-cover-still-available-as-premiums-hit-10/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Yesterday's report had marine war-risk premiums at roughly 3% of vessel value. The picture has fragmented since: pricing now ranges from around 3% up to 10% of hull value depending on the vessel, route and underwriter's own claims history, up from a pre-conflict baseline of about 0.25%. On a $100 million vessel, the top of that range is roughly $10 million for a single voyage, against roughly $250,000 before the war. Marsh's Marcus Baker notes the market has become genuinely uneven — some underwriters have taken repeated losses and are pricing accordingly, while others have gone months claim-free and are pricing far more competitively — which is a materially different market structure from a uniform "war surcharge" and makes shopping cover (and the case for a government-backed reinsurance backstop, as raised again today with reference to Ukraine's wartime marine scheme) more relevant, not less.

Underwriters still describe cover as available rather than withdrawn — this is a pricing story, not a market-closure story — and Baker's framing that war-risk cost on a Very Large Crude Carrier still works out to roughly five cents a barrel suggests affordability of the premium itself isn't the barrier stopping transits; the constraint is closer to physical risk tolerance and insurer capacity limits than cost. Directly relevant to InsureCompare and to anyone in the portfolio touching freight, logistics or trade finance: this is now a two-tier market, and the tier a shipper lands in depends on specifics (vessel flag, route, underwriter relationship) rather than a single quoted Gulf war-risk rate.
