## Story 1: Iran-US ceasefire "effectively collapsed," ninth consecutive night of strikes, Brent crosses $90

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/energy/brent-tops-90-after-us-iran-attacks-what-could-happen-next-1.500613793
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

The US has carried out a ninth consecutive night of strikes on Iranian military infrastructure, and Iran has responded with drone and projectile attacks — a Kuwaiti petroleum facility has taken significant drone damage, and a vessel caught fire northwest of Oman after being hit by an unidentified projectile. Iran has stated its ceasefire with the US has "effectively collapsed." Brent crude jumped 2.5%, touching as much as 3.8% intraday to cross $91 — its highest level in over a month — while WTI climbed toward $84-85.

Analyst Linh Tran (XS.com) puts $88-90 as the range if attacks start directly hitting ports, tankers or shipping infrastructure, treating $100 as an extreme-risk scenario rather than a base case; a de-escalation scenario could send prices back toward $78-80. OPEC+'s planned 188,000 barrel/day August production increase is a mitigating factor if safe transport holds. For the UAE specifically, the article notes fuel prices haven't moved in lockstep with the crude spike yet — some reporting even points to falling local petrol prices — but a prolonged disruption is the kind of thing that eventually shows up in grocery and transport costs regionally, not just at the pump.

This is the story to actually track this week rather than file away: nine consecutive nights of strikes is a materially different situation from the "$1/barrel Iran-Oman transit fee proposal" that's been parked in recent reports, and it's the driver behind both Story 2 (flight suspensions) and Story 3 (war-risk insurance) below.

## Story 2: European carriers pull out of Dubai and Riyadh as conflict escalates — but Dubai Airports says it's still running near full capacity

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/aviation/air-france-temporarily-halts-dubai-riyadh-flights-and-extends-beirut-suspension-1.500614725 and https://gulfnews.com/business/aviation/dubai-airport-operating-near-full-capacity-despite-regional-conflict-1.500614416
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Air France has suspended Dubai arrivals until 27 July (departures to 28 July), Riyadh until 24 July (departures to 25 July), and extended its existing Beirut suspension through 2 August, citing "the ongoing operational situation in the Middle East" and a "rapidly changing" security picture. Emirates, Etihad and Air Arabia have also cancelled some services, particularly to Kuwait, and European aviation safety advisories are contributing to airlines pulling back even where the airports themselves remain open.

Dubai Airports CEO Paul Griffiths pushed back on the idea that this amounts to a shutdown: the hub is "operating at pretty much full capacity," with daily traffic regularly above 200,000 passengers, roughly 6 million handled since the regional crisis began, and Emirates running at around 90% of its normal schedule. Only about 50 international carriers are currently operating — roughly half the usual roster — with some, including British Airways and Air France, held back more by government travel advisories and insurance difficulty than by any actual restriction on Dubai's own airspace. Airlines that are still flying are routing around the affected corridor via Saudi and southern airspace to keep connectivity up.

The split here matters for anyone reading the headlines: Dubai's aviation infrastructure is coping, the constraint is external (insurance, advisories, a subset of foreign carriers), not a UAE-side capacity problem. Worth watching whether the 24-28 July suspension windows get extended again once they lapse, which would be the real signal that this is settling in rather than resolving.

## Story 3: UAE expands war-risk insurance cover for homes, cars and cargo as marine premiums climb again

**Source:** web search (The National, Insurance Business, Gulf News) | https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/2026/07/17/war-risk-shipping-premium-surges-again-as-tensions-escalate-at-strait-of-hormuz/ ; https://gulfnews.com/business/banking/uae-residents-can-now-get-war-risk-cover-for-homes-cars-and-cargo-1.500540124
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Marine war-risk premiums are back on the rise — up to roughly 3% of a vessel's value by some reporting — as Iran's resumed tanker attacks (including strikes on two UAE-flagged supertankers, killing one sailor) sharply reduce Strait of Hormuz crossings; around 6,000 seafarers are reported stranded regionally while the IMO works on evacuation routes. Marsh's global head of marine, cargo and logistics, Marcus Baker, describes war rates as "roller-coastering" in line with oil prices — some early pricing improvement is visible, but underwriters want two to four weeks of confirmed de-escalation before rates meaningfully soften, with a full return to pre-conflict pricing potentially taking months even if that de-escalation holds.

Against that backdrop, UAE insurers have separately rolled out expanded war-risk cover specifically for households — homes, cars and cargo, not just commercial marine risk — a direct regulatory and product response to residents asking exactly the question a recent Gulf News piece posed: "will UAE insurance premiums rise if the conflict escalates further?" Today's escalation is the practical answer.

This is squarely relevant to [[Comparison Network]]'s InsureCompare vertical: rising and volatile premiums, a newly expanded consumer product category (household war-risk cover), and a population of residents actively searching for guidance on exactly this question is close to ideal conditions for a comparison-and-education content push, if InsureCompare has anything live or in planning that touches home/motor insurance. Worth a look at whether the site should be covering this now rather than after the news cycle moves on.

## Story 4: UAE rolls out Jaywan, its first national payment card scheme

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/banking/uae-rolls-out-jaywan-the-first-national-payment-card-banks-launch-new-schemes-1.500614102
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Jaywan — a domestic card scheme run by Al Etihad Payments, a Central Bank subsidiary — formally launched today, inaugurated by Sheikh Mansour bin Zayed Al Nahyan. First Abu Dhabi Bank is issuing Jaywan debit cards to eligible account holders, Commercial Bank of Dubai is offering Jaywan prepaid cards through corporate arrangements, Network International has enabled online merchant acceptance across thousands of UAE retailers, and Al Ansari Exchange is participating through its licensed exchange houses. The scheme supports debit, prepaid and credit products, with co-badged options for international use through partnerships with Visa, Mastercard, Discover and UnionPay — so this isn't a closed domestic-only system, more a sovereignty layer sitting underneath the existing international networks.

The stated rationale — keeping payment data and routing domestic, reducing reliance on international card networks for local transactions — is consistent with the broader "financial sovereignty" framing UAE banking policy has leaned on all year. No direct MoneyCompare angle today since this is card infrastructure rather than a consumer rate or product comparison, but worth watching for when Jaywan-branded cards start actually reaching comparison-relevant categories (cashback, rewards, fee structures) as banks build out the rest of the range.

## Story 5: Dubai Q2 2026 property data confirms volume down, price-per-square-foot up — market described as finding "true value"

**Source:** AGBI | https://www.agbi.com/analysis/real-estate/2026/07/dubai-property-sales-tumble-but-market-settling-at-true-value/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Dubai Land Department figures for Q2 2026 show just over 38,000 residential transactions, down nearly a third from the year-earlier peak, with sales value down about 40% year-on-year to AED110.4 billion ($30 billion). Against that, price per square foot is actually up 6.5%, and roughly 27,000 completed homes were delivered in the quarter — the highest five-year completion volume. Betterhomes and Savills both frame this as healthy normalisation rather than a broad correction: fewer transactions and fewer speculative off-plan launches letting pricing "find its true value" after total sales value grew 866% between 2020 and 2025.

This lands next to the ongoing distressed-deals narrative circulating in the wider property press (10-30% below-market discounts reported in pockets of Dubai Marina and Palm Jumeirah from motivated sellers), which is consistent with a market where volume is down but headline price metrics are holding — distress at the individual-seller level coexisting with resilience at the aggregate level. Worth flagging for the distressed deals group: the macro data doesn't contradict opportunistic below-market buying, it's the kind of environment (falling transaction counts, elevated completions, sellers wanting to exit) that tends to produce exactly those individual pockets of discount.

## Story 6: Abu Dhabi's Sanad posts a record Dh4.31 billion first half as global aircraft engine shortage drives MRO demand

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/aviation/abu-dhabis-sanad-posts-record-dh431-billion-as-airline-engine-demand-rises-1.500614015

Sanad, the Abu Dhabi aerospace engineering group, posted Dh4.31 billion in H1 2026 revenue, up 35% year-on-year, inducting 120 engines (+33.3%) and lifting delivery rates 53.8% despite ongoing global supply-chain constraints on parts. International customers made up 99% of revenue. The company is expanding well beyond traditional maintenance-repair-overhaul work into engine acquisition, testing, asset management and used-serviceable-material trading — its Asset Management division alone deployed roughly Dh165 million on engine purchases and a Trent 700 rebuild programme in its first full operating period.

Sanad is investing over Dh800 million in new capacity, including a Dh480 million Repair Center of Excellence in Al Ain (targeting 2030) and a new GTF engine facility (late 2028), and is targeting 300 annual shop visits by year-end with an explicit ambition to become the world's fifth-largest independent engine-MRO provider. This sits alongside the airline-suspension story above as a reminder that the same global aviation stress (parts shortages, capacity constraints) creating headaches for passenger carriers is a tailwind for Abu Dhabi's MRO ambitions specifically — no direct portfolio read-through today, but a solid marker of Abu Dhabi's aerospace-hub strategy continuing to compound.
