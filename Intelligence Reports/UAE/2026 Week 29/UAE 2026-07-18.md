## Story 1: Iran and Oman put a number on the Hormuz transit fee — $1 a barrel, ~$6.8bn a year

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/markets/will-a-new-hormuz-fee-make-oil-and-shipping-more-expensive-1.500611122
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Where yesterday's report flagged Oman's two-corridor proposal as still unfinalised ahead of Saturday's talks in Muscat, today's coverage confirms the shape it's taking: Iran and Oman are proposing a transit charge on vessels passing through the Strait of Hormuz of roughly $1 per barrel of oil — about 1.2% of the current ~$86 Brent price. Based on pre-war shipping volumes, that would raise an estimated $6.8bn annually, more than the roughly $4.7bn Egypt collected from Suez Canal fees in 2025-26.

The direct cost is modest against cargo value, but the framing from Oxford Economics is the more important point: "a transparent, non-discriminatory fee structure... would likely more than offset any direct cost of the levy through lower risk premia" — i.e., a known, published toll is cheaper for everyone than the current war-risk insurance premium (around 5% of vessel value) baked in by uncertainty. The catch is durability — the framework is explicitly flagged as "vulnerable to future breaches by Iran or other parties," and there's a longer-run risk that higher transit costs simply accelerate the shift toward alternative export routes that bypass Hormuz altogether, which would erode the fee's own revenue base over time.

For Orwell this is the most concrete number to come out of the Hormuz crisis since it reignited. If a formal fee structure gets signed and holds, marine war-risk insurance premiums are the number to watch next — a meaningful drop there is the first real signal the crisis is genuinely de-escalating rather than just pausing, and it's directly relevant to the GCC insurance-comparison thesis behind [[Comparison Network]].

## Story 2: Modon's Bashayer waterfront homes on Hudayriyat Island sell out in a day — Dh1.25bn, 71% new buyers

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/property/dh125b-worth-of-hudayriyat-island-homes-sell-out-in-one-day-1.500611911
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Abu Dhabi developer Modon sold all 300 homes in the final phase of its Bashayer waterfront community on Hudayriyat Island within 24 hours of launch, totalling Dh1.25bn across a mix of one- to three-bedroom apartments, four-bedroom penthouses and townhouses. The standout figure is buyer composition: 71% were new customers to Modon, not repeat buyers cycling through existing developments — a genuine demand signal rather than portfolio churn.

The project leans hard on lifestyle amenities (a 3.5km waterfront promenade, parks, gyms, retail) rather than pure investment yield, which fits the broader 2026 pattern already noted in prior reports: more Dubai and Abu Dhabi buyers are purchasing to live in, not just to flip, partly on the back of the Golden Visa's 10-year residency pull. This is a healthy demand data point for Abu Dhabi specifically at a moment when Dubai's own price growth has been cooling — worth banking as a counterpoint to the Northern Emirates rotation story flagged in Thursday's and Friday's reports; demand isn't just shifting cheaper, it's also concentrating in strong lifestyle-led Abu Dhabi launches.

## Story 3: e& banks Dh4.8bn net return on completed Vodafone exit

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/markets/e-completes-vodafone-exit-with-dh219-billion-in-proceeds-1.500611373

e& has completed the sale of its entire Vodafone stake to an acquisition vehicle owned by the Niel family group, for total consideration of Dh21.9bn ($5.95bn) — gross cash proceeds of Dh21.5bn at 110.5 pence per share, plus a final Dh400m dividend due 30 July. After costs, e&'s net cash return is Dh4.8bn ($1.3bn). The company frames this as sharpening its focus on core businesses while banking the value built up in the Vodafone position.

This is a straightforward capital-recycling move rather than a surprise, but it's a useful regional data point on how UAE state-linked telecom and investment groups are actively rotating out of legacy European stakes and presumably redeploying into core telecom, digital infrastructure, or AI-adjacent assets — a pattern worth watching if any of that capital lands in UAE-domestic tech or infrastructure plays relevant to the wider portfolio.

## Story 4: Brent nears $90 after an eleven-week run, with the Hormuz fee talks the immediate driver

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/energy/what-could-push-oil-above-90-after-an-11-weekly-surge-1.500611348

Crude has been on an eleven-week upward run and is now trading close to $90/barrel — a meaningful move from the ~$85 level cited in yesterday's report as "flat" and well below the crisis peak. The proximate driver is exactly the Hormuz transit-fee news in Story 1: a formal toll mechanism, even a modest one, reads to the market as confirmation that transit through the strait is being priced as an ongoing cost of doing business rather than a temporary risk, and that repricing is showing up in the benchmark.

This directly updates the market signals table from every report this week — Brent had been holding in the mid-$80s through Thursday and Friday; the jump toward $90 is the sharpest single-day move flagged since the tanker strikes on 14 July. Watch whether this holds through next week or reverses once the fee framework is confirmed or rejected.

## What this means for Orwell

The Hormuz transit-fee proposal is the story to actually watch out of today: if it converts into a signed, durable mechanism, it's the first genuine off-ramp from the war-risk pricing that's been baked into Gulf shipping, oil and insurance costs since the crisis reignited, and a real drop in war-risk premiums would be a meaningful positive signal for the wider GCC insurance-comparison thesis behind [[Comparison Network]]. Property demand stayed resilient today (the Hudayriyat Island sell-out is a genuinely strong number, not boosterism), and the steady drumbeat of new Dubai company formations is a useful reminder that SME activity keeps ticking over underneath the bigger geopolitical headlines. Nothing here requires action this week, but the Hormuz fee framework is worth flagging clearly for Monday's report regardless of which way it breaks.
