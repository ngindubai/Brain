## Story 1: Strait of Hormuz shipping grinds to a near-standstill; US issues a public ultimatum to Iran

**Source:** UN News, Al Jazeera, The Hill | https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/07/1167905 , https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/7/11/iran-war-live-us-demands-iran-publicly-state-strait-of-hormuz-open-for-all , https://thehill.com/homenews/5963580-u-s-demands-iran-affirm-strait-of-hormuz-is-fully-open-after-exchange-of-strikes-officials-say/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

The numbers are stark. As of Thursday-Friday, around 6,000 seafarers remain stranded aboard hundreds of vessels in and around the strait; a live tracker cited by The Hill puts transit at just 15 ships in the past 24 hours against a pre-war baseline of roughly 130 daily transits, with at least 285 vessels — mostly tankers — waiting to pass. The IMO has evacuated 136 ships and 2,900 seafarers to date, but suspended further evacuation operations on security grounds, and IMO Secretary-General Arsenio Dominguez has advised that all transit "should be avoided until the necessary safety conditions are in place." Separately, the IMO Council formally condemned Iran's "continued threats" against Gulf states and specifically rejected Iran's move to "establish an entity purporting to control traffic through the strait," calling on member states not to recognise any Iranian claim of jurisdiction over the waterway or third-state maritime zones around it.

As of today, senior US officials are demanding Iran issue an unambiguous public statement that the strait is open to all shipping and that Iran will not attack transiting civilian vessels — with Trump reportedly telling aides there won't be "a good outcome" if Tehran refuses. Iran's Foreign Ministry denies it has sought negotiations at all, though it has accepted a Qatari mediation visit.

This is a genuine, material update to the "de-escalation" narrative this brain flagged as pending yesterday. Thursday's calmer market session reflected a strike-and-pause tempo, not a resolution — and the shipping data confirms the underlying disruption never actually eased; if anything the standstill has deepened. For the marine war-risk insurance thread tracked in previous reports, this is further confirmation that the Q4 2026-Q1 2027 normalisation timeline is the right working assumption, not an optimistic one. For the comparison network broadly, there is no near-term reopening signal to build content around; the Khor Fakkan bypass (previously reported at 65,000 TEU/week, up from 8,000 pre-disruption) remains the more durable trade story than any Hormuz reopening.

Brent is trading around $75.80-76/bbl, up roughly 5% on the week, having spiked toward $78 on Tuesday's strikes before easing slightly as the market settled into pricing a prolonged disruption rather than a fresh escalation leg. With the strait carrying roughly a fifth of global oil and gas trade and genuinely reduced to a fraction of normal traffic (Story 1), the current price reflects real, ongoing supply-route risk rather than headline-driven speculation. ## Story 3: CBUAE grants Remitly a Stored Value Facilities licence — first cohort of international remittance firms formally recognised

**Source:** GlobeNewswire | https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/07/09/3325212/0/en/Remitly-Granted-Stored-Value-Facilities-License-from-the-Central-Bank-of-the-UAE.html
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Remitly became one of the first international remittance companies to secure a Stored Value Facilities licence with Exchange Business Category IV from the CBUAE, following what the company describes as a rigorous review process. This is a regulatory-recognition story rather than a rate or pricing one: it formalises international remittance players' standing under CBUAE's exchange-business framework, which is relevant background for MoneyCompare if remittance comparison content is ever built out, since it signals the regulator is actively bringing licensed international players into the same framework as domestic exchange houses rather than treating them as a grey area.
