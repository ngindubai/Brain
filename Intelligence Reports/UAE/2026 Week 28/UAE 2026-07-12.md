## Editor's note

The Hormuz standoff stopped being a shipping story and became a direct-hit story overnight. Iran fired missiles and drones at the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain and Jordan in the early hours of this morning after Saturday's US strikes on Iran, and Ncema told residents to shelter in place, the UAE's first live missile alert in months that wasn't a false alarm. Markets reopen today after the weekend into that backdrop, and at time of writing no one has published a confirmed DFM/ADX print. Away from the war, the numbers that did land today are good: Dubai's H1 property market is the second-strongest half-year on record and UAE airports set a passenger record. Treat the report below as split between a live security situation and business-as-usual data that arrived despite it.

## Story 1: Iran strikes the UAE directly, Ncema issues shelter alert

**Source:** The National | https://www.thenationalnews.com/news/uae/2026/07/12/uae-issues-missile-alert-and-urges-residents-to-seek-shelter/ and https://www.thenationalnews.com/news/mena/2026/07/12/live-missile-alert-us-iran-war/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

At around 6.30am today the UAE's Ministry of Defence and Ncema (the National Emergency Crisis and Disaster Management Authority) issued an alert telling residents to "find a safe location until further notice." This is not the technical-glitch false alarm from 26 June, this is a real one: Iran fired missiles and drones at the UAE after Washington struck Iranian targets, including Bushehr and Bandar Abbas, on Saturday night. Military jets were reported patrolling UAE airspace. Qatar and Bahrain issued parallel shelter warnings as their forces intercepted Iranian projectiles, and Jordan's air defences intercepted missiles with sirens sounding in Amman.

The chain that led here: Iran's Revolutionary Guards attacked the Cyprus-flagged container ship GFS Galaxy in the Strait of Hormuz on Saturday, leaving one crew member missing and the vessel disabled by fire and engine-room damage. US Central Command opened strikes on Iran about an hour later, at 7.15pm Eastern. Iran responded by declaring the Strait of Hormuz fully closed and firing on Gulf states it accuses of enabling the US route through the strait. Trump has said the ceasefire is "over" while also saying he doesn't believe this tips into full-scale war, a contradiction that is itself the market's problem this week.

This is the first time in this cycle that the UAE itself, not just shipping near it, has been targeted. It lands on a Sunday, the first UAE trading and business day after the weekend, directly on top of the market reopening. No casualties or property damage inside the UAE had been reported at the time of writing. The precedent that matters here is 2 March 2026, when a comparable Iranian missile and drone barrage that killed one person at Abu Dhabi airport led the Capital Markets Authority to shut ADX and DFM for two days. Nobody has confirmed that call has been made again today, but it is the live risk hanging over every other story below.

## Story 2: Strait of Hormuz declared fully closed, oil spikes to $78

**Source:** CNBC (via web search) and Al Jazeera (via web search) | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/11/us-airstrikes-iran-strait-hormuz.html
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Comparison Network]]

Brent jumped 5.2% to settle at $78.02 on Saturday after the US revoked its 60-day waiver on Iranian oil sanctions (transactions banned from 17 July) and Trump threatened a second day of strikes plus reimposing a naval blockade. Prices eased slightly off the session high after Trump said he didn't expect full-scale war, but the direction is up and the risk premium that has defined every UAE market report this month just got wider, not narrower. Iran's IRGC says it is now "enforcing" the closure it declared, which is a step beyond the informal near-standstill covered in yesterday's report, this is Iran actively firing on vessels using the non-Iranian route rather than simply obstructing it. Oman is still trying to broker a compromise, floating a proposal for free navigation through its own southern territorial waters as an alternative corridor, and Iran's foreign minister Araghchi held talks in Muscat on "safe passage" even as his own forces were firing on ships and neighbouring states. That contradiction, negotiating while attacking, is exactly the pattern that has made this crisis impossible to call resolved at any point in the last month.

For the [[Comparison Network]], this is the fourth consecutive day this fuel and freight cost story has been live; MoneyCompare's cost-of-living angle and InsureCompare's marine cover angle both have fresh material again today, not a rehash.

## Story 4: Dubai posts second-strongest half-year in its history, down from last year's record

**Source:** Arabian Business and Economy Middle East (via web search, direct fetch blocked) | https://www.arabianbusiness.com/real-estate/dubai-property-sales-h1-2026
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[SEO Portfolio]]

Dubai's real estate market generated more than AED 286.44 billion ($78 billion) in property sales across H1 2026, its second-highest first-half performance on record, but below the AED 326.6 billion ($88.9 billion) set in the record H1 2025. More than 86,000 sales transactions completed, split roughly 71,500 residential, 7,296 building and 7,129 land deals; total real estate transactions including mortgages and gifts reached AED 419.94 billion ($114.3 billion). The residential slice alone was AED 221.3 billion ($60.26 billion) across close to 79,200 transactions, down 13.8% in volume and 15.7% in value year-on-year against H1 2025's exceptional run.

June itself picked up: nearly 12,315 transactions worth AED 25.17 billion, up close to a third on May's 9,500 deals worth AED 22 billion. This is the first hard H1 aggregate to land since yesterday's report, which only had the standalone June monthly figure. The read is a market normalising off an unrepeatable 2025 peak rather than one in trouble: project launches in H1 2026 hit a record AED 275 billion across 250 new registered projects, so developer confidence hasn't followed transaction volumes down. Analysts continue to frame oversupply risk as concentrated in specific submarkets, roughly 45% of under-construction stock sits across JVC/JVT, Dubai South, MBR City, Business Bay and Dubailand, and skewed toward studios and one-beds (about 66% of upcoming supply), rather than a market-wide problem. This is the number worth watching against the geopolitical backdrop: a war escalation landing on top of a market that is already cooling from a record year is a different risk than one landing on an overheating one.

## Story 5: UAE airports post record 156.8 million passengers in 2025

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/aviation/uae-airports-handle-1568-million-passengers-in-2025-up-61-1.500603528
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Published 10 July: UAE airports handled a record 156.8 million passengers in 2025, up 6.1% on 2024's 147.8 million, with aircraft movements up 6.8% to 855,300. Dubai carried 104.5 million (+3.5%), Abu Dhabi 34.2 million (+10.7%), Sharjah 16.9 million (+10.7%), Ras Al Khaimah crossed 1 million (+56.8%) and Fujairah hit 164,600 (+170%, off a small base). Transit passengers, the number that best reflects the UAE's hub role rather than pure tourism demand, rose 8% to 69.5 million. Economy Minister Abdulla bin Touq Al Marri called it evidence of aviation's growing weight in the non-oil economy. This data predates this morning's strikes and says nothing about how a live regional war affects Q3 booking behaviour, but it's the baseline number to watch for erosion. Ras Al Khaimah and Fujairah's growth rates are the standout, both are still low-base but both are gateways with visa-on-arrival and free zone angles relevant to inbound investor flow.

## Story 6: EGA restarts Al Taweelah alumina refinery, seven months after Iranian strike

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/markets/ega-restarts-al-taweelah-alumina-refinery-after-march-suspension-1.500603301
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Published 10 July: Emirates Global Aluminium has restarted alumina production at its Al Taweelah refinery, which shut down on 28 March after Iranian missile and drone attacks on the Khalifa Economic Zone in Abu Dhabi, the same category of direct-strike risk that materialised again this morning. Hydrate production resumed 24 June, alumina output resumed in July, and the plant is targeting 50% capacity within days with full operational capability by end of 2026. Before the March suspension the refinery produced 2.4 million tonnes a year and supplied 46% of EGA's total alumina needs. EGA's CEO called it "another big milestone" in restoring the site.

This is a genuine recovery story and worth having on record precisely because of what happened at 6.30am today: it shows what a full restoration timeline from a direct strike on Abu Dhabi industrial infrastructure actually looks like (three-plus months to first restart, nine-plus months to full capacity), which is the realistic bar against which to judge any damage from today's escalation if it turns out to have hit UAE assets. No indication in the source that today's attacks affected Al Taweelah or any other EGA site.

## Story 7: Sharjah rolls out utility relief package for tourism operators

**Source:** Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/uae/sharjah-launches-incentives-to-support-tourism-businesses-1.500603689
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Published 10 July: the Sharjah Electricity, Water and Gas Authority (SEWA) launched a support package for tourism establishments running June to August 2026, aimed at "supporting the sector's sustainability" and the emirate's investment environment. It's a relief package rather than a growth incentive: extended bill payment deadlines, instalment options, a three-month waiver on disconnection penalties, instalment security deposits for new accounts, three months of exemption from engineering plan approval fees, frozen service fees for inactive hotel meters, and bank charges covered on transfer payments for three months. Serviced apartment investors can also disconnect utilities on unused units without penalty.

Read together with the industrial recovery at Al Taweelah, this is a second Northern Emirates data point of authorities cushioning operators through a rough patch rather than a sign of acute distress, Sharjah's tourism sector isn't collapsing, this looks pre-emptive given the wider regional security backdrop and the cost pressure that comes with it (freight, insurance, fuel all up this month). Modest relevance to the distressed deals group as a leading indicator, not yet a trigger.

## What this means for Orwell

What happens to the DFM/ADX open, and whether the Capital Markets Authority pulls the same two-day suspension lever it used in March, is the thing to check before doing anything active this week: freight, insurance and fuel costs are all moving against the portfolio in real time, and the comparison network's insurance and mortgage verticals have genuine, non-forced content angles again today rather than a rehash of the standing Hormuz story.
