## Editor's note

The story today is the pause, not the war. After two days of US strikes and Iranian retaliation against Bahrain and Kuwait, both sides appear to be deliberately holding fire while Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Turkey and Pakistan work the phones. Gulf markets read it that way: Thursday's session was a mild, not a panic, decline, and Dubai's index is trading firmer intraday today. Nothing structural has changed underneath (rates, DLD trend, mortgage pricing are all steady), but the market's tolerance for these strike cycles is visibly shortening each time. This is a wait state, not a resolution.

## Story 1: Gulf markets steady as US and Iran deliberately hold fire, Khamenei buried without incident

**Source:** Reuters (via Business Recorder/Zawya Mideast Stocks wire) | https://www.brecorder.com/news/40429128/most-gulf-markets-slip-on-me-hostilities and https://www.zawya.com/en/capital-markets/equities/mideast-stocks-most-gulf-markets-slip-on-middle-east-hostilities-qxifzpka
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Thursday's close (9 July) confirmed the softer scenario flagged as pending in yesterday's report: Dubai's DFM General fell a modest 0.2%, Abu Dhabi's ADX General finished flat, and Saudi's Tadawul dropped 0.4%. That is a fraction of Wednesday's 1.5% DFM slide and 2.7% Salik hit. The proximate cause on Thursday was Iran's retaliatory strikes on US-linked military infrastructure in Bahrain (Juffair, Sheikh Isa) and Kuwait (Camp Arifjan, Ali Al Salem), which triggered air raid sirens in both states but caused no reported casualties.

Today, intraday data on the DFM General shows the index trading around 5,718, up from an open near 5,690 — a stabilisation move, not a rally, but notable given the previous two sessions were both down. The explanation appears to be a deliberate US "strike and pause" posture: Washington has been alternating rounds of strikes with pauses explicitly to give mediators room to work, rather than escalating continuously. Reporting from PBS and CBS today describes US officials saying strikes tonight remain prepared but are being held back while diplomacy is given priority. Qatar's prime minister spoke by phone with Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi on Thursday urging both sides back to the MoU; Araghchi separately worked the phones with his Saudi, Turkish and Omani counterparts and Pakistan's army chief.

Separately, and without market disruption, former Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was finally laid to rest early Friday at the Imam Reza shrine in Mashhad — a domestic Iranian event that had been delayed by the fighting, and which passed without additional escalation.

None of this amounts to a resolution. The MoU that Trump declared "over" on Wednesday has not been formally revived, and the US Treasury's move to revoke the Iranian oil sanctions waiver earlier in the week remains in place. But for Gareth's purposes, the relevant signal is that markets are pricing a de-escalation attempt, not a fresh crisis leg, for the first time since Tuesday. If that holds through the weekend, expect Sunday's DFM open to test whether this is a genuine floor or just quiet trading ahead of the weekend.

## Story 3: Marine war-risk premiums still 8-10x baseline, full normalisation pushed to 2027

**Source:** Industry roundup via Lloyd's List, Howden, WEF, dubaicargos.com coverage | https://www.lloydslist.com/LL1156586/Gulf-war-risk-premiums-topping-double-digit-millions-of-dollars-per-trip and https://dubaicargos.com/hormuz-war-risk-insurance-premiums-2026/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

An update on the marine war-risk thread flagged in previous reports: premiums for Gulf transits remain at roughly 8 to 10 times baseline as of mid-July, with insurers citing incomplete mine-clearance certification and continued intermittent IRGC vessel incidents as the reason for holding elevated pricing rather than reacting to any single day's news. At normal rates, war-risk cover on a VLCC costs roughly $150,000-225,000 per voyage; at current multiples that becomes a serious line item for any operator still transiting the Strait rather than routing via Khor Fakkan or Fujairah.

The more useful data point for planning purposes: brokers are now projecting the Lloyd's Joint War Committee could begin downgrading the Hormuz Listed Area designation in Q4 2026, with full normalisation not expected before Q1-Q2 2027. That is a materially longer timeline than the "reopening" optimism this brain flagged and then reversed back in June — worth carrying as the working assumption for InsureCompare commercial-marine content rather than assuming a near-term snap-back.

## Story 4: Gulftainer's Khor Fakkan bypass is now doing real volume, not just headline capacity

**Source:** AGBI, The National | https://www.agbi.com/logistics/2026/07/gulftainer-accelerates-expansion-of-sharjahs-khor-fakkan-port/ and https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/energy/2026/07/07/khor-fakkan-port-terminal-capacity-to-be-doubled-amid-hormuz-disruption/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Concrete numbers on the Khor Fakkan story flagged previously: weekly container throughput at the Sharjah-based terminal has surged from around 8,000 TEUs before the Hormuz disruption to roughly 65,000 TEUs now — an eight-fold increase in real cargo flow, not just an announced expansion target. Gulftainer's operators say the terminal, once its $2bn expansion to 10 million TEU annual capacity completes, could absorb 80-90% of total UAE container demand if the Strait were blockaded again. The Sajaa dry port (70 hectares, opened this year) and the planned Etihad Rail connection add road and rail redundancy on top of the sea route.

The strategic read for Orwell: this is a live case study in a UAE logistics chokepoint being engineered around within months, not years — a pattern worth watching if any venture-side thinking touches supply chain resilience or freight-adjacent content. It also underlines why the marine war-risk premium story above may resolve faster for UAE-bound cargo specifically than for the wider Gulf, since a growing share of freight simply isn't transiting Hormuz any more.

## Story 5: Visa overstay grace period expired 9 July — standard fines confirmed at AED 50/day, not AED 200/day

**Source:** Gulf Buzz, Arabian Business, Khaleej Times | https://www.gulfbuzz.com/uae-sets-july-9-deadline-for-expired-visas/ and https://www.arabianbusiness.com/business/tourism-hospitality/uae-visa-deadline-overstay-fine
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Correction and close-out on this thread: the ICP's 30-day grace period (covering people whose visas expired due to the regional flight disruptions, originally exempted from fines back in March) ran 10 June to 9 July and has now expired. But the standard fine that resumes is AED 50 per day from day one, uncapped, unified across all seven emirates since a February 2026 ICP reform — not the AED 200/day figure carried in this brain's working assumption. Overstays beyond 30 days also require a separate exit permit (roughly AED 250-300) before departure is possible. Worth updating any comparison-network content or client-facing material that cites the old AED 200/day figure, since it is now stale by five months, not just today's news.

## Story 6: Dubai property closes a strong June — 13,766 sales, AED 32.66bn, best month since April

**Source:** Sherwoods Property (DLD data), Arabian Business | https://sherwoodsproperty.com/dubai-real-estate-transactions-june-2026/ and https://www.arabianbusiness.com/real-estate/dubai-property-sales-h1-2026
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

June's DLD numbers, now fully tallied: 13,766 sales transactions worth AED 32.66 billion, up 31.3% in volume month-on-month over May, and the strongest single month since April 2026. Standout deals included a AED 200 million apartment sale at Bugatti Residences (Business Bay) and a AED 124 million office deal at Vision Tower. This is the data underlying the H1 2026 aggregate already reported (AED 286.4bn, second-highest H1 on record, down 12% on 2025's record H1) — the June rebound suggests the H1 dip was front-loaded into weaker spring months rather than a trend that is still deepening into Q3. Worth a cautious read though: one strong month against a war backdrop is not yet confirmation of a durable recovery, and July's numbers (which will include the two days of active strikes this week) are the real test.
