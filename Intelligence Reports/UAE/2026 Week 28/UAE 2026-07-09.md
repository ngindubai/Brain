## Story 1: Ceasefire declared "over" — Hormuz effectively closed again, second day of US strikes
**Source:** Al Jazeera, NBC News, Washington Post | https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/8/trump-says-ceasefire-over-after-us-iran-trade-attacks | https://www.nbcnews.com/world/iran/live-blog/live-updates-iran-attacks-gulf-us-strikes-tehran-ships-hormuz-oil-rcna353439

The US-Iran memorandum of understanding that reopened the Strait of Hormuz roughly three weeks ago has broken down. Iran struck three commercial vessels transiting the strait on 6-7 July; the US responded with strikes on Iranian air defence systems, coastal surveillance sites and drone-launch positions. Speaking from the NATO summit in Ankara on Wednesday (8 July), Trump said the ceasefire was "over," called Iranian leaders "scum," and said the US was reimposing its naval blockade. US Central Command confirmed a second consecutive day of strikes today, aimed at "further degrading Iran's ability to threaten freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz," with explosions reported near Sirik, Bushehr, Konarak and Chabahar. Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf responded on X that Hormuz "will remain open only through Iranian arrangements," warning "if you strike, you will be struck."

Shipping data now shows roughly 34 transits a day through the strait against a pre-crisis norm of around 178-83 — a fall of 80-95%. Shipowners are split: three of five surveyed by Bloomberg are actively reassessing whether transit is still safe, two have not yet changed policy. This is the third time in 2026 the strait has effectively closed to normal traffic (following the original February-March closure and the brief April-June reopening), and the pattern is now familiar enough that markets and insurers are pricing it faster than in March.

For a report built around a UAE comparison network and distressed-deals interest, the direct read is this: every open loop tracked over the past fortnight — Gulftainer's Hormuz-bypass hedge, marine war-risk insurance, the visa grace period tied to the June disruption — has just been overtaken by a fresh, live version of the same crisis. Nothing here is settled; treat any "stabilisation" language in older sources (some cached search results still describe the situation as calming as of early July) as stale.

## Story 2: Oil spikes to $78, Gulf markets sell off — Dubai index down 1.5%, Salik down 2.7%
**Source:** Business Recorder (Reuters), Fortune | https://www.brecorder.com/news/40429094/most-gulf-markets-slip-on-middle-east-hostilities | https://fortune.com/2026/07/08/iran-strikes-gulf-global-selloff-stocks-oil-price/

Brent jumped 5.3% on Wednesday to around $78 a barrel (WTI +5.4% to over $74), the sharpest one-day move since the March closure, after Trump's "over" comment and the US striking more than 80 targets inside Iran. Iran's Revolutionary Guards claimed strikes on US positions in Bahrain and Kuwait, with air-raid sirens sounding in both.

Most Gulf bourses closed lower on the news. Dubai's main index fell 1.5%, dragged by a 2.7% slide in toll operator Salik — a proxy for how quickly Dubai-listed names reprice geopolitical risk even when the direct economic linkage (a toll-road operator) is thin. This is the same decoupling pattern seen in March: oil spikes normally lift Gulf energy-heavy indices (Saudi Arabia's Tadawul has previously been "turbo-charged" by such moves), but Dubai's index is more exposed to real estate and consumer sentiment and has tended to fall on proximity risk rather than rally on oil. Confirmed DFM figures now run to Wednesday 8 July close; no fresh Thursday print was retrievable at time of writing (markets trade Sunday-Thursday, so today's close is the one to watch).

## Story 3: Visa grace period expires today — standard overstay fines resume mid-crisis
**Source:** Khaleej Times, Gulf News | https://www.khaleejtimes.com/uae/uae-visa-grace-period-what-visitors-should-do-before-july-9-deadline | https://gulfnews.com/uae/government/uae-announces-30-day-visa-grace-period-for-travellers-affected-by-regional-disruptions-1.500579291

The 30-day ICP overstay-fine exemption granted to visitors and residents stranded by June's airspace disruptions ends today, 9 July. From today, standard penalties apply in full: AED 200 per day for the first six months of overstay, escalating thereafter. No extension has been announced as of this report; immigration consultants were telling clients as late as 1 July not to assume more time would be granted.

The timing is awkward. The grace period was designed to close out the tail of the previous disruption just as a new one — the Hormuz re-escalation in Story 1 — begins generating fresh flight cancellations and airspace risk. If today's strikes produce a new wave of stranded travellers, expect either a fast-follow ICP statement extending relief again, or a period where affected people are caught between an expired amnesty and a live crisis. Worth flagging to anyone in the network's orbit with UAE visa exposure: verify status today rather than assume the authorities will reopen the window automatically.

## Story 4: Gulftainer CEO — traffic still below pre-conflict levels despite $2bn expansion push
**Source:** AGBI | https://www.agbi.com/logistics/2026/07/gulftainer-accelerates-expansion-of-sharjahs-khor-fakkan-port/

Published yesterday (8 July) but with a detail worth carrying forward: Gulftainer CEO Farid Belbouab told AGBI that "despite last month's ceasefire talks, shipping traffic remains below pre-conflict levels" — an admission that the June reopening never fully normalised trade before this week's collapse. The expansion numbers reported Tuesday stand (3.5m to 5m TEU capacity within three months, 10m TEU within three years, container throughput up from 8,000 to 65,000 a week, truck movements up from ~100 to 8,500 a day), and Gulftainer still estimates it is handling 80-90% of UAE container demand during the disruption. Given Story 1, this is no longer a contingency hedge — it is now the primary route carrying the load in real time.

## Story 5: War-risk marine insurance repricing again as Hormuz risk resets
**Source:** Lloyd's Market Association, S&P Global | https://lmalloyds.com/safety-concerns-not-insurance-availability-driving-reduced-vessel-traffic-in-the-strait-of-hormuz/

The Joint War Committee's "high-risk" designation, which had been expected to ease as the ceasefire held through June, is back under pressure. War-risk premiums for Gulf transits have been reported moving from roughly 0.2% to as much as 1% of hull value within 48 hours of the latest escalation — in VLCC terms, a five-year-old tanker could see cover costed at $10-14m for a single Hormuz voyage, in line with the levels seen during March's closure. Several P&I clubs that had resumed standard cover in June are understood to be reviewing positions again. This is a direct read-across for InsureCompare's marine/commercial lines content — the "insurance gap" story (standard travel and cargo policies excluding conflict-related disruption) that surfaced in late June is now live again rather than historical.
