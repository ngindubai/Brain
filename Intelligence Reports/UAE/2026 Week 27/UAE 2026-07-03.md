## Story 1: UAE oil exports claw back to near pre-war levels, bypassing Hormuz
**Source:** Bloomberg, "UAE Oil Flows Hit Pre-War Levels on Dark Ships and Hormuz Bypass" (1 July 2026) | https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/uae-oil-flows-hit-pre-war-levels-on-dark-ships-and-hormuz-bypass — cross-checked against The National, "UAE oil exports approach highest level since 2017 amid postwar recovery" (2 July 2026) | https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/energy/2026/07/02/uae-oil-exports-back-near-prewar-levels/ and Gulf News | https://gulfnews.com/business/energy/uae-oil-exports-are-returning-to-near-record-levels-as-pre-war-flows-resume-1.500594541
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

UAE crude and condensate exports rose roughly 30% month-on-month in June to more than 3.9 million barrels a day — nearing levels last seen in 2017, and close to pre-war throughput, despite the Strait of Hormuz remaining formally closed to normal commercial traffic (day 124+ of disruption).

The route is not the strait. Abu Dhabi is moving crude via the Habshan–Fujairah pipeline (capacity 1.8m bpd) and the separate Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline, both of which land at Fujairah on the Gulf of Oman side, sidestepping Hormuz entirely. That flow is backstopped by the 42-million-barrel Mandous underground storage facility near Fujairah, which lets Abu Dhabi smooth loadings even when tanker movements through the strait itself are erratic. On top of the pipeline route, a meaningful share of exports has moved via "dark" tankers — vessels running with AIS transponders off to obscure position and ownership, a workaround that has become common practice across Gulf exporters since the crisis began in February.

This matters because it directly undercuts the logic behind the CBUAE's own 30 June Quarterly Review, which cut its 2026 GDP forecast to 1.7% from an earlier 5.6–5.7%, citing Hormuz-linked oil production losses of -34.3%/-30.4% year-on-year in March and April. If June's export data holds — and it is corroborated by three independent outlets (Bloomberg, The National, Gulf News), not just one — the UAE's own output picture is recovering faster than the central bank's headline forecast implies. That forecast was not showing signs of revision as of 2 July; nothing new on that front today either.

The read-through for oil markets: this UAE supply recovery, alongside Saudi sales to Asia and emergency reserve releases, is part of why Brent has been sliding — crude fell to $68.43/bbl on 3 July (down 0.38% on the day, TradingEconomics), extending a drop that took Brent below $71 on 2 July, its lowest level since late February. A genuine UAE and Saudi supply rebound, not just de-escalation optimism, is now doing real work in that price move.

Caveat: none of this means Hormuz itself is safe or normalising. It means the UAE specifically has engineered a workaround. Other Gulf exporters without a Fujairah-style bypass pipeline are not in the same position, and the underlying strait remains contested (see Story 2). Treat this as a UAE-specific resilience story, not a regional all-clear.

## Story 2: Iran re-escalates rhetoric on Hormuz; no change to physical transit picture
**Source:** PBS News, "Iran warns oil tankers to use approved routes in Strait of Hormuz or face a 'forceful response'" (2 July 2026) | https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/iran-warns-oil-tankers-to-use-approved-routes-in-strait-of-hormuz-or-face-a-forceful-response — background: Euronews, "Ship runs aground in Hormuz as Iran insists on controlling the strait" (1 July 2026) | https://www.euronews.com/2026/07/01/ship-runs-aground-in-hormuz-as-iran-insists-on-controlling-the-strait
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Iran's joint military command warned on 2 July that tankers not using Iran-designated routes through Hormuz face an "immediate and forceful response," and separately threatened a "rapid and decisive reaction" to any US military interference. The trigger was a meeting in Bahrain between US military leaders and Gulf officials reaffirming commitment to free passage — which Tehran read as interference. This sits on top of the existing interim deal (the toll-free 60-day passage arrangement), which Iran continues to say it will end by asserting route control and eventual transit fees, a position Washington and Gulf states reject.

Separately, a foreign container ship ran aground on 1 July in shallow water on the Omani side of the strait while using the alternative Oman corridor set up last week with the IMO. Iran's IRGC blamed the grounding on the vessel straying from its "approved" route and used the incident to reinforce its warning to shipowners.

No new physical transit data since yesterday's report: last official count remains 27 vessels/24hr against a normal ~84–130/day, with roughly 180–380 vessels backed up depending on source. War-risk insurance for VLCCs is unchanged at roughly $2.5m per vessel (around 8x pre-crisis, a ~700% premium increase), with six P&I clubs still not offering Gulf coverage. This is a rhetorical escalation layered on a physical situation that hasn't moved — treat it as noise on the open loop, not a new development, pending an actual change in transit volumes or insurance pricing.
