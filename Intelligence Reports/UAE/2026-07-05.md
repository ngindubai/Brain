## Story 1: Iran-Oman agreement on Hormuz traffic management emerges as first real de-escalation mechanism

**Source:** Bloomberg, "Tankers U-Turn in Hormuz, With Some Choosing Iran Route" (4 July, via search summary, direct fetch 403'd) | https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-04/tankers-u-turn-in-hormuz-with-some-taking-iran-route-instead — straits.live tracker (Day 126) | https://straits.live/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

At least eight ships attempting to leave the Gulf along the Omani coast turned back between Friday and Saturday rather than risk transit — some then switched to a route closer to the Iranian coast, sailing under Iran-dictated terms instead (one crude tanker, two products tankers, one bulk carrier took this route). That's the same "use Iran's routes or don't go" pattern flagged in prior reports, now with concrete vessel-level evidence.

The more significant development: Iranian parliamentary speaker Ghalibaf announced an Iran-Oman agreement on Hormuz traffic management, and the UK and France are said to be preparing a joint maritime security mission. This is the first reported instance of an actual management mechanism for the strait, rather than unilateral Iranian control plus international monitoring from the sidelines. It doesn't resolve the diplomatic pause around the Khamenei funeral proceedings (running 4-9 July, as logged previously) — the Doha US-Iran talks remain paused until after that period — but a third-party (Oman) brokering traffic terms is a structurally different situation to report than "Iran controls the strait and everyone else watches."

Brent held broadly flat at $72.12-72.30/bbl through the weekend, effectively pricing out a short-term war premium despite the operational reality that transit remains heavily disrupted (27 ships/24hr per IMF PortWatch data, versus ~84/day normally). Read this as the market treating the Iran-Oman development as incrementally positive without yet pricing a full reopening.

## Story 2: Dubai June transactions show broad-based recovery — 13,766 sales, AED 32.66bn, volume up 31.3% on May

**Source:** Edwards & Towers, "Dubai Real Estate Market June 2026: 13,766 Sales & Emaar's AED 200 Billion Mega City" (via web search, cross-checked against Economy Middle East's H1 half-year figure) | https://edwardsandtowers.com/dubai-real-estate-market-june-2026-emaar-200-billion-mega-city/ — Economy Middle East, "Dubai posts second-highest half-year real estate sales on record at $77.88 billion in H1 2026" | https://economymiddleeast.com/news/dubai-posts-second-highest-half-year-real-estate-sales-on-record-at-77-88-billion-in-h1-2026/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Dubai recorded 13,766 sales transactions worth AED 32.66 billion in June 2026 — a 31.3% increase in volume and 10.9% rise in value versus May, making June the strongest month since April. Residential transactions made up 96.9% of volume and 82.7% of value (18,077 transactions, AED 39.77bn across the broader transaction mix including mortgages and gifts). The volume rise outpacing the value rise is the notable detail: it points to broad-based recovery across a wider range of price points and buyers, particularly off-plan and high-volume apartment segments, rather than a handful of ultra-prime deals skewing the numbers.

This cross-checks against the half-year figure of $77.88bn (~AED 286bn) in H1 2026 sales — the second-highest half-year on record, behind only H1 2025's AED 326.6bn. Caution flag: **the official DLD Q2 quarterly release itself does not yet appear to be published** — this is monthly/aggregator data, not the DLD's own quarterly report, so the open loop on "full official Q2 data pending" from prior reports stays open, just less blind than before now that June's monthly print is out.

Read alongside the Accor hotel-recovery timeline logged 4 July (Q4 2026 volumes, Q2 2027 full rate recovery) and the softer 5-8% 2026 price-appreciation forecast circulating versus 12-22% in 2024/25 — the picture is consistent: transaction volume is recovering faster than pricing power, which fits a market where buyers now have leverage and sellers are still working through post-conflict repricing.
