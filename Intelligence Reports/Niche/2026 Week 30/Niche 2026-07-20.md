## 1. US Pest Control

### Bug Barometer: above-average summer ahead, all regions on alert

The NPMA released its 2026 Spring and Summer Bug Barometer predicting heightened pest pressure across the entire continental US, driven by erratic winter weather that allowed more pests to survive overwintering while creating ideal early-season breeding conditions. The forecast is nationally significant: every major region is flagging elevated activity for at least two or three pest categories through September.

Regional breakdown:
- **Southeast** (FL, GA, AL, SC, NC): tropical storms and wet heat will drive fly, cockroach, and mosquito surges. Standing water from storm events creates rapid population spikes.
- **Mid-Atlantic & New England**: tick activity elevated after a warm early season; late summer will bring yellowjacket and wasp pressure alongside a secondary cockroach uptick.
- **Great Lakes / Midwest**: heavy summer rainfall pushing ants and cockroaches indoors; fly and tick populations spiking.
- **Southwest**: monsoon season driving scorpions and spiders indoors to seek shelter -- a perennial summer signal but forecast to be more pronounced in 2026.
- **Northwest**: mosquitoes, flies, and yellowjackets all on the move earlier than typical.

This is the seasonal demand signal that justifies summer marketing spend and technician capacity planning. Pest activity is outpacing consumer awareness -- the gap between "pests are active" and "customer calls" is where content works. ## 2. Pet Transport

### IATA releases in-cabin pet travel operational guidelines -- the biggest industry-wide development in years

On 15 July, IATA published its first comprehensive operational guidelines for in-cabin pet travel, covering every stage from booking through post-arrival. This is not a regulatory instrument -- airlines are not required to adopt it -- but it represents a significant shift in how the industry is moving toward standardisation, and it will influence how IPATA member agents advise clients.

**What the guidelines cover:**
- Pre-booking: airlines should publish transparent policy pages with reservation timing requirements (typically 48-72 hours), health certificate and vaccination record requirements, approved carrier dimensions, and aircraft-specific restrictions
- Check-in: staff verification of documentation, carrier inspection, and confirmation of animal fitness before boarding
- In-flight: pets must remain in approved carriers throughout; security and customs procedures made explicit
- Connecting flights: airlines should communicate availability of pet relief areas at transit airports

**Why IATA moved now:** A 2025 IATA survey found 41% of passengers who travel with pets were not certain their animal qualified to fly, 36% were confused about airline policies, and 34% did not understand the process at all. These knowledge gaps generate last-minute complications that disrupt operations and generate complaints. The guidelines are framed as a "shift towards clearer communication rather than stricter regulation" -- airlines retain policy-making authority, IATA is standardising the communication of those policies.

**The service dog / emotional support animal clarification** is notable: service dogs (trained assistance animals) fly under international recognition frameworks and typically travel free with proper documentation. Emotional support animals have no universal recognition and are treated as standard pets by most airlines and destinations. This line has blurred in the consumer market; the new guidance sharpens it.

**IATA LAR 2026 updates** (separate but related): the Live Animals Regulations are also being updated this cycle, with new guidance on container strength, animal densities for cargo, brachycephalic breed restrictions, and seafood transport. The LAR governs cargo-hold transport, which is the primary channel for IPATA's professional shippers. No specific effective date published this week but these updates are in preparation for the 2026 edition.

For [[Agent Org]] and the pet-transport site: the IATA guidelines create a content window. The gap between what airlines are now recommended to communicate and what passengers actually understand is where informational content converts. "What the new IATA pet travel guidelines mean for your shipment" is a high-intent search query that does not yet have a definitive answer page.

## 3. Funeral Repatriation

### UK Funeral Director Code -- the sector now has a unified national standard

The major regulatory development for this niche in 2026 is not a July news story but it remains an open loop that operators and content targeting this sector need to be aware of. In March 2026, NAFD and SAIF (Society of Allied and Independent Funeral Directors) jointly adopted the UK Funeral Director Code -- a single national code of practice modelled on the Scottish Funeral Director Code (approved 2018). This code applies across all four UK jurisdictions.

For repatriation specifically: the SAIF Code of Practice 2026 confirms that a Service Level Agreement is not required for singular ad hoc arrangements such as repatriation. This means the code does not impose additional commercial formalisation requirements on one-off international repatriation cases, which is the dominant use case for the funeral-repatriation niche. The code does, however, set welfare, documentation, and communication standards that all NAFD- and SAIF-affiliated directors must meet.

The broader statutory regulation framework is still in development. The Law Commission and government are working toward enforceable funeral industry regulation; a robust statutory framework is expected to emerge from this work but no legislative timetable has been confirmed. The common code from NAFD and SAIF demonstrates the industry can self-regulate coherently -- which typically precedes government action by a couple of years.

**Cost context for content:** 2026 repatriation benchmarks are: Europe £3,000-£7,000; long-haul (US, Australia, Southeast Asia) £7,000-£20,000+. These figures are being published by NAFD-affiliated directories and are useful anchor data for pricing-oriented content.

Sources: [UK Funeral Director Code (NAFD)](https://www.nafd.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/UK-Funeral-Director-Code-1.pdf) | [SAIF Code of Practice March 2026](https://saif.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/SAIF-Code-of-Practice-March-2026.pdf) | [NAFD Funeral Directory -- Repatriation guide](https://www.funeral-directory.co.uk/guides/repatriation-bringing-a-body-home/)

## 4. Close Protection

### SIA terminates BIIAB recognition -- biggest enforcement action against a training provider in recent memory

This is the lead story for the close protection niche this week. The SIA has moved to strip BIIAB (British Institute of Innkeeping Awarding Body) of its recognition as an approved awarding organisation for SIA licence-linked qualifications.

**Timeline:**
- **9 July 2026**: Ofqual suspended BIIAB from accepting new learners on a set of security qualifications. The SIA simultaneously removed BIIAB from its course finder tool for door supervisor and security guarding qualifications.
- **15 July 2026**: The SIA issued formal notice to BIIAB to terminate the recognition agreement covering all other SIA licence-linked qualifications.
- **10 August 2026**: Hard deadline -- BIIAB must not issue any new licence-linked qualifications after this date.

**Why:** The SIA conducted 24 unannounced inspections of BIIAB as part of Operation RESOLUTE, its ongoing enforcement initiative targeting substandard training providers. The findings revealed "serious and persistent material breaches of their obligations which are not capable of being remedied." SIA Executive Director Tim Archer stated that "protecting the integrity of the SIA licence and mandated entry-level training is essential for both public safety."

**Affected qualifications confirmed:** door supervision and security guarding, including refresher courses. The BIIAB suspension was specifically referenced in the context of these two categories by both Ofqual and the SIA. Close protection qualifications were not explicitly named in the Ofqual action, but the SIA's 15 July notice extends to "all other SIA licence-linked qualifications" -- which includes any BIIAB close protection awards.

**What this means for the sector:** Any individual currently enrolled in a BIIAB-delivered SIA course is in a difficult position. Training providers must transition learners to alternative awarding bodies. The SIA has not published transition guidance as of this week. Anyone using BIIAB for close protection refresher training to renew their licence after April 2026 should contact their provider immediately and confirm qualification validity before the August 10 deadline.

Sources: [GOV.UK -- SIA removes BIIAB](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/awarding-body-removed-from-sia-directory) | [GOV.UK -- SIA terminates recognition](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/private-security-regulator-removes-recognition-of-awarding-body) | [UKNIP](https://uknip.co.uk/news/uk/uk-news/sia-removes-biiab-from-security-course-directory-over-standards/) | [FE Week](https://feweek.co.uk/security-regulator-bans-biiab-after-fraud-probe/)

### Close protection refresher training -- now a licence renewal requirement

Separately from the BIIAB story: from 1 April 2026, close protection operatives must complete refresher training to renew their SIA licence. This rule was introduced as part of SIA licence-linked training reforms and applies to all CP renewals. Refresher training has been available since October 2024. The April 2026 date made it mandatory rather than optional.

With BIIAB now suspended, any CP operative who was relying on BIIAB for their refresher course to meet this renewal requirement needs an alternative provider urgently. The combination of the mandatory refresh requirement and the BIIAB removal is creating genuine urgency in the market. Source: [GovWire](https://www.govwire.co.uk/news/security-industry-authority)

## 5. Bus Hire

### Government mandates local authorities to plan for coach travel -- structural win for the sector

The most significant structural development for bus hire this year: from 2 April 2026, the Department for Transport's new transport planning guidance requires every local authority in England to recognise and plan for the role of coach services in their transport strategy. Specifically, authorities must:

- Evaluate current and prospective coach demand
- Collaborate with highway authorities on coach provision vision
- Provide suitable parking, pickup, and drop-off infrastructure
- Allow coaches access to bus lanes (except where this slows local buses)
- Consider funding mechanisms for new or expanded scheduled services

CPT's Alison Edwards described it as "a game changer for the coach industry." Towns can now pursue CPT's "Coach Friendly" accreditation to formalise their commitment. The practical effect is that coach operators have a statutory hook to demand proper facilities from local authorities rather than treating it as a favour. This will compound over the next 12-24 months as local transport plans are updated. Source: [CPT-UK](https://www.cpt-uk.org/news/game-changer-for-coach-travel-in-england-as-government-orders-national-planning-overhaul/)

### Summer VAT scheme -- demand boost incoming for bus hire operators

The UK government's "Great British Summer Savings" scheme cut VAT from 20% to 5% on qualifying tourism and hospitality services from 25 June to 1 September 2026. Coach and bus fares are already zero-rated for VAT, so the direct pricing effect is nil. The indirect effect matters: lower admission costs at theme parks, zoos, museums, and similar attractions will drive more families to book day trips and excursions. Combined with the August free child bus travel scheme (5-15 year olds travel free on local bus services in England through August), there is a meaningful demand stimulus for summer group travel.

For bus hire specifically: operators offering packaged excursions with qualifying attractions may have Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (TOMS) implications worth reviewing with an accountant. Operators should be marketing summer packages now while consumer interest is at its seasonal peak. Source: [EVM UK](https://evm.uk/summer-vat-cut-what-coach-bus-operators-need-to-know/)

### Driver shortage: CPT escalates lobbying for regulatory reform

The coach industry's structural labour problem has not improved. The current gap stands at 12.4% for coach drivers (equivalent to over 4,000 vacancies) and 3.4% for bus drivers. CPT is pushing the government on two specific regulatory barriers:

1. **Age restriction**: drivers aged 18-19 are restricted to regular services of no more than 50km. This blocks most coaching positions for young entrants. CPT argues -- and safety data supports -- that age is not a key determinant of collision risk in this context. The government recently lowered the minimum age for train drivers to 18, providing an adjacent precedent.
2. **Pre-licence training**: new recruits cannot begin Driver CPC theory and off-road modules until after their provisional licence is granted, a process that often takes weeks to months. By the time licences arrive, candidates have accepted positions elsewhere. CPT wants this restriction removed.

No government response confirmed yet. This campaign is ongoing. Source: [CPT-UK](https://www.cpt-uk.org/news/cpt-calls-for-government-action-to-make-it-easier-to-hire-bus-and-coach-drivers/)

The Coach & Bus UK show is running 27 July at Carver Barracks, near Saffron Walden, Essex. Annual trade event for operators to see new vehicles and products. No specific policy announcements expected from the show but it is a relationship-building venue for the sector.
