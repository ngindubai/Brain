## Story 1: Ceasefire declared over — third round of US strikes, Strait of Hormuz redeclared closed

**Source:** CBS News | https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-trump-ceasefire-talks-strait-of-hormuz/ | South China Morning Post | https://www.scmp.com/news/world/middle-east/article/3360254/iran-declares-strait-hormuz-closed-us-launches-new-strikes | Middle East Eye (via search) | The War Zone | https://www.twz.com/news-features/u-s-strikes-iran-in-retaliation-for-multiple-attacks-on-shipping-in-strait-of-hormuz-over-last-24-hours
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Trump said earlier this week that the US-Iran memorandum of understanding was "over," calling further dealing with Tehran "a waste of time." That was tested Saturday night: at 7:15pm ET, US Central Command began a third round of strikes this week against Iran, after IRGC forces attacked the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy as it transited the Strait using what Iran called an "unauthorised route." The ship suffered significant engine-room damage and fire; one crew member is missing. CENTCOM's statement was pointed: Iran had been "provided yet another opportunity to demonstrate adherence to the Memorandum of Understanding" and "has again failed." Iran's navy responded by redeclaring the Strait closed "until further notice," and Middle East Eye reported explosions on Qeshm Island — part of Iran's "arch defence" near the Strait — in the early hours of Sunday local time.

Trump has separately threatened to "decimate and destroy" Iran over any attempt on his life, stating "1,000 missiles are locked and loaded and aimed at" Iran; Iran's supreme leader vowed revenge in a Saturday statement. Diplomatically, the picture is not fully dead: Iranian FM Araghchi met his Omani counterpart Saturday to discuss transit mechanisms under Article 5 of the Islamabad MoU, and an Iranian delegation travelled to Oman the same day to continue indirect talks via Qatari and Pakistani mediators. Iranian officials have privately told Trump advisers they "made a mistake" in the ship attacks and want talks resumed — a genuine divergence between Tehran's private and public postures worth flagging. No adversary-state (RT/TASS/IRGC) framing of this specific weekend's events could be sourced despite repeated searches, a gap now three reports running; treated as a coverage limitation, not as those outlets going quiet.

Tanker traffic through the Strait, already reduced to roughly 13-14 vessels a day against a ~130/day norm before this weekend, is the number to watch into Monday. This is now the single most consequential story for Monday's oil and equity open — see today's Markets report for the direct read-through.

Separate from the ship-and-Strait story above, CBS reports the UAE and Bahrain's own air defence systems were actively engaging Iranian missile and drone attacks in the early hours of this Sunday morning — the direct regional-spillover consequence of Saturday night's US strikes. This is not a new pattern (the UAE has intercepted Iranian missiles and drones on multiple occasions since May), but it is a fresh, today-dated instance, and the first since the ceasefire's collapse this week. Precise interception counts specific to today were not independently confirmable from a source dated to this exact date at time of writing — the UAE Ministry of Defence's standard practice has been to publish exact figures (ballistic missiles/drones intercepted, any debris impact) within hours, and that statement should be checked directly tomorrow.

## Story 3: Ukraine takes a heavy overnight missile barrage; Russian strikes continue on civilian infrastructure

**Source:** Ukrinform (via search) | https://www.ukrinform.net/rubric-ato
**Related vault notes:** none directly

Twelve missiles, roughly half of them ballistic, were launched at Ukraine overnight into Saturday, with Ukrainian air defences unable to intercept all of them. This follows a run of deadly strikes through the week: eight killed in shelling of the Kramatorsk community (Donetsk region) on 10 July, and rising death/injury tolls from earlier strikes on Sumy and Zaporizhzhia. Separately, Ukrainian forces struck 21 Russian tankers, four tugboats, two dry cargo ships and a dredger in the Sea of Azov — a notable escalation in Ukraine's campaign against Russia's shadow-fleet and Azov shipping. No single development here rises to a standalone story on its own merits against the Gulf news, but the pattern — intensifying missile barrages plus a new maritime campaign against Russian Azov shipping — is worth carrying as a continuing thread into the EU's 21st sanctions package, due for adoption tomorrow (13 July).

## Story 4: DR Congo's Ebola outbreak officially confirmed in a fourth province

**Source:** CIDRAP | https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/ebola/ebola-outbreak-spreads-4th-province-dr-congo | Xinhua/CGTN (state-media framing, read accordingly) | https://english.news.cn/africa/20260711/7c08149a43bb4c56a5509c3a5a3f2ced/c.html
**Related vault notes:** none directly

Haut-Uele province officially declared an Ebola outbreak on Friday 11 July, after the National Institute for Biomedical Research confirmed seven fatal cases of Bundibugyo Ebola in the Wamba health zone, traced to a patient who had travelled from Ituri, the outbreak's epicentre. This is the fourth province now affected (after Ituri and others), and Haut-Uele borders both South Sudan and the Central African Republic — a genuine cross-border risk that wasn't captured in yesterday's report. Case counts have climbed past 1,300 confirmed with over 370 deaths as of end-June figures; frontline health workers in Ituri have separately threatened to strike over unpaid benefits and poor conditions, an operational risk to the response itself. No direct Orwell relevance beyond regional humanitarian awareness, but worth tracking given the cross-border spread pattern.

## Story 5: China formalises a public enforcement mechanism for critical-minerals export violations

**Source:** Morgan Lewis client alert | https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/07/recent-china-export-control-actions-signal-active-enforcement-for-rare-earths-and-strategic-minerals
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Effective 1 July, China established a formal public reporting mechanism encouraging organisations and individuals to report suspected violations of its export controls on rare earths and strategic minerals — a shift toward active enforcement rather than the rule-setting phase of the past year. Recent enforcement actions bracket the announcement: two Japanese nationals were detained in Dalian in May over alleged rare-earth-related smuggling, and a Chinese precision-optics company's chairman was placed under compulsory measures by Shanghai Customs in June for falsely declaring germanium-containing lenses as ordinary optical glass to dodge licensing. This sits in the same picture as yesterday's story on China easing Nvidia H200 access for its top AI firms — Beijing simultaneously loosening chip-import friction while tightening critical-mineral export enforcement, two levers moving in different directions on the same broader compute supply chain. Direct relevance to the GPU compute venture's input-cost and supply-chain planning.
