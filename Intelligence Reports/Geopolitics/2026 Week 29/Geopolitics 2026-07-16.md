## Story 1: Iran declares the ceasefire MOU void, calls itself in an "existential war" with the US

**Source:** [Al Jazeera live coverage](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/7/16/iran-war-live-sirens-in-bahrain-kuwait-us-disables-iranian-oil-tanker), [Al Jazeera, "Iran says peace deal voided"](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/15/iran-says-peace-deal-voided-fighting-existential-war-after-us-attacks), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/15/nx-s1-5894582/us-iran-updates), [Semafor](https://www.semafor.com/article/07/15/2026/trump-threatens-irans-civilian-infrastructure-as-war-resumes)
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]], [[Markets]]

Tehran's foreign ministry said today that repeated US strikes had voided the Islamabad memorandum of understanding signed 17 June, and that Iran's top negotiator now describes the country as fighting an "essential and existential war with America" with "complete freedom of action" against what it calls US aggression — a sharper framing than at any point since the June ceasefire. The facts on the ground back it up: the US naval blockade of Iranian ports, terminals and coastal waters — reinstated 14 July after Washington declared the MOU collapsed — remains in force; fresh US strikes hit Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas and Chabahar overnight, reportedly killing seven Iranian troops; and the US Navy disabled the Curaçao-flagged tanker Belma with Hellfire missiles after it ignored blockade warnings while attempting to reach an Iranian port. Bahrain activated air-raid sirens again; Kuwait's air defences remained active against what it called ongoing Iranian aggression. Trump, speaking on Fox News last night, gave Tehran one week to reach a deal before the US expands targeting to power plants and bridges — a threat he has made and deferred at least three times before (most recently backing off in April), but the surrounding context is now materially worse than those earlier instances: a formally voided MOU, a reinstated blockade, and four consecutive days of exchanged fire rather than an isolated incident. The framing divergence worth flagging: Iranian state channels present this as legitimate self-defence against an aggressor that reneged first; US and Gulf-state statements present it as Iran abandoning a deal it signed weeks ago. Both narratives are being used to justify further escalation, which is itself the risk — neither side currently has an off-ramp that doesn't look like capitulation.

## Story 2: Ukraine's parliament votes today on Koretskyi as new PM, hours after fresh missile strikes on Kyiv

**Source:** [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/15/zelenskyy-backs-state-energy-company-chief-koretskyi-for-new-pm), [Kyiv Independent](https://kyivindependent.com/)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Ukraine's parliament is expected to vote today on Sergii Koretskyi, head of state energy firm Naftogaz, as the country's next prime minister — the second cabinet reshuffle in a year, following parliament's acceptance of Yulia Svyrydenko's resignation on Tuesday. Koretskyi has no political affiliation but a track record of keeping Ukraine's gas supply intact through two wartime winters; Zelensky's stated rationale is preparing for another difficult winter and strengthening relations with foreign partners, with particular scrutiny on whether Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov keeps his post given recent battlefield gains from Ukraine's long-range drone campaign. The reshuffle lands hours after Russia hit Kyiv with ballistic missiles shortly before 1am local time; Ukrainian air defence reported shooting down 101 of 122 drones launched since 14 July. Separately, the EU-Ukraine Defence Industrial Partnership and "Drone Deal" signed yesterday during von der Leyen's Kyiv visit — a €10 billion program (roughly €6bn drones, €1.5bn missiles, €2.5bn Gripen jets) plus an immediate €1bn disbursement — starts converting into procurement now that the political leadership question is being resolved in parallel.

## Story 3: EU freezes the Russian oil price cap for one more week — Greece the lone holdout

**Source:** [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/07/10/eu-countries-rush-to-avoid-disastrous-revision-of-russian-oil-price-cap), Kyiv Independent reporting pattern
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

EU ambassadors agreed yesterday to freeze the Russian oil price cap at $44.10/barrel for another week rather than let it auto-revert toward roughly $58 today as scheduled — a stopgap, not a resolution. The single blocker is Greece's objection to a proposed EU-wide ban on trading LNG to third countries, part of the broader 21st sanctions package; earlier rounds of the same package were held up by Portugal (cod) and Bulgaria (sanctioning Patriarch Kirill and Vagit Alekperov), both since resolved. Negotiations continue to 23 July. This sits directly on top of the Hormuz-driven oil premium in Story 1 — two independent upward pressures on the global oil price floor, neither yet resolved, both flowing straight through to UAE energy and freight costs.

## Story 5: China's new critical-minerals whistleblower portal goes live, tightening the export-control chokepoint further

**Source:** [Morgan Lewis](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/07/recent-china-export-control-actions-signal-active-enforcement-for-rare-earths-and-strategic-minerals), [CSIS](https://www.csis.org/analysis/chinas-new-rare-earth-and-magnet-restrictions-threaten-us-defense-supply-chains)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

China's Ministry of Commerce Announcement No. 26 — a formal reporting mechanism, including a dedicated hotline and online portal, for the public to report unauthorised exports of strategic minerals — took effect 1 July and is now in active use, alongside a pattern of enforcement actions logged through June and July: two Japanese nationals detained in Dalian over alleged rare-earth smuggling, a Shanghai optics-company chairman detained over mis-declared germanium content in exported lenses, and ten US entities (including rare-earth miners MP Materials and USA Rare Earth) added to China's own export control list. This isn't a single today-dated event so much as a hardening pattern worth tracking as one thread: Beijing is building genuine enforcement infrastructure around critical-minerals export controls, not just issuing headline restrictions — the same "licences look permissive, enforcement is tight" pattern flagged in this week's Nvidia H200 coverage, mirrored on the Chinese side of the ledger.
