## Story 1: Khamenei's Funeral Begins — And a New Israeli Threat Against His Son Lands in the Same Week

**Source:** Wikipedia (State funeral of Ali Khamenei, cross-checked) | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_funeral_of_Ali_Khamenei | Times of Israel | https://www.timesofisrael.com/pakistan-says-pm-sharif-a-key-us-iran-mediator-will-attend-khamenei-funeral-ceremony/ | Express Tribune | https://tribune.com.pk/story/2615919/araghchi-warns-israel-of-powerful-response-over-katzs-death-threat-against-mojtaba-khamenei | Fox News live blog | https://www.foxnews.com/live-news/us-iran-war-strait-hormuz-trump-mou-july-2
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Today, Friday 3 July, is the first formal day of Ali Khamenei's state funeral: a ceremony for foreign dignitaries in Tehran this afternoon, ahead of the body lying in state and a cross-country procession running through Qom, Iraq (Najaf and Karbala) and ending with burial at the Imam Reza shrine in Mashhad on 9 July. Roughly 100 countries are sending representatives — heads of state, parliament speakers, foreign ministers or envoys. Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, a lead Doha mediator, is in Iran from 3-5 July specifically for this. Senior Russian, Chinese and Indian officials are also attending, which is itself a data point on where Tehran's post-war diplomatic alignment sits.

**Note on schedule reporting:** sources genuinely disagree on the exact day-by-day sequence — Wikipedia's own page gives one timeline (3-4 July Tehran, 5 July Qom, 7 July Iraq, 8 July Mashhad), Iran International gives another (4 July Tehran start, burial 9 July Mashhad), and Pakistani/Israeli press coverage (Times of Israel, Geo TV) describes today, 3 July, as the dignitaries' ceremony with the procession still to come. That spread of dates across otherwise credible outlets is itself worth flagging — even the logistics of Iran's biggest state event in years aren't being reported consistently, which says something about how chaotic information flow out of Tehran currently is.

**The genuinely new and dangerous thread:** Israeli Defence Minister Israel Katz said this week that Iran's actual current Supreme Leader — Mojtaba Khamenei, Ali Khamenei's son and successor since March — is "marked for death," alongside dismissive comments calling Iranians "good merchants" trying to extract concessions in the Doha talks. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi responded publicly that "any threat against our people and leadership will receive immediate powerful response" and separately told the US to "muzzle its pets" in Tel Aviv. Mojtaba Khamenei will reportedly skip his own father's funeral over security concerns arising directly from this threat. Separately, Ali Abdollahi, commander of Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya joint military command, publicly warned the US and Israel this week to "avoid any miscalculation" and think about the "harsh retaliation" Iran's armed forces would deliver to any attack during the funeral period.

**Why this matters more than a routine threat exchange:** this is happening in the exact week the formal US-Iran channel is frozen, with no mediator contact scheduled until after 9 July. If Israel or Iran acts on any of this rhetoric while Doha is dark, there's no immediate diplomatic backstop to de-escalate it — a genuine gap, not the calm pause the "talks paused for the funeral" headline implies.

## Story 2: Hormuz — Iran's Military Issues a "Forceful Response" Order as Parliament Votes on the MoU That's Supposed to Prevent Exactly That

**Source:** TWZ (via search) | https://www.twz.com/category/iran | US News/Times of Israel (Katz/Araghchi threads above) | Bloomberg (Rubio Gulf tour) | https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/rubio-rejects-hormuz-tolls-after-touring-anxious-gulf-nations
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]]

Two structural threads sharpened today. First: Iran's joint military command this week upgraded its posture from "warn stray vessels" to an explicit "immediate and forceful response" order against any ship not using Tehran-designated Hormuz routes — a harder line than the grounding-near-Larak-Island incident logged in yesterday's report. Second, and more consequential: Iran's parliament is voting on ratification of the June MoU this week (reporting places the vote around 2-3 July, timing not fully confirmed independently), with analysts flagging rejection as the live risk. The dangerous part is the calendar: Khamenei's funeral locks Iranian domestic politics into six days of succession-adjacent ceremony from today, which several analysts (TWZ, cross-checked against ACLED's framing of Iran's "narrowing path" on maritime control) describe as a window where hardliners could push through a blockade-formalising vote before the funeral period constrains political bandwidth, and before it becomes harder to unwind once ratified.

Underneath that sits the toll dispute that's been simmering since April: Iran's parliament construction-committee chair Mohammad Rezaei-Kouchi has said Iran plans a law barring boats from "hostile" states outright while charging tolls to everyone else, once the 60-day free-passage window in the MoU expires around mid-August. Secretary of State Rubio has already rejected this outright from Bahrain (25 June), calling it something that would spread "like a contagion" to other international waterways if accepted, and said Gulf states themselves have "zero support" for it. Iran's own senior sources say Tehran will pursue this "even if it has to do so by force" and "would not back down even if it led to renewed — and intensified — confrontation with the US." None of this is resolved; all of it is now sitting unaddressed through the funeral week with no formal channel to work it.

**Divergence on shipping data:** yesterday's Kpler figure was 34 verified crossings on 30 June. TWZ's more recent figure (2 July) puts traffic at 28% of pre-war levels — 27 vessels/day, tanker transits specifically at 22% (12/day). These aren't necessarily contradictory (different methodologies, different day), but the trend read is the same either way: "operational continuity, not a settled return to normal."

## Story 4: Ukraine Widens Its Refinery Campaign East to Kstovo, After Russia's Deadly Kyiv Strike Killed at Least 22

**Source:** Al Jazeera | https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/2/kyiv-attacked-after-ukraines-zelenskyy-warns-of-massive-russian-strike | CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/02/russia-launches-missile-drone-strikes-ukraine-kyiv.html | TASS | https://tass.com/politics/2154381
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The bigger event in this thread happened Wednesday night into Thursday: Russia launched a mass missile-and-drone strike on Kyiv that killed at least 22 people and destroyed 64 apartments in one residential building, after Zelenskyy had publicly warned a "massive" Russian strike was coming. Russia's Defence Ministry described it as retaliation for Ukrainian strikes on Russian "civilian infrastructure" (Moscow's framing — Ukraine's strikes have targeted refineries and military-linked sites, not civilian infrastructure by its own account, so this is a direct factual divergence between the two sides worth flagging rather than resolving). Zelenskyy vowed Ukraine would "definitely" retaliate.

**What's new today:** overnight Thursday into Friday, Ukraine's military said it struck one of Russia's largest oil refineries at Kstovo — several hundred miles east of Moscow, a materially different target than the Kapotnya plant (10 miles from the Kremlin) hit twice earlier this week — along with a railway bridge over the Donets River used for Russian military logistics, and a Russian command-and-observation post near Kharkiv. This is the promised reprisal, and the geographic widening (Moscow-area to deep interior) signals Ukraine's strike campaign has range and is not limiting itself to symbolic near-Kremlin targets. On the Russian side, TASS reported that General Staff chief Gerasimov briefed Putin on the retaliatory Kyiv strike results, with Kremlin spokesman Peskov (Russian government framing, read defensively) asserting Russian strikes hit only "military or military-related facilities" — a claim inconsistent with the reported civilian death toll and residential damage, and worth flagging as the kind of divergence that doesn't resolve from open reporting alone.

The underlying fuel-rationing crisis (roughly 55 of 83 Russian federal entities still rationing, gasoline export ban to 31 July) is unchanged and continues to deepen with each additional refinery strike.
