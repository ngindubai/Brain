## Story 1: Iran — Hormuz shipping data contradicts the "normalisation" narrative; Khamenei's state funeral deepens succession uncertainty

**Source:** straits.live | https://straits.live/ | hormuzstraitmonitor.com | https://hormuzstraitmonitor.com/ | CSIS | https://www.csis.org/analysis/strait-hormuz-8-charts | Middle East Eye | https://www.middleeasteye.net/news/israel-eyes-return-gaza-war-within-months-channel-12-reports (funeral reference) | Al Jazeera | https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/6/khamenei-family-mourns-but-mojtabas-absence-fuels-public-insecurity
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Comparison Network]]

Two threads on Iran worth separating and then reconnecting. On shipping: multiple live trackers (straits.live, hormuzstraitmonitor.com) show the Strait of Hormuz still running at roughly 32% of pre-crisis volume — about 27 vessel transits per day against a normal ~84 — more than three weeks after the 17 June US-Iran memorandum of understanding on toll-free passage. War-risk insurance is still pricing at roughly 8x pre-crisis levels, with six P&I clubs having withdrawn cover entirely. This directly contradicts oil-market commentary picked up in today's Markets research describing Hormuz flows as "fully restored" — a real divergence between financial-press framing (reading OPEC+'s output-hike decision and modestly softer oil prices as confirmation of normalisation) and primary shipping data (showing only a limited, uneven restart). The gap matters because it's exactly the kind of thing markets tend to reprice suddenly once it's noticed, not gradually.

On succession: Iran's six-day state funeral for Ali Khamenei — killed in the 28 February US-Israeli strike that also wounded his son and named successor, Mojtaba Khamenei — is drawing dignitaries from dozens of countries, per Al Jazeera reporting. Mojtaba has still not appeared in public since the strike, and his continued absence from his own father's funeral proceedings is now being read domestically as a genuine insecurity signal rather than simple caution, per Al Jazeera's framing. The two threads connect directly: the same February war that killed Iran's Supreme Leader is the proximate cause of the Hormuz disruption still showing in shipping data today, five months on. Neither the succession question nor the shipping recovery is close to resolved, and both feed the same regional risk-premium picture [[Comparison Network]] has to price.

## Story 5: China test-fires submarine-launched ballistic missile alongside Russia; Taiwan, Japan, Australia, New Zealand condemn

**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]]

China test-fired a long-range ballistic missile (dummy warhead) from a nuclear-powered submarine in the South Pacific at 12:01pm local time yesterday, 6 July, per state broadcaster Xinhua, timed to the start of the "Joint Sea-2026" exercise with Russia at Qingdao — where a Russian guided-missile cruiser, the Varyag, had arrived the day before. The China-Russia joint naval framing is the notable escalation here beyond the missile test itself: a bilateral military exercise timed within days of both the SLBM test and ongoing PLA leadership-reshuffle reporting this brief has tracked as the more direct near-term indicator of Taiwan Strait risk than any single legal or missile event alone.

## Story 6: Washington opens forced-labour tariff hearings targeting China, 60 countries

**Source:** PBS NewsHour | https://www.pbs.org/newshour/economy/u-s-says-it-plans-extra-tariffs-of-10-or-more-for-most-trading-partners-after-forced-labor-probe | ESG Dive | https://www.esgdive.com/news/us-eyes-new-tariffs-for-china-eu-mexico-and-more-after-labor-probes/822115/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The US Trade Representative opened public hearings today on a proposed Section 301 tariff of 12.5% on China stemming from a forced-labour investigation, part of a broader review covering 59 other countries the USTR alleges have failed to adequately prohibit or enforce against forced-labour imports. A Chinese government spokesperson denied the allegation and called for dialogue rather than confrontation. This runs alongside, not instead of, the existing US-China tariff truce (the November 2025 agreement holding reciprocal tariffs at 10% through 10 November 2026) — a reminder that the broader tariff truce doesn't cover every enforcement track Washington is running simultaneously, and forced-labour-based Section 301 actions sit outside the reciprocal-tariff framework entirely, meaning this can escalate without technically breaching the truce.
