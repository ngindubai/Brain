## Story 1: Iran war's 12th night — Trump threatens Iranian bridges and power plants for every Hormuz ship hit; Araghchi vows "eye for an eye"

**Source:** [Al Jazeera live blog](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/7/23/iran-war-live-us-launches-new-attacks-houthis-attack-2-saudi-oil-tankers)
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Ventures]], [[Markets]]

The US bombed Iran for a 12th consecutive night, with explosions reported in Bushehr, Ahvaz and Sirik. The escalatory news today is a specific new US threat: Trump said American forces would strike an Iranian bridge or power plant every time Iranian forces hit a ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz, a direct linkage of infrastructure targets to shipping attacks that wasn't stated this explicitly before. Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi responded by describing Tehran's defence doctrine as "an eye for an eye." UN official Martin Griffiths said diplomacy remained the best path forward, though no active negotiations were reported. Strait of Hormuz traffic remains near a three-week low per the Joint Maritime Information Center in Bahrain. This is an incremental but real escalation from yesterday's Pickaxe Mountain threat: yesterday's rhetoric targeted a single nuclear site, today's targets any bridge or power plant on a rolling, reactive basis, which is a broader and more open-ended threat structure. No independent corroboration is available today on Houthi capability to sustain attacks against Bab el-Mandeb shipping at scale; see Story 2.

## Story 2: Houthi forces claim strikes on two more Saudi-linked tankers, escalating the Red Sea front from threat to attack

**Source:** [Xinhua](https://english.news.cn/20260723/c9e6886dbe1a4a3497857d97eb1fad3b/c.html), search-indexed Seatrade Maritime/Gateway Pundit coverage
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Markets]]

The Houthi movement claimed today that it struck two more Saudi-linked tankers with drones and ballistic missiles: the Saudi-flagged product tanker Encelia, hit off Al Shuqaiq in the Red Sea and reported on fire (no casualties, according to maritime security firm Vanguard Tech), and the Bahri-owned VLCC Layla, also reported on fire though this second attack is not yet independently confirmed. The Houthis frame the strikes as enforcement of the port blockade they announced Monday, in retaliation for Saudi restrictions on Yemeni ports and last week's targeting of Sanaa airport. This is the direct answer to yesterday's open loop, whether Saudi Arabia's vow to protect its shipping "by all necessary measures" would be tested: it now has been, and the Bab el-Mandeb corridor is a live, attacked chokepoint rather than a declared-but-unenforced one. Worth a direct check tomorrow on whether Saudi Arabia responds militarily or restricts port access further, and on independent confirmation of the Layla strike.

## Story 3: Israel withdraws from a first Lebanon pilot zone; Hezbollah continues to reject the deal outright

**Source:** search-indexed Times of Israel/News-Tribune coverage
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Israeli troops withdrew today from Zawtar Al Gharbeye, one of the three southern Lebanon "pilot zones" under the 26 June framework, with the Lebanese Armed Forces moving in behind them. Lebanon's prime minister visited the village and planted a Lebanese flag, calling it the start of a complete Israeli withdrawal, coming two days after President Aoun's Washington meeting with Trump produced US-Lebanon flights but no firm withdrawal timeline. This is the concrete deliverable yesterday's brief flagged as pending. Hezbollah, however, continues to reject the ceasefire arrangement outright and is demanding unconditional, complete withdrawal rather than the phased pilot-zone approach, meaning today's move is a genuine step but not a resolution: the gap between "Israel is withdrawing from something" and "Hezbollah accepts the deal" remains the thing to watch, particularly given Trump's signal this week that he'd speak to Hezbollah directly if it helped.

## Story 4: Russian strikes cripple Black Sea shipping, killing nine crew across three vessels; Ukraine forces an emergency UN Security Council session

**Source:** search-indexed UNITED24 Media/Ukrinform/Interfax-Ukraine coverage
**Related vault notes:** —

Russian strikes on Ukraine's Black Sea grain corridor have hit at least three civilian cargo ships in recent days, killing several dozen crew combined, with the bulk carrier Golden Rose the latest, struck by a drone that hit its stern and started a major fire; all 16 foreign crew (14 Egyptian, two Syrian nationals) were evacuated safely by Ukrainian border guards. Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha said zero vessels passed through Ukraine's Black Sea corridor at what should be peak traffic, and called the strikes an attempt to "hold global food security hostage," warning that families across Africa, Asia, the Middle East and Latin America face rising prices and hunger if it continues. Ukraine has forced an emergency UN Security Council session, scheduled for 27 July. This is a distinct and separate thread from yesterday's Syrskyi-Drapatyi leadership story: it's Moscow escalating against civilian shipping and food-export infrastructure specifically, a global-supply-chain angle rather than a battlefield one, and worth tracking through the UNSC session for whatever, if anything, comes out of it.

## Story 5: Lavrov and Rubio meet face to face in Manila, first direct high-level US-Russia contact on Ukraine in weeks

**Source:** search-indexed Kyiv Independent coverage
**Related vault notes:** —

Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov confirmed today he'll meet US Secretary of State Marco Rubio on the sidelines of the ASEAN foreign ministers' conference in Manila, with Ukraine peace efforts on the agenda. Lavrov said he intends to press Rubio on Trump's recent comments about a quick resolution, and reiterated Russia's position that Washington hasn't formally rejected the Alaska summit proposals, understood to include a full Ukrainian withdrawal from Donbas; Rubio has previously disputed that any such proposal was agreed. This is the first confirmed direct high-level contact on Ukraine since talks froze, occurring the same day as Story 4's Black Sea escalation, an odd juxtaposition worth watching: whether the meeting produces anything beyond restating known positions is the open question for tomorrow.

## Story 6: DR Congo's protest deadline resolves peacefully: opposition stands down, national dialogue set with an 15 August deadline

**Source:** search-indexed Africanews/Rio Times coverage
**Related vault notes:** —

Yesterday's open flashpoint has an outcome: the C64 opposition coalition postponed its planned protest after President Tshisekedi announced a national dialogue, following a 17 July meeting with the country's main religious leaders. The opposition has given Tshisekedi and church mediators until 15 August to actually convene the talks, warning it will call for renewed civic action if that deadline slips. The underlying dispute, Tshisekedi's proposed constitutional referendum widely read as a vehicle for a third term, remains completely unresolved; today's news is procedural de-escalation, not a settlement. Genuinely good news in the sense that today passed without the violence seen in June's protests, but the clock now just moved to a new date.
