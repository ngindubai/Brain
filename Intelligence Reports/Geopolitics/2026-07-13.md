## Story 1: Hormuz crisis, today's read, no de-escalation, no fresh confirmed UAE toll

**Source:** [Manila Times](https://www.manilatimes.net/2026/07/13/world/americas-emea/us-strikes-iran-tehran-says-strait-of-hormuz-closed-gulf-states-hit/2382950/) (Reuters wire pickup), [NBC News live blog](https://www.nbcnews.com/world/iran/live-blog/live-updates-iran-attacks-gulf-us-strikes-tehran-ships-hormuz-oil-rcna353439), [IRNA](https://en.irna.ir/news/86206899/Iran-Oman-agree-to-continue-talks-on-Strait-of-Hormuz-shipping) (Iranian state media, framing only), [The National](https://www.thenationalnews.com/news/mena/2026/07/11/live-us-iran-war-trump/), [Khaleej Times](https://www.khaleejtimes.com/uae/uae-air-defences-respond-missile-drone-threats-july-12)

This is the one Middle East slot today, covering only what is new since yesterday's report, not a retelling of the weekend's strikes. Three things moved today. First, AP's world roundup reports the US military hit roughly 140 targets in Iran overnight into Monday, the clearest indication yet that Saturday night's third round of strikes was not a one-off. Second, Iran's new supreme leader (Mojtaba Khamenei, still not seen or heard in public since his father's assassination in February) issued his first attributed public statement of the war: "We pledge to avenge the blood of the martyred leader and all the martyrs," a rhetorical escalation with no operational content attached. Third, the diplomatic track kept moving in parallel with the military one: Iranian FM Araghchi's Muscat meeting with Omani counterpart Badr bin Hamad Al Albusaidi (Qatar also present) produced an agreement to continue "political and technical-legal consultations" on Strait of Hormuz shipping safety, not a resolution, a process.

**Where sources diverge:** Iranian state media (IRNA) frames the Oman talks as substantive progress toward a shipping security "common understanding." Western wire coverage (Reuters via NBC, Manila Times) frames the same meeting as Tehran hedging, continuing to talk while the IRGC continues to fire, consistent with the private "we made a mistake" message reported to Trump advisers in yesterday's cycle. The US position, per NBC, remains that Iran's closure claim is not legally recognised, the White House treats Hormuz as an international waterway regardless of Iranian declarations, so "closed" is a contested word, not a settled fact, in every filing today.

**The gap that matters:** Yesterday's report flagged a pending UAE Ministry of Defence statement with exact interception figures for this new wave. It is still pending. The UAE's last hard numbers (549 ballistic missiles, 29 cruise missiles, 2,260 drones intercepted cumulatively since the war's February start, current as of the 4 May lull) predate this weekend's resumption entirely. NCEMA has confirmed Sunday's threats stayed outside UAE borders and there is no reported UAE casualty from this round, Qatar reported three injured including a child from falling debris, Bahrain and Jordan reported intercepts with no confirmed casualties as of filing. A clean, dated MoD tally for the 12-13 July wave specifically has not been published. Treat any figure claiming to be "today's" UAE interception count with caution; one UAE-datelined wire item circulating with a precise 304/15/1,627 cumulative figure was checked against its own metadata and found to carry a March date, not July, and has been excluded here rather than repeated.

**Analysis:** The pattern across all three tracks, military, rhetorical, diplomatic, is escalation-with-a-door-left-open. That is the same characterisation as yesterday, which is itself the finding: this is now a stable equilibrium of low-grade war rather than a crisis moving cleanly toward either ceasefire or full regional war. For Gulf risk purposes, the absence of a confirmed UAE casualty or major infrastructure hit through two nights of interceptions is the single most reassuring fact in an otherwise deteriorating picture.

## Story 2: EU moves on two Russia sanctions tracks at once

**Source:** [EEAS/Consilium](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/sanctions-against-russia/timeline-sanctions-against-russia/), [Ukrainska Pravda](https://www.pravda.com.ua/eng/news/2026/07/09/8043181/), [European Pravda](https://www.eurointegration.com.ua/eng/news/2026/07/9/7241314/), [Kyiv Post](https://www.kyivpost.com/opinion/79623)

The EU Foreign Affairs Council met in Brussels today on two separate sanctions tracks that had been running in parallel. The first, a narrower package proposed by High Representative Kaja Kallas in direct response to the 1-2 July mass strike on Kyiv, targets five entities and one individual supplying components for Shahed- and Geran-type drones; sources described it as having broad member-state support going in. The second, the long-delayed 21st package, was up for adoption in a softened compromise form: roughly 90 additional banks blacklisted (pushing the total past 100, over half of Russia's internationally connected lenders), a six-month freeze on the existing oil price cap rather than a cut, and, per last-minute horse-trading reported yesterday, the removal of a proposed listing for Patriarch Kirill and softened language on an entry ban for Russian combatants. As of filing this evening Dubai time, wire coverage confirms the Council convened and the package was on the agenda for same-day adoption; formal confirmation of passage should be treated as pending until tomorrow's report, not yet a closed loop.

**Where sources diverge:** Kyiv-aligned outlets (Kyiv Post, Ukrainska Pravda) frame the softening, dropped Kirill listing, watered-down combatant ban, as evidence the EU's sanctions machine is losing teeth under member-state fatigue. Consilium's own framing is procedural and upbeat, presenting the bank count and price-cap freeze as the substantive story. Both are consistent with the same set of facts; the divergence is about which half of a mixed package gets emphasised.

**Analysis:** For Orwell's purposes this is a paper story, not a market one, the price cap freeze at $47.6/barrel (set at the 18th package) matters more for Russian export revenue than for Gulf oil pricing, but it does confirm the EU is treating the war as a long-haul sanctions grind rather than reaching for anything dramatic, which tracks with the Coalition of the Willing summit below.

## Story 3: Coalition of the Willing convenes in Paris, 37 nations, €70bn on the table

**Source:** [Kyiv Post](https://www.kyivpost.com/post/80035), [Ukrinform](https://www.ukrinform.net/rubric-polytics/4143007-zelensky-to-attend-coalition-of-the-willing-meeting-in-paris.html), [RBC-Ukraine](https://newsukraine.rbc.ua/news/france-announces-new-coalition-of-the-willing-1783699015.html)

Emmanuel Macron hosted the Coalition of the Willing in Paris today, at least 25 heads of state and government from a coalition that has grown to 37 nations, with Zelensky and NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte attending. On the table: continuation of the pledged €70bn in 2026 military assistance, an integrated air and missile defence system for Ukraine, parameters for licensed in-country production of Patriot interceptors, further pressure on Russia's shadow fleet, and deeper operational coordination among Ukraine's backers. The stated political purpose, per French framing, is to signal to Moscow that Western "conflict fatigue" narratives are wrong.

**Where sources diverge:** This is one of the cleaner stories today in terms of source agreement, Ukrainian, French and NATO framing all describe the same agenda and the same intent (deterrence signalling). The genuine open question, not addressed by any source checked today, is whether the €70bn is new money or a re-announcement of previously pledged funding; treat that as unconfirmed pending a formal Elysée or Council readout.

**Analysis:** Paired with Story 2, today reads as the EU/coalition apparatus running its normal cadence, sanctions plus funding plus summitry, against a war that, unlike Hormuz, has not had a ceasefire to collapse. No surprises, but confirmation that Western support architecture is still functioning at scale a week into the Iran crisis pulling attention elsewhere.

## Story 4: China's island-chain playbook gets a name, and a record vessel count

**Source:** [Taipei Times](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2026/07/13/2003860660) (Taiwan government-adjacent framing, read as such), background via [US News](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2026-07-07/china-japan-trade-conflicting-accounts-of-confrontation-around-senkaku-islands), [Japan Times](https://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2026/07/07/japan/japan-china-ships-expel-near-senkaku/)

A Taipei Times report published today lays out what a security source there calls China's three-phase playbook across the First Island Chain: state-backed academics reinterpret historical claims, China Coast Guard "law enforcement" patrols follow, then missile tests. The piece names three live applications: the Ryukyu Islands (questioning Japanese sovereignty since 2013, with Xi referencing "historical archives" in 2023), the Senkaku/Diaoyutai chain (Chinese coast guard vessels detected in the contiguous zone 357 days last year), and, newly, academic claims over the Philippines' Batanes Islands based on a selective reading of 1895-98 treaties. Today's hard data point: Taiwan has tracked over 110 Chinese naval and coast guard vessels around the First Island Chain, a record high, alongside a long-range submarine-launched ballistic missile test that overflew northern Luzon.

**Where sources diverge:** This is Taiwan-sourced framing describing Chinese intent as coordinated strategy; Chinese state outlets (not directly cited in today's piece but consistent with prior coverage of the 7 July Senkaku coast guard stand-off) describe the same coast guard activity as routine sovereignty patrols responding to Japanese "intrusion." Both framings agree on the underlying vessel-count facts; they disagree entirely on intent, which is the actual intelligence question here.

**Analysis:** A record vessel count and a missile overflight of Luzon on the same reporting day, while global attention sits on Hormuz and Ukraine, fits a recognisable pattern of using cover from other crises to advance harder in the Pacific. Worth flagging for [[Ventures]] given the GPU compute venture's exposure to any US-China trade or export-control shock, a First Island Chain incident involving Taiwan directly would move faster and harder on chip supply chains than anything currently in the Hormuz or Ukraine tracks.

## Story 5: Oil holds a real risk premium as Gulf shipping stays disrupted

**Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/12/oil-price-strait-hormuz-iran-trump-tanker.html), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-12/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-13), [EnergyConnects](https://www.energyconnects.com/news/oil/2026/july/oil-climbs-as-fresh-tanker-strike-highlights-risks-around-hormuz/), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/qatar-pauses-push-to-ramp-up-lng-after-hormuz-tanker-attack)

Brent rose toward $79 a barrel and WTI traded near $74 today, both up roughly 4% on the session, snapping a two-day dip, as the US-Iran exchange over the weekend kept a live risk premium in the market. Roughly 20% of global oil and gas trade transits Hormuz; shipping through it remains "significantly disrupted" per wire reporting rather than fully blocked. Separately, QatarEnergy's CEO has kept the Ras Laffan complex at minimum throughput since the 21 June Barzan plant explosion and has now also paused plans to ramp up output further given the Hormuz tanker-attack risk, a second, compounding reason for Qatari LNG caution layered on top of the original accident.

**Where sources diverge:** None significant, this is one of the more consensus-driven wire stories today; the only live disagreement is over magnitude of the risk premium itself, some analysts (per Bloomberg) treat the current ~$74-79 range as already pricing in a meaningful disruption, others frame it as still short of what a genuine full closure would produce, implying further upside if the crisis worsens rather than a ceiling already reached.

**Analysis:** This is the story that ties everything else in the brief back to Dubai. A sustained mid-$70s to high-$70s Brent price, absent a full closure, is manageable for UAE fiscal planning and unlikely by itself to force a CBUAE rate move, but the Qatari LNG caution and the still-open Hormuz insurance question (war-risk premiums on tanker cover have not been separately confirmed today but logically follow from continued attacks) both point toward the crisis being priced as structural rather than transient by the market that matters most.
