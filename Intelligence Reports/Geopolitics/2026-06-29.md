## LIVE UPDATE — evening 29 June 2026

**Iran strikes US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain.** In the early hours of 29 June, the IRGC launched ballistic missiles and drones at two US military sites: the Ali Al Salem airbase in Kuwait and Port Salman (US Fifth Fleet) in Bahrain. This is the most direct Iranian escalation against US forces since the June 17 MOU was signed. Kuwait's air defences intercepted two ballistic missiles with no casualties. In Bahrain, an Iranian projectile destroyed the top floor of a residential building near the international airport; no deaths reported. Qatar said one Qatari citizen sustained injuries from "regional military operations." Both Kuwait and Bahrain issued formal condemnations. Iran's IRGC said the strikes were "in retaliation for American aggression against Iranian territory" — specifically responding to the US strikes on Sirik, Bandar-e Lengeh, and Qeshm Island on 27 June, which were themselves retaliation for the Kiku tanker hit.

**The escalation chain as of this evening:** Ever Lovely struck 25 June → US hits Iran 26 June → Kiku struck 27 June → US hits Iran again 27 June → Iran strikes US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain 28-29 June → unknown next step. Trump has publicly stated "there may come a point when we are no longer able to be reasonable," which is language the markets will read as a threat of widened strikes. Iran skipped scheduled Sunday technical talks, citing unmet US commitments on frozen-asset access. The MOU has not been formally declared void by either side, but it is not being observed by either side in practice.

**Iran threatens to halt negotiations entirely.** Foreign Minister Araghchi warned that continued US strikes will "exacerbate the situation and delay reopening of the strait." The Iran MFA condemned US action as a violation of both the MOU and the UN Charter. Democratic Congressman Ro Khanna has argued the US strikes violated the War Powers Resolution. So far the Trump administration has not sought congressional authorisation for any action taken since February 28.

**Oil price context.** Brent fell to approximately $72/bbl on 26 June as Hormuz traffic recovered toward 75% of pre-war levels and Saudi Arabia began loading at Ras Tanura. The June 28-29 strikes are a fresh upside shock. Markets closed before the Iran-Bahrain/Kuwait attacks broke; the Monday open will be the first real-time price signal. Pre-war Brent was approximately $70; the war peak was $119.94 (Brent front month, late April). The current $72 level already prices in significant Hormuz reopening progress — any reversal of that progress would move it quickly back toward $85-95 range.

**UAE exits OPEC.** The UAE formally announced its withdrawal from OPEC during the April conflict period, timed to avoid maximum disruption but signalling a fundamental break with Saudi Arabia over quota discipline. The motivation is straightforward: the UAE wants unconstrained production expansion once Hormuz reopens. ADNOC has been targeting 5 mbd capacity and OPEC quotas have blocked it. The withdrawal removes the UAE as OPEC's swing-capacity shock absorber. For the comparison network: post-Hormuz reopening UAE oil revenue policy will be set in Abu Dhabi, not Riyadh — a material shift for the fiscal backdrop that drives property and mortgage liquidity. [[Ventures]] note: ADNOC's production expansion is infrastructure capex at scale, which feeds regional economic activity and demand for services the comparison network indexes.

**China's rare earth counter-move.** On 22 June, Beijing added 10 US entities to its export control list, including rare earth developers MP Materials (Mountain Pass, California) and USA Rare Earth (Texas mine-to-magnet supply chain). Both companies are explicitly designed to reduce US dependence on Chinese rare earths. By targeting them, China signals it will sanction the very firms building the alternative. War on the Rocks characterises this as "Beijing actively targeting American companies building domestic rare earth processing capacity." The practical impact is currently limited — both companies say they have already cut Chinese supply ties — but the strategic intent is clear: China is willing to weaponise the chokepoint in critical minerals the same way Iran has weaponised Hormuz. For [[Ventures]] and GPU compute, the magnet supply chain for data-centre cooling, EV drivetrains, and defence systems runs through this contested terrain.

**Russia-Ukraine: Kyiv's 40-day escalation.** Ukraine launched its largest drone attack of the past year (660 drones intercepted) targeting a dozen Russian regions plus Crimea. Zelenskyy has ordered a "40-day influence operation" aimed at forcing Russia to the negotiating table, having received G7 commitments at the recent summit. Russia's front-line stall correlates with a rise in desertion requests. Putin has signalled openness to talks as the Russian economy weakens further. The Iran war has absorbed the Western and Gulf media oxygen, but the Ukraine front is quietly reaching a critical juncture — if the 40-day campaign produces a ceasefire offer, it will be a major geopolitical event with knock-on effects for European energy and reconstruction investment.

**US-China: 60-day tariff pause in force.** A trade deal announced 11 June keeps the 20% fentanyl tariff and 10% reciprocal tariff (30% total) in place while pausing higher rates for 60 days. The US has also initiated Section 301 investigations into forced-labour practices by 60 economies including China, the EU, and Japan. A new US-China Board of Trade framework is in consultation. The key risk for the comparison network: if the 60-day pause expires without a deal, tariff escalation resumes during a period when global supply chains are already stressed by the Hormuz disruption.

## Editor's note

One story dominates and it is on Gareth's doorstep. The Strait of Hormuz reopening is not holding. A US-Iran memorandum was signed on 17 June, the blockade lifted on 18 June, yet within the last 72 hours Iran has struck two vessels on the one corridor the US Navy designated as safe, the US has retaliated against Iranian targets, and freight rates have hit a 22-month high. This sits 100km from Gareth's front door and drives the oil price, the UAE economy, regional insurance costs, and by extension the property and mortgage markets the comparison network depends on. Everything else today is secondary to this. The deeper pattern across all of today's stories is the same one the tech brief keeps surfacing from a different angle: the rules-based order is fragmenting into spheres, chokepoints are being weaponised, and middle powers are hedging rather than aligning.

## Story 1: The Hormuz reopening is failing in real time
**Spine (wire/primary):** [CBS live](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-israel-hezbollah-strait-of-hormuz-peace-deal-talks/) | [Congress.gov CRS R45281](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45281) | [Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/from-chokepoint-to-crisis-the-strait-of-hormuz-and-global-oil-markets/)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Comparison Network]]

The factual sequence, stripped of any side's framing. The US and Iran signed the Islamabad Memorandum on 17 June to end the war that began on 28 February 2026 (when US and Israeli strikes killed Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei). The US lifted its naval blockade on 18 June. The strait began reopening. Then it started coming apart. On 25 June a projectile struck the Singapore-flagged container ship Ever Lovely on the Omani corridor as it exited. On 26 June CENTCOM struck Iranian missile, drone and radar sites on Qeshm Island and the southern coast in response. On 27 June the JMIC (the US Navy-overseen Joint Maritime Information Center) widened the Omani coastal corridor to allow two-way traffic, and within hours the Panama-flagged crude tanker Kiku was struck by a projectile on that same corridor, damaging its bridge.

The hard operational facts as of today: the central channel of the strait remains blocked by approximately 80 IRGC-laid naval mines. The Omani coastal corridor is the only route the JMIC designates as viable, and it has now been hit twice in 48 hours. The IMO has paused its framework to evacuate the 11,000-plus seafarers stranded since February. Major container lines have not resumed normal schedules. Kpler counted 70 Hormuz crossings on 24 June (up 105% day-on-day) before the latest strikes, so traffic was rebuilding until the shocks. Around 20 million barrels per day, roughly 20% of world seaborne oil and 27% of maritime oil trade, normally transits here.

Now the divergence, which is the actual intelligence. **Washington's framing:** Trump called the Ever Lovely strike "a foolish violation of our ceasefire agreement," the US and Gulf Arab states reject any Iranian claim to control passage, and the JMIC corridor widening is a deliberate signal that the US, not Iran, governs the waterway. **Tehran's framing (read defensively, as position not fact):** Iran's IRGC argues it struck the Kiku because the vessel used a route outside Tehran-designated lanes, and cites clause 5 of the 17 June MOU as granting Iran authority over passage arrangements. Foreign Minister Araghchi warned that "any attempt to adopt new or separate arrangements" beyond what Iran is running will "increase the tensions." The IRGC says transit is safe only on Iran-approved routes.

That gap is the whole story. The US says the strait is reopening under US coordination. Iran says nothing transits without its say-so and has demonstrated it can hit the "safe" corridor at will. Regional energy analysts side with the uncomfortable read: Iran retains effective deterrence over Hormuz regardless of what the MOU stipulates, and markets have not resolved the gap between Washington's stated position and the reality that one drone reopens the risk premium. The Brookings analysis put it bluntly weeks ago: Iran has shown that threats and a few attacks can effectively block the strait. Nothing since has disproved that.

**Why this matters for Gareth specifically.** The UAE's Habshan-Fujairah pipeline is running at full capacity (1.8 mbd to Fujairah, bypassing the strait), which partially insulates UAE exports, but the regional risk premium, insurance costs, and the broader Gulf economic climate all flow from this. A sustained or re-escalating Hormuz crisis pushes oil up, feeds UAE inflation, and changes the CBUAE rate path, which feeds straight into the mortgage and property dynamics the [[Comparison Network]] tracks. For the [[Ventures]] GPU compute thesis, energy price volatility is an input cost variable. Watch the corridor daily.

## Story 2: The Lebanon track threatens the whole deal
**Spine:** [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/23/nx-s1-5867322/us-iran-finalize-war-ending-deal) | [House of Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10637/)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The Hormuz crisis cannot be separated from Lebanon, and this is the linkage most single-source coverage misses. Iran has demanded that a full truce in Lebanon between Israel and Hezbollah be part of any comprehensive deal. On 26 June, Secretary of State Rubio announced a trilateral Israel-Lebanon-US framework agreement, signed in Washington. But analysts immediately questioned what it actually changes: it is not obviously different from the ceasefire already in place, which Hezbollah has defied with near-daily friction. Israeli military chief Eyal Zamir approved "continued operations in the security zone," and Israel's defence minister said the country will not withdraw from land seized in Lebanon. Hezbollah said there will be "no return to the situation that existed before March 2."

The mechanism that makes this a Hormuz story: every time fighting flares in southern Lebanon, Iran cites it as a violation and tightens the strait. Iran closed the strait again on 20 June over Israeli actions in Lebanon. So the chain is Lebanon friction → Iranian justification → Hormuz disruption → oil spike. Anyone watching only the oil price without watching Lebanon is watching the effect and missing the cause. The framework signed on 26 June is the kind of paper agreement that has repeatedly been announced and not implemented on the ground, so treat any "Lebanon resolved" headline with scepticism until the daily strike reports actually stop.

## Story 3: The nuclear-inspection dispute that could unravel the MOU
**Spine:** [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/23/nx-s1-5867322/us-iran-finalize-war-ending-deal) | [2025-26 Iran-US negotiations (event tracker)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025%E2%80%932026_Iran%E2%80%93United_States_negotiations)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Beneath the shipping drama, the core dispute is unresolved: whether Iran has actually agreed to let UN inspectors view its bombed nuclear sites. As of last week the US and Iran were in open disagreement over whether this was ever conceded. This is the hinge of the entire permanent settlement. Vance's negotiating position included a striking detail: if Iranian frozen assets were unfrozen, the US wants them spent on American corn, wheat and soy, with the US and Qatar holding approval over the process. Iran's foreign ministry pushed back that import decisions would be based on "prices and quality," and spokesman Baghaei landed the sharpest line of the negotiations: "the philosophy and goal of the war, which was the destruction of the Iranian civilization... has become enriching American farmers."

That quote matters beyond rhetoric. It signals that Iran's negotiating class views the economic terms as humiliation dressed as peace, which is exactly the kind of grievance that makes a signed MOU fragile. A deal the losing side experiences as extortion is a deal with a short half-life. Hold the optimism on a permanent settlement.

## Story 4: Middle powers are hedging, not aligning
**Spine:** [EY Geostrategy June 2026](https://www.ey.com/en_gl/insights/geostrategy/geostrategic-analysis) | [Geopolitical Monitor](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/) | [Lazard 2026 trends](https://www.lazard.com/research-insights/top-geopolitical-trends-in-2026/)
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]], [[Ventures]]

Three threads that look separate but describe one pattern: the fragmentation of the US-led order into hedged, regionalised blocs.

First, Pakistan has emerged as the indispensable mediator of the Iran war, brokering ceasefires through army chief Asim Munir and hosting the Islamabad Memorandum. A nuclear-armed state most Western coverage treats as peripheral is now the diplomatic hinge of the Gulf. Iran's president visited Islamabad on 23 June. This is a genuine realignment that the wire services under-weight.

Second, the EU's 3 June Tech Sovereignty Package (Cloud and AI Development Act, Chips Act 2, aiming to triple EU data centre capacity in five to seven years) is Europe acting on the recognition that more than 80% of its key digital products and infrastructure are sourced externally. France is the loudest voice. This connects directly to the tech brief's Anthropic-ban fallout: the EU is treating dependence on US AI infrastructure as a strategic vulnerability, and the export-control weaponisation of frontier models has accelerated that.

Third, Canada aligned more closely with European partners than with Washington at the June G7, and joined the European Political Community summit in May, the first non-European country to do so. Lazard's framing for 2026 is that Washington's fusion of economic interventionism and transactional dealmaking is going global, and the age of governments as referees is giving way to governments as major players in the corporate arena.

For Orwell, the through-line is concrete: technological sovereignty is becoming a hard requirement, not a slogan. Single-vendor, single-jurisdiction dependence (the exact risk the Anthropic ban exposed for the [[Agent Org]] stack) is now a board-level concern for governments and enterprises alike. That is a tailwind for any positioning that emphasises model optionality and jurisdictional resilience.

## Story 5: The copper and critical-minerals squeeze behind the AI build-out
**Spine:** [Geopolitical Monitor](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/) | [Congress.gov CRS](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45281)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

A slower-burning structural story that ties the geopolitics brief to the tech and markets briefs. Copper is a key input for AI data centres, and copper supply chains carry heavy geopolitical and capital risk. The same chokepoint logic that governs oil governs critical minerals: the US-backed IMEC corridor (India-Middle East-Europe), designed to build resilience against weaponised chokepoints, was itself disrupted by the Iran war closing the very waters it relies on. So the infrastructure meant to reduce chokepoint risk was taken out by a chokepoint event. Europe is simultaneously bracing for Chinese overcapacity spreading from EVs into wind, solar and mature-node semiconductors.

The relevance to [[Ventures]] and the GPU compute thesis: the AI infrastructure build-out is exposed not just to chip supply (the Micron HBM story from the markets brief) but to raw-material and corridor risk that the headline AI coverage rarely connects. Copper, power, and shipping corridors are the physical substrate under the AI boom, and all three are now contested terrain. Anyone modelling compute economics on a multi-year horizon should treat critical-mineral and energy-corridor risk as a real input, not background noise.
