## Story 1: Google loses key DMCA claims against SerpApi — a meaningful precedent for SERP-scraping and rank-tracking tools

**Source:** Search Engine Land | https://searchengineland.com/google-loses-key-dmca-claims-against-serpapi-in-scraping-lawsuit-483185
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

The US District Court for the Northern District of California dismissed Google's DMCA claims against SerpApi on 20 July, in a lawsuit Google filed in December 2025 accusing SerpApi of bypassing SearchGuard — Google's anti-scraping defence — to extract and resell Google Search results. Claims covering non-copyrighted search results were dismissed outright; claims touching copyrighted content were dismissed with leave to amend, the court finding Google hadn't shown SearchGuard operated "with the authority of the copyright owner." Google has 21 days to refile. The court did reject SerpApi's broader argument that Google lacks DMCA standing at all, so this isn't a total loss for Google, but the practical effect is a real narrowing of Google's ability to use copyright law as a blunt instrument against scraping tools.

This matters beyond the two named parties: rank-tracking platforms, competitive-analysis tools, and any AI system that trains on or cites SERP data all face reduced legal exposure from this specific enforcement angle while the case plays out. It's not a green light for scraping Google outright — SearchGuard itself remains a live technical barrier regardless of the legal outcome — but it's a data point worth having on file the next time a "can we do this" question comes up about SERP data collection for the portfolio or comparison network's own competitive research.

## Story 2: A concrete framework for restructuring local service pages around semantics rather than page-count

**Source:** Search Engine Land | https://searchengineland.com/how-semantics-and-topical-authority-improve-local-seo-482980
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]]

Koray Tuğberk GÜBÜR published a detailed piece on using "query deserves a page" logic and semantic structuring for local service businesses: don't create a page for every location-service permutation, judge each variant on genuine search demand and entity uniqueness, and put the strongest location-service pairing on the homepage itself rather than diluting authority across dozens of near-duplicate city pages. A worked attorney example consolidated 19 similar city pages down to a handful and picked up over 232 new ranking queries as a result. The piece also argues Google has ranked functional pages — booking systems, comparison tools, filters — over purely informational ones since the Helpful Content Update, and recommends structuring pages with visual components (hero sections, review carousels, credential strips, service tabs) rather than long blocks of text.

This is directly actionable for every local-service vertical in the portfolio — pet transport, funeral repatriation, close protection, bus hire, pest control all run on location-service page structures, and the "when to consolidate vs. split" question is exactly the kind of decision these sites make constantly without a clear framework. Worth a genuine read against whichever portfolio site currently has the most location-page sprawl, rather than filing as generic advice.
