## Story 1: Google AI Mode shows ads on 1 in 3 commercial keywords — and pets is the most monetised niche

**Source:** Search Engine Journal (citing SE Ranking analysis) | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-ai-mode-shows-ads-on-1-in-3-commercial-keywords/582976/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

SE Ranking tested a large set of commercial keywords in Google AI Mode and found text ads appearing on 29% of them overall, rising sharply with commercial intent: 24% below $2 CPC, 32.45% between $2 and $10, and 53.56% above $10 CPC. Ad presence tracked cost-per-click far more tightly than search volume or keyword difficulty, meaning AI Mode's ad load is now a function of advertiser willingness to pay, not query popularity.

Two details matter more than the headline number. First, only 11% of the domains shown as ads also appeared as AI Mode source citations, and just 1.95% matched on exact URL — paying for a slot buys visibility, not credibility with the model, and the two channels should be tracked and budgeted separately. Second, and most relevant here: niche variation ran from 72% ad presence in Pets down to 2% in Healthcare. Pets is the *most* monetised commercial category AI Mode currently serves ads against.

That lands squarely on the pet-transport site in the [[SEO Portfolio]]. A 72% ad-presence rate means most pet-relocation and pet-transport-adjacent commercial searches now surface a paid unit inside AI Mode before any organic result, including the site's own. This is a visibility tax on the category, not a penalty on the site specifically, but it changes the arithmetic: if AI Mode is intercepting demand at the top of the funnel with paid units that don't correlate with organic rankings, the site's organic strategy needs to be judged against what's left after AI Mode's ad layer, not against the old SERP.

For the [[Comparison Network]], the CPC correlation is the more useful signal. Mortgage, insurance and legal-consultancy queries are exactly the high-CPC categories (above $10) where SE Ranking found ad presence over 53%. If UAE-market equivalents behave similarly, insurecompare-ae and mortgagecompare-ae should assume AI Mode is running paid units against their core commercial terms today, whether or not those sites have any presence in the citations themselves. Worth a manual spot-check of a handful of core comparison keywords in AI Mode this week — cheap to do, and it tells you whether the paid layer is already competing for the same clicks the network is chasing organically.

The broader point for GEO strategy: AI Mode monetisation and AI Mode citation are decoupling. Optimising to be cited is necessary but no longer sufficient if a paid unit sits above the citation anyway on high-value terms.

## Story 2: SerpApi beats back Google's DMCA lawsuit over SERP scraping

**Source:** Search Engine Journal / MediaPost | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/court-dismisses-googles-dmca-claims-against-serpapi/583033/ ; https://www.mediapost.com/publications/article/416687/judge-dismisses-google-complaint-against-serpapi-o.html
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

A federal judge (Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers, N.D. Cal.) has dismissed the core of Google's DMCA lawsuit against SerpApi, the SERP-scraping service Google sued in December for bypassing its SearchGuard anti-scraping system. The ruling draws a sharp line: DMCA anti-circumvention claims require circumvention of access controls on *copyrighted* material. Since raw organic search results aren't themselves copyrighted, blocking automated access to them and then getting bypassed isn't a DMCA violation — it's just a scraping dispute, which DMCA doesn't reach. The narrower claim over Knowledge Panel images (which do carry licensed copyright) survives, but only with leave for Google to amend and re-plead within 21 days; as it stands, it's dismissed too.

This is being reported today off a ruling issued 20 July, with SEJ and MediaPost both running it as breaking analysis this morning. SerpApi's CEO is framing it publicly as a win for "open access to public data," and it plainly matters beyond this one case: any SEO or rank-tracking tool that scrapes SERPs (Semrush, Ahrefs, Sistrix, the lot) has effectively been operating under the same legal cloud Google was trying to use against SerpApi. This ruling narrows Google's ability to use DMCA specifically as a scraping deterrent, though Google retains other levers (rate limiting, CAPTCHA walls, ToS breach-of-contract claims) that this case doesn't touch.

Read-through for Orwell: none of the portfolio or comparison-network sites are scraping Google directly, so there's no direct exposure. The relevance is upstream — the rank-tracking and competitor-research data Gareth's tools (Semrush included) rely on depends on scraping infrastructure whose legal footing just got marginally safer. Worth flagging only because a contrary ruling would have been a real risk to tool availability and pricing across the industry; this one removes a tail risk rather than creating an opportunity.

## Story 3: Google's Liz Reid describes "persistent autonomous search" as the next phase of Search

**Source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/googles-liz-reid-discusses-persistent-autonomous-search/582948/
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]], [[SEO Portfolio]]

Liz Reid, Google's head of Search, laid out a vision where a query stops being a one-shot transaction and becomes a standing background task: "you don't have to worry about it. When you're ready, you get the information, you can get direct to the source." Her examples — tracking a theatre show for tickets, monitoring a stock against specific parameters — describe search that keeps running after the user has closed the tab, and pushes a notification (in-app, cross-device, via Assistant) only once the answer clears a quality bar.

The mechanism that matters for SEO: Reid describes this activating when current results fail to meet completeness or authoritativeness standards at query time — i.e., the system doesn't settle for a mediocre answer now, it keeps checking sources until a good one appears, then serves it later. That inverts the usual SEO assumption that visibility is decided once, at the moment of the query. If Google is building infrastructure to keep re-evaluating sources against a task over time, being freshly correct and re-crawlable matters more than being highly ranked at any single instant.

This is squarely an Agent Org-relevant development rather than an immediate portfolio action item — it's the kind of protocol-level shift the Tech & AI report's Agentic Workflow Watch section should also be tracking, since it's as much an orchestration pattern (standing task, delayed fulfilment, cross-surface notification) as a search feature. No concrete SEO tactic follows from this yet; it's a six-to-twelve-month watch item, not a this-week fix. Flagging here so it isn't lost between reports.

## Story 4: Study finds 70% of top retailers structurally invisible to agentic commerce

**Source:** Search Engine Journal (Reza Moaiandin / SALT.agency) | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/70-of-top-retailers-are-invisible-to-agentic-commerce-heres-why/581198/
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[SEO Portfolio]]

SALT.agency audited 207 product detail pages from 29 top-traffic retailers, scoring 141 pages against a 10-point readiness rubric built on Google's Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP) documentation. The top-line number: 70% of retailers are missing all three UCP-mandatory fields (`priceValidUntil`, `shippingDetails.deliveryTime`, `merchantReturnDays`). Basic schema is nearly universal — 99% have price/availability/SKU, 100% have adopted product schema in some form — but the fields agents actually need to transact are largely absent. A further 65% are missing GTIN identifiers, which blocks cross-retailer price comparison entirely, and 15% of the sample (naming Adidas, UGG, Converse) is fully invisible to agents because of bot-blocking defences.

The framing worth keeping: this isn't a ranking problem, it's a data-completeness problem. Agentic commerce systems don't read page content or weigh backlinks — they extract structured fields from feeds and schema, and if the three mandatory fields aren't there, "your product won't be included, period," regardless of how well the page otherwise ranks.

Direct applicability to the portfolio is limited since none of the [[SEO Portfolio]] or [[Comparison Network]] sites are UCP-style product retailers. But the underlying diagnostic — audit for the specific structured-data fields an agent needs to transact, not just the schema an agent needs to understand a page exists — is exactly the exercise worth running against the comparison network's own listings (mortgage products, insurance policies, legal-service packages) once Google or another agent platform publishes an equivalent schema spec for financial/services comparison content. Nothing to action today; a template for a future audit once that spec exists.

## Story 6: 2027 marketing budgets — CMOs told to fund functions, not bigger AI line items

**Source:** Search Engine Journal (Greg Jarboe, citing Gartner CMO Spend Survey and Duke Fuqua CMO Survey) | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/2027-marketing-budgets-why-new-categories-beat-bigger-ai-line-items/582688/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Priorities]]

Gartner's CMO Spend Survey has awareness-and-conversion spend at 62.6% of media budgets, up 10 points since 2024, while loyalty and retention spend has fallen 29% to under 15% and labour costs have climbed from 21.9% to 24.5% of budgets. CMOs report allocating 15.3% of budget to AI initiatives but only 30% say they're actually ready to scale it. Duke's Fuqua survey (35th edition, January 2026) has GEO already in active use at four in ten companies, yet no marketing tech category scores above 5 on a 7-point performance scale — spend is running ahead of proven return. Separately, only 28% of Americans say they trust AI search results at all.

The article's actual argument is that the standard budget line-item structure (SEO, paid media, social, each with a growing "AI" sub-line) is the wrong unit of account. It proposes five functional categories instead: AI visibility/citation management, trust verification (structuring brand data so AI models can verify it), distribution engineering (pushing content to owned, earned and AI-crawled surfaces at once), human editorial oversight, and a measurement rebuild around citation-based metrics rather than last-click attribution.

This is a genuinely useful frame for the client-build channel rather than the owned portfolio: KlientFlo-type engagements increasingly need to be sold and scoped against "AI visibility," "trust verification" and "distribution engineering" as line items a client's finance function will recognise, not as an add-on percentage bolted onto a traditional SEO retainer. Worth stealing the five-category language directly for proposal templates — it's the kind of structure a non-technical buyer's CFO will nod along to, which is exactly the audience for a repeatable paid-build offer.
