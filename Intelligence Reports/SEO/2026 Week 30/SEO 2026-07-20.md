## Story 1: Google warns "are you a bot" verification screens can get indexed instead of your real content — and knock your page out of the index

**Source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/are-you-a-bot-screens-can-get-your-pages-dropped-by-google/582801/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

Google's John Mueller explained on a recent episode of Search Off the Record that bot-protection verification screens — the interstitial "checking your browser" or "are you a bot" pages served by CDNs, hosting security layers, or bot-mitigation services — can themselves get crawled and indexed by Google as if they were the page's actual content. Worse: when Google encounters the same generic verification screen across multiple sites using the same security vendor, it can pick a different site's version of that screen as the canonical page, and mark your real content as a duplicate of someone else's bot-check page. The failure mode is close to invisible during a normal audit, because regular human visitors never see the verification screen at all — only Googlebot does, if it gets flagged as suspicious by the security layer. Mueller's guidance: check Search Console's Page Indexing report and the URL Inspection tool for signs another site's URL has been selected as canonical over yours, contact the CDN/hosting/security provider if that's happening, and use Search Console's "Validate Fix" once corrected (a feature Search Engine Journal separately covered in detail two days ago, on 18 July).

This is genuinely useful, actionable technical SEO — worth a direct check across the exposed programmatic SEO portfolio (pet transport, funeral repatriation, close protection, bus hire, pest control) and the comparison network, especially any site sitting behind Cloudflare or a similar CDN with bot-fight or under-attack modes enabled. It's a low-probability, high-severity failure mode: unlikely to be actively happening on any given site, but cheap to rule out and expensive to leave unchecked, since the standard symptom (rankings quietly disappearing with no obvious cause) is exactly the kind of thing that could sit unnoticed for weeks. Worth a five-minute Search Console spot-check on each portfolio property this week rather than treating it as a someday item.
