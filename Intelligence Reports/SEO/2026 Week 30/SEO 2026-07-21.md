## Story 1: EU orders Google to share anonymised search data with rivals and open Android to competing AI assistants

**Source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-must-share-anonymized-search-data-with-rivals/582922/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

The European Commission has adopted two binding decisions under the Digital Markets Act. First, Google must give eligible rival search engines and AI chatbots access to an anonymised dataset covering search queries, clicks, views, result positions and metadata (language, device type, viewed URLs) — though notably not Google's actual ranking algorithms, and with account-level details and timestamps suppressed for privacy. Second, Google must open Android features to competing AI assistants, letting them handle voice activation and in-app functionality on the same footing Gemini currently enjoys.

The Commission was blunt about why: "Google's current data-sharing approach has failed." The timeline is slow by design — Google develops the dataset and proposes pricing by January 2027, most Android AI-assistant features must be opened by August 2027, concurrent voice activation by August 2028, with the Commission reviewing the whole arrangement every two years. The scale context matters too: all AI platforms combined reportedly represented only about 0.24% of global internet traffic as of January, so this is a bet on enabling future competition rather than a response to Google having already lost meaningful share.

For the portfolio and comparison network, this is multi-year structural background rather than a this-week story — but it's worth flagging as the clearest sign yet that regulators intend to make Google's search-interaction data a shared resource rather than a moat, which over time could mean more credible AI-search alternatives capable of citing sources and sending referral traffic. Worth a mental bookmark for 2027 rather than action now.

## Story 2: AI answers about business locations are wrong more often than most site owners assume

**Source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/ai-answers-about-your-locations-are-often-wrong-check-before-customers-do/582205/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

New testing from Searchable found that 93% of London businesses checked had at least one basic fact wrong or missing in AI-generated answers about them, with UK high-street retailers showing errors in roughly one in 16 answers and wrong postcodes appearing in one in 10 answers despite location-specific prompts — smaller businesses had meaningfully higher error rates than larger ones. Accuracy varies by platform: Perplexity showed a 10% inaccuracy rate versus 5% for Gemini and 4% for ChatGPT on the same test set. The adoption context makes this matter more than it would have a year ago — BrightLocal found 45% of consumers now use ChatGPT or similar tools for local business recommendations, up from just 6% the year before.

The mechanism is the real issue, not the specific numbers: unlike a traditional search results page offering several sources to cross-check, an AI answer collapses everything into one confidently-worded statement, and customers increasingly treat that statement as fact rather than a starting point. Searchable's recommended approach is straightforward — build a standing list of questions about hours, services and location, test them periodically across Google AI Overviews, AI Mode, ChatGPT, Gemini and Perplexity, fix the underlying source data (Google Business Profile, website, location pages) where it's inconsistent, and don't assume a source fix propagates to AI answers immediately.

This is a direct, cheap action item for every portfolio vertical that depends on local trust signals — pet transport, funeral repatriation, close protection, bus hire, pest control all have real addresses, service areas and hours that AI answer engines can get wrong in ways a human customer won't catch until they show up at the wrong time or place. Worth running Searchable's basic test — five or six questions per site, across the major AI answer engines — this week rather than treating it as a someday item, especially given how much more exposed smaller operators are according to today's data.

## Story 3: Google publicly downplays its own Search Console error reports — "many aren't real problems"

**Source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-downplays-search-console-error-reports-says-many-arent-real-problems/582890/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

John Mueller and Martin Splitt both pushed back this week on the instinct to treat every Search Console report as an actionable error. Pages showing as "not indexed" are frequently normal and expected rather than a sign of a problem — Mueller specifically flagged the reflex to read "hundreds of pages not indexed" as "clearly an error on my side." 404s are often the correct, intended outcome rather than a fault, and non-indexed pages during a redirect-heavy migration usually reflect expected behaviour rather than failure. Google acknowledged its own UI design contributes to the confusion — the "mark as fixed" workflow and blanket "error" labelling both nudge site owners toward treating every report as something to remediate, when Search Console is better used to spot unusual patterns and deviations from a site's normal baseline.

This lands directly next to Monday's report on bot-verification screens getting indexed in place of real content (still worth checking across the portfolio if that spot-check hasn't happened yet) — both stories are really about the same underlying discipline: read Search Console for genuine deviation from baseline, not as a literal checklist, but don't use that as an excuse to skip the specific, well-evidenced failure modes (like canonical-selection errors from bot-check pages) that really do need a fix. Worth keeping both stories in mind together rather than either one in isolation — "don't panic about every red flag" and "some red flags are real" are not in tension, they're the same skill.
