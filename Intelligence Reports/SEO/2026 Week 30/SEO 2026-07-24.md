## Story 1: EU fines Google €890 million under the DMA for favouring its own comparison products over rivals in Search

**Source:** Search Engine Land / European Commission (via web search) | https://searchengineland.com/google-fined-e460-million-over-self-preferencing-search-and-e430-over-google-play-483302
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[SEO Portfolio]]

The European Commission issued its first-ever fine under the Digital Markets Act on 23 July, splitting an €890 million ($1.02bn) penalty into two decisions: €460 million for self-preferencing in Google Search, and €430 million for restricting app developers on Google Play. The Search decision is the one that matters here — regulators found Google gives prominent placement to its own Hotels, Flights, Shopping and Maps results, complete with star ratings, enhanced visuals and direct booking interfaces, while comparable third-party comparison and booking services get no equivalent treatment. Under the DMA, gatekeepers are barred from ranking their own services more favourably than rivals offering the same function — and this is now the largest fine any company has drawn under that law.

This is not abstract for Orwell: it's a regulator formally confirming, with a nine-figure penalty attached, that Google systematically privileges its own comparison-style products over independent ones — precisely the competitive disadvantage the entire [[Comparison Network]] operates against every day in mortgages, money transfer and insurance search. It doesn't change anything about how MortgageCompare or InsureCompare rank tomorrow — the DMA applies to the EU, not the UAE, and Google has said it will appeal — but it's a genuinely useful data point on file: an EU regulator has now built a formal evidentiary record of exactly how Google self-preferences against comparison-site competitors, which is worth revisiting if the UAE's own DMA-equivalent regulatory conversations ever mature, or simply as ammunition the next time the "is this worth fighting on SEO grounds alone" question comes up for a comparison vertical.

## Story 2: ChatGPT gains direct access to Yelp reviews, ratings and photos

**Source:** Search Engine Land | https://searchengineland.com/openai-yelp-deal-483326
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]]

OpenAI and Yelp announced a data partnership on 23 July giving ChatGPT direct access to Yelp's review corpus, star ratings and photos for local-business queries answered inside ChatGPT rather than via a web search. This is a concrete, dated example of the GEO shift this report has flagged repeatedly in the abstract: AI answer engines are now striking direct data deals with review platforms rather than relying purely on crawled web content, which changes where local-business visibility actually gets won.

Directly relevant to every location-service vertical in the portfolio — pet transport, funeral repatriation, close protection, bus hire, pest control all depend on local trust signals, and if ChatGPT increasingly answers "best pest control near me"-style queries from Yelp data rather than organic search results, that's a distribution channel worth understanding rather than ignoring. Worth watching whether Yelp coverage exists (or is worth building) for the portfolio's own service areas, and whether similar deals appear with Google's own AI Overviews or other review platforms the portfolio already has presence on.

## Story 3: Google says its new "AI Max" campaign type is unlocking billions of new monetizable searches

**Source:** Search Engine Land | https://searchengineland.com/google-ai-max-billions-new-monetizable-searches-483347
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Google published data on 23 July claiming its AI Max campaign format — which layers AI-driven query expansion and creative optimisation on top of standard Search campaigns — is surfacing billions of previously unmonetized search queries. This lands alongside two related pieces from the same day: AI Max has been spotted expanding into Standard Shopping campaigns, and Search Engine Land published a migration guide for advertisers as Google's older Dynamic Search Ads format heads toward sunset in favour of AI Max.

Relevant primarily to any paid-search spend across the [[Comparison Network]] or portfolio sites — if AI Max genuinely surfaces new query volume rather than just reshuffling existing budget, it's worth a look at whether current campaigns are opted in, particularly ahead of DSA's formal sunset. No urgent action, but worth flagging to whoever currently manages paid search for the comparison sites.
