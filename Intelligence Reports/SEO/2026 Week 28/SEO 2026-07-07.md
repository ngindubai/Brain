## Story 1: Study of 6.77 million sessions confirms ChatGPT's near-total dominance of AI referral traffic — and exposes a structural blind spot

**Source:** Search Engine Land, "ChatGPT commands 92% of AI referral traffic" (6 July) | https://searchengineland.com/chatgpt-ai-referral-traffic-sessions-data-481630
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

New research analysing 6.77 million LLM-driven sessions across 166 GA4 properties from November 2024 to May 2026 puts real numbers behind what's been directional context in prior reports. ChatGPT holds 92.4% of trackable LLM referral traffic and has grown 12.8x over the study period with no sign of slowing. Behind it, the field is reshuffling: Claude grew 64x and overtook Perplexity in March 2026, while Perplexity has dropped 61% from its March 2025 peak and Copilot has collapsed 96%. Gemini sits a steady, unspectacular second with 3.2x growth.

The more useful finding for anyone running content sites is structural rather than a leaderboard: ChatGPT sends 28.8% of its referred traffic to internal search-results pages rather than to a specific landing page — it trusts a domain enough to send a user there, but frequently can't identify the right specific page to land them on. That's a concrete, actionable gap: it means a site's internal search experience is now functioning as an AI-traffic landing page whether or not it was designed for that, and that pricing pages, category pages and other structured content need to be legible enough for a model to route to directly rather than defaulting to internal search. Publishers specifically are the worst-served group in this data, capturing only 0.11% penetration despite producing plenty of citable content — worth keeping in mind for anything in the portfolio that leans on being cited rather than being the transactional destination itself. For sites that get AI referral traffic at all (SEO Portfolio, Comparison Network), the immediate takeaway is to check whether internal search on the highest-traffic properties is actually usable as a landing experience, since it may now be doing more work than its design assumes.

## Story 2: Ahrefs' own self-promotion experiment shows LLMs will cite a brand without recommending it

**Source:** Ahrefs Blog, "Self-Promotional Content Works—Until It Backfires (AI SEO Experiment)" (6 July) | https://ahrefs.com/blog/self-promotional-content-ai-seo-experiment/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

Ahrefs published 34 self-promotional pages across multiple domains, pushing two of its own products (a new conference, Ahrefs Evolve, and an existing tool, Ahrefs Brand Radar), then tracked citation and recommendation behaviour across nearly 10,000 AI responses from February to May. The results split cleanly by brand maturity. For the genuinely new product, the tactic worked: in 72 previously-empty recommendation slots, 82% of new mentions came from the promotional pages themselves — the content filled a real gap in what the models knew. For the already-well-documented existing tool, it barely moved anything: only 6% of new mentions traced back to Ahrefs' own pages, with 94% coming from third-party sources the models already trusted more.

The sharper finding is the backfire case: among answers that cited one of the conference-promoting pages, 43% never mentioned the conference at all — the model used the page as a source for the format (a "best of" listicle) but recommended a competitor also named on the same page. Citations were also transient, appearing on only about a third of eligible days even when they landed. The practical read for the portfolio and comparison-network sites: self-published "best X" or roundup-style content only earns durable AI recommendation when it's filling an actual awareness gap for a genuinely under-documented product or service — for anything already well covered elsewhere (which describes most of the comparison-network's categories), the same tactic risks amplifying competitors who happen to sit in the same list rather than the page's own subject. Worth factoring into any AI-visibility content plan before assuming more roundup content is automatically good defence.

## Story 3: Google Business Profile reviews issue still unresolved, John Mueller dismisses Content Signals robots.txt directive as pointless

**Source:** Search Engine Land / Search Engine Roundtable coverage (via web search), original GBP story 3 July, previously logged | https://searchengineland.com/google-is-investigating-reports-of-reviews-going-missing-and-pausing-reviews-on-local-listings-481616
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

No resolution on the Google Business Profile reviews issue — fourth consecutive report carrying it, still no timeline from Google. The one genuinely new item from today's sweep: Google's John Mueller stated publicly that the "Content Signals" robots.txt directive — the one some sites have adopted to try to signal AI-training/crawling preferences — "has no effects whatsoever for any crawler or LLM" and "just adds bloat and future maintenance" to a robots.txt file. That's a direct, on-record Google statement that a mechanism some publishers have been treating as a meaningful AI-opt-out lever does nothing. Anything in the portfolio that's adopted Content Signals directives on the assumption they carry weight should be treated as cosmetic rather than functional; it's not harmful to leave in place, but it's not doing the job some sites think it is.

Separately and unrelated: Microsoft/Bing is testing richer product-detail overlays in Shopping results (images, price history, related products), and Google/YouTube's Shopify-to-Merchant-Center sync app is being deprecated from 18 August, requiring product IDs to be rewritten on reinstall — relevant only to anything in the portfolio running Shopify-to-Google Shopping sync, none of which is flagged as currently in use.

## What this means for Orwell

Two things today are worth more than a passing read. First, the AI-referral traffic study is the clearest evidence yet that "being cited" and "being the destination" are drifting apart — nearly a third of ChatGPT's referred traffic dead-ends on internal search rather than a specific page, which means the portfolio's highest-AI-referral properties should be checked for whether their internal search is actually a usable landing experience rather than an afterthought. Second, Ahrefs' own experiment is a useful gut-check against assuming more "best X" roundup content is automatically good AI-visibility defence for the comparison network — it only reliably works when filling a genuine awareness gap, and most of what the comparison sites compete in is already well covered elsewhere, which is exactly the case where the experiment showed the tactic backfiring. The Semrush decision remains the one standing item that's overdue rather than merely open — six days of the same error is no longer worth logging passively.
