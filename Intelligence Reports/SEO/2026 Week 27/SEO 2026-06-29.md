## Story 2: Google officially endorses GEO as "still SEO"; AI Overviews in 5-15% of searches; what actually matters

**Source:** Google Search Central (via DigitalApplied) | https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/google-official-seo-docs-generative-ai-optimization-june-2026
**Additional source:** Search Engine Journal | https://www.searchenginejournal.com/googles-new-ai-search-guide-calls-aeo-and-geo-still-seo/575026/
**Additional source:** Marie Haynes | https://www.mariehaynes.com/okf/
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]], [[Comparison Network]]

On June 5, 2026, Google updated its Search Central documentation to formally name GEO and AEO (Answer Engine Optimisation) as legitimate SEO practices. The key statement: "optimizing for generative AI search is optimizing for the search experience, and thus still SEO." This resolves the practitioner debate about whether to treat Google's AI features as a separate optimisation surface.

What Google says NOT to do (explicitly listed in the documentation):

- `llms.txt` files: Google's systems do not read these for Generative AI ranking.
- Content chunking for AI: Google does not require or use AI-specific chunking.
- AI-specific schema markup: No separate schema is needed beyond standard structured data.
- Inauthentic brand mentions on low-quality domains: Specifically called out as a black-hat GEO tactic.

What Google says DOES matter:

- Ranking organically in the top results. Studies show nearly all pages cited in AI Overviews come from top organic positions. You must rank first to be cited.
- Entity authority and structured data (existing best practices).
- Content that directly and completely answers the query — the same brief-answer optimisation that has driven featured snippet capture.

AI Overviews are currently visible in 5-15% of searches. That is a significant slice for informational and navigational queries, which is exactly where the SEO portfolio's informational content lives. The comparison network's "what is a UAE mortgage" type content is in direct competition for AI Overview citations.

Marie Haynes is worth reading closely this week. She has published two important posts:

1. **Google's Open Knowledge Format (OKF)** (https://www.mariehaynes.com/okf/): Google is developing OKF as "a standardized way for agents to access knowledge via simple directories of Markdown files." This is directly relevant to [[Agent Org]] — if Google's own agents consume content in OKF format, structuring knowledge bases in OKF is a zero-cost way to be indexed by Google's AI search features.

2. **Build an OKF brain like mine** (https://www.mariehaynes.com/build-an-okf-brain-like-mine/): Haynes has built a personal knowledge structure using OKF, demonstrating the implementation pattern. The vault's Markdown-based structure is already close to OKF-compatible. Worth reviewing whether the SEO portfolio's topic cluster content could be published in an OKF-compatible format to maximise AI searchability.

**Implication for the programmatic SEO portfolio:** The GEO formalisation means the programmatic build strategy needs to incorporate AI Overview capture as a design goal, not an afterthought. Pages that answer the query directly and completely in the first 200 words — with structured data and high organic rankings — will be the ones cited in AI Overviews. Pages that exist purely for keyword coverage will not.

## Story 3: The paid brand mention problem in GEO; black-hat tactics emerging

**Source:** Search Engine Land | https://searchengineland.com/paid-brand-mention-geo-481092
**Related vault notes:** [[SEO Portfolio]]

Search Engine Land covered a growing problem: GEO vendors selling paid brand placements on low-quality domains and private blog networks (PBNs) to influence AI Overview citations. The tactic mirrors black-hat link building — buying mentions across a network of thin sites to increase the citation count for a brand in AI training data or AI Overview retrieval.

Google's official guidance specifically called this out as a violation. More practically, this tactic does not work on Google's current AI Overview system (which relies on organic ranking, not unstructured mentions). It may have marginal effect on ChatGPT/Perplexity/Claude citation patterns, where training data and web retrieval are less tightly coupled to organic rank.

For the SEO portfolio: this creates an arms race risk. If competitors use paid GEO mentions, the portfolio sites may temporarily see reduced AI Overview presence for shared queries — but not from a ranking penalty. The correct response is not to replicate the tactic but to ensure every portfolio page has the organic rank, entity coverage, and direct-answer structure to be the preferred citation when Google surfaces AI Overviews on relevant queries.
