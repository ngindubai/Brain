## Story 1: Cambridge study finds Boko Haram running dedicated AI units across every major chatbot
**Source:** The Decoder | https://the-decoder.com/terrorist-groups-are-using-every-major-ai-chatbot-for-attack-planning-and-weapons-development/
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]]

A study from the Cambridge Programme on AI Science & Policy, led by researcher Antonia Jülich, was published 11 July and is still the dominant safety story moving through the news cycle today. It is based on 57 interviews with 27 former members of Boko Haram and its ISWAP faction in northeast Nigeria. Both factions had established dedicated internal AI units. Fighters used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, Meta AI and DeepSeek to design and improve explosive devices, identify captured military hardware, troubleshoot weapons, get battlefield tactics advice and review past attacks. ISIS reportedly taught prompt engineering and jailbreak technique to affiliated groups from 2023 onward. One detail stands out: ISWAP used AI to help replicate a motorcycle jump stunt from a film so fighters could clear trenches during raids. Eighteen fighters died in the training.

The study's headline conclusion is blunt: voluntary self-regulation is not working. Safety filters across every major lab failed to reliably block this misuse over a sustained period, not a one-off jailbreak.

This is a genuine watershed for the "which lab is safer" argument, because the finding is that none of them are, consistently, against a motivated adversary running its own AI unit rather than a lone actor probing for a jailbreak. Expect this to feed directly into the five-lab jailbreak severity scoring standard due around 1 August (see open loops), and to strengthen the case for state and export-control style restrictions rather than lab self-policing. For anyone building agentic systems, the practical read is that content filtering at the prompt layer is not sufficient defence in depth; if a state-level terrorist group can sustain misuse across six frontier chatbots for years, a business relying on the same guardrails to keep its own agents "safe by default" is not as protected as it assumes.

## Story 2: China forces ByteDance and Alibaba to kill AI companion agent features ahead of 15 July law
**Source:** Quartz | https://qz.com/bytedance-alibaba-ai-companion-chatbot-china-rules-070626
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]], [[Agent Org]]

With China's Interim Measures for the Administration of AI Anthropomorphic Interaction Services taking effect 15 July, ByteDance and Alibaba have started shutting down affected features early rather than risk being caught out of compliance. Alibaba's Qwen stopped its humanlike, user-created agent personas on 10 July, with its wider agent services following five days later. ByteDance's Doubao is taking its agent function offline on the 15th itself, describing it publicly as a "product function adjustment." The rules, issued jointly by the Cyberspace Administration of China and four other agencies back in April, ban virtual companion or relative services for minors, require guardian consent and spending controls for under-14s, mandate addiction-detection mechanisms, and require algorithm filing and security assessment for any provider offering sustained emotional interaction.

This is the clearest sign yet that Beijing is willing to force product shutdowns rather than settle for fines, and it is happening to two of the most commercially important consumer AI products in China. The scope carve-out matters too: work assistants, customer service and education tools are explicitly exempt, so this is targeted at companion and roleplay products specifically, not agents generally. For the comparison network and any China-model-dependent tooling, the read is that Chinese regulators are moving faster and harder on consumer-facing emotional AI than either the US or EU, while leaving enterprise and productivity agents largely untouched. That is the opposite of the Western regulatory instinct, which tends to worry about agentic autonomy in business contexts first.

## Story 3: GPT-5.6 Sol takes the top spot on Artificial Analysis's Intelligence Index
**Source:** Artificial Analysis | https://artificialanalysis.ai/articles/openai-gpt5-5-is-the-new-leading-AI-model
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]], [[Products]]

Artificial Analysis's independent benchmark data, verified 11 July and still the current state of the leaderboard today, has OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol leading its Intelligence Index at 58.9%, ahead of Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8 at 55.7% and OpenAI's own GPT-5.6 Terra at 55.0%. Artificial Analysis now tracks over 400 models and 50-plus inference providers, and has expanded well beyond text into image, video, speech, music, hardware and agent benchmarking. Its newest addition, AutomationBench-AA, launched 6 July as an independent leaderboard built on Zapier's AutomationBench: 657 tasks across finance, HR, marketing, operations, sales and support, run across 40 simulated app environments including Gmail, Sheets, Slack, Salesforce, Zendesk, Jira and HubSpot, testing whether agents can actually execute real SaaS workflows under business rules rather than just answer questions about them.

The gap between GPT-5.6 Sol and Claude Opus 4.8 is narrow (3.2 points) and well within the range that shifts with each release cycle, so this is not a durable OpenAI lead so much as evidence the frontier is now genuinely contested at the top rather than Anthropic- or OpenAI-dominated. The more interesting signal is AutomationBench-AA itself: benchmark providers are visibly pivoting from "can it reason" to "can it actually do the job inside real business software," which is the exact question that determines whether an agent is deployable in a client build rather than just impressive in a demo.

## Story 5: US data-centre buildout keeps outrunning the power grid
**Source:** Washington Times | https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/jul/11/renewable-energy-allies-fighting-cleaner-alternatives-gas-plants-rise/ ; Help Net Security | https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/07/07/ai-data-centers-demand-expansion/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Agent Org]]

The power-constraint story kept developing over the weekend rather than breaking fresh today, but it's worth a proper look because the numbers have got starker. At least 75 US data-centre projects worth a combined $130 billion have been postponed or cancelled, not for lack of funding or chips but for lack of grid power. PJM Interconnection, the largest US grid operator covering 65 million people across 13 states, is projecting it will be six gigawatts short of its own reliability requirement by 2027, and has asked the Department of Energy to let it order data centres onto backup generators within 15 minutes of an emergency signal. FERC has issued show-cause orders to the six largest grid operators demanding they defend or rewrite interconnection rules for gigawatt-scale loads. Virginia's new data-centre electricity consumption tax, $0.011 per kWh, took effect 1 July. Gas plant construction is accelerating to fill the gap faster than renewables can be brought online, which is now an active political fight, per the Washington Times piece published yesterday.

This is the structural constraint underneath every other AI infrastructure headline this year, including the Meta, Anthropic and hyperscaler capex numbers covered in market signals below. It is also the best argument for the GPU compute venture idea sitting in [[Ventures]]: if grid interconnection, not chip supply, is now the binding constraint on US AI capacity, the value is shifting toward whoever controls power and siting, not just whoever controls GPUs.

None of this is single-day news, but the trend line is the story: developer attention has fully shifted from "should I use agents" to "how do I run more of them, faster, with less babysitting." Agent infrastructure, orchestration, and eval tooling are now treated as first-class dev-stack layers alongside CI/CD, not experimental add-ons.
