## Story 1: Anthropic reshapes Claude Fable 5 access across every subscription tier, effective today

**Source:** The Decoder | https://the-decoder.com/anthropic-slashes-claude-fable-5-limits-in-max-and-team-premium-and-pushes-pro-users-toward-api-pricing/ (cross-checked against Anthropic's own @claudeai statement)
**Related vault notes:** [[Products]], [[Agent Org]]

Beginning today, Claude Fable 5 becomes a permanent (rather than promotional) feature of Max and Team Premium plans — but at 50% of usage limits, and those baseline limits are themselves being cut by 33% today as the temporary "bonus usage" phase that's been running since Fable 5 launched comes to an end. In practice that's two cuts stacking on the same day: a smaller pool overall, and Fable 5 capped at half of that smaller pool. Pro and Team Standard users lose default subscription access to Fable 5 entirely; they get a one-time $100 usage credit and then pay standard API rates for anything beyond it.

Anthropic's own framing is that Fable 5 demand has been "hard to manage and frustrating for users" and that the company is still investing in capacity — a polite way of saying the model has been more popular than the infrastructure can currently serve profitably at flat subscription pricing. The competitive read-through matters too: OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol is being pitched at roughly a third of the cost for comparable performance, and the wave of cheap, capable Chinese open-weight models (Kimi K3 chief among them, still fresh from its 16 July debut) is compressing the price Anthropic can defend at the frontier. Reversing an earlier plan to strip Fable 5 out of subscriptions altogether, in favour of this capped-but-included compromise, reads as a concession to that pressure rather than a confident move.

For Orwell specifically, this is the one story today worth acting on rather than just noting. Claude Code on Max 20x is the primary build tool — a 33% cut to baseline limits plus a 50% cap on Fable specifically is a real reduction in available capacity for whichever workloads have been running on Fable 5, not a rounding error. Worth a deliberate check today or tomorrow on which routines and sessions actually depend on Fable 5 versus Sonnet 5, and whether the reduced ceiling changes how work should be sequenced across the day (this Monday-morning intelligence brief run being one visible example of exactly that kind of usage). No need to panic — the $100 API credit and the fact that Fable remains included at all suggests Anthropic is managing this as a capacity problem, not a wind-down — but it's the first concrete cost-of-doing-business change to the toolchain since the Fable rollout began, and it's worth watching whether the 50%/33% split holds or gets adjusted again once Anthropic's IPO-prep period (see the ongoing investor-meeting reporting) puts more scrutiny on unit economics.

## Story 3: Agent safety and governance research cluster continues — three more papers on the "should this be automated" question

**Source:** arXiv cs.AI listing (direct fetch) | arXiv:2607.15944, arXiv:2607.16112, arXiv:2607.15992
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]]

Today's arXiv sweep adds to the same theme that's been building across the last several reports (AgentWorm and Bad Memory on Saturday, the broader agent-liability cluster before that), though with a different emphasis: less about attack surface, more about where automation should stop. "When Not to Automate: Human Preservation Protocol" (2607.15944) proposes formal protocols for balancing AI automation against preserving human roles inside organisations — a direct, if academic, engagement with the exact question Orwell's whole thesis rests on (which roles get replaced and which don't). "Harmonizing AI Safety Thresholds" (2607.16112) argues for consistency across the different safety-threshold standards currently being set piecemeal by different labs and regulators, and "Closing the AI Trust Gap: Independent Certification" (2607.15992) proposes third-party certification frameworks for trustworthy AI deployment — the research-side counterpart to the FTC's AI-accuracy comment period, which stays open until 31 July.

None of these are product news and none require a decision this week, but "Human Preservation Protocol" in particular is worth a flag for [[Agent Org]] thinking: it's a sign the academic literature is starting to formalise the exact automate/preserve boundary question that Orwell answers empirically, one department at a time, rather than theoretically. Worth a skim if the framing turns out to be useful for how Orwell documents its own automation decisions.
