## Story 1: Alibaba reportedly bans employees from using Claude Code, points staff to its own Qoder tool

**Source:** TechCrunch, "Alibaba reportedly bans employees from using Claude Code" (4 July) | https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/04/alibaba-reportedly-bans-employees-from-using-claude-code/
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]]

Alibaba has classified Claude Code as high-risk software and is banning staff from using it, effective 10 July 2026, per Reuters and Morningstar reporting picked up by TechCrunch. Employees are being redirected to Qoder, Alibaba's own coding assistant. The stated reason is a security concern: some versions of Claude Code allegedly contained functionality that could identify Chinese users. Anthropic's Thariq Shihipar pushed back on the framing, calling it "an experiment we launched in March that was meant to prevent account abuse" that the company had "been meaning to take down for a while" — i.e., an anti-abuse fingerprinting check, not a surveillance feature aimed at China specifically.

Context that matters here: Anthropic already prohibits Chinese companies, and foreign entities owned by them, from using its models under its usage policies, and has reportedly been closing access loopholes (per FT reporting referenced in the piece). Alibaba's ban reads at least partly as a geopolitical hedge — a Chinese tech giant pre-empting reliance on a US frontier-lab tool it's not supposed to have full access to anyway — layered on top of a genuine, if overstated, security concern.

Signal for Orwell: none directly — this is an enterprise-security and geopolitical story about a Chinese company's internal tooling policy, not a change to Claude Code itself, its terms, or its availability to Orwell. Filed as background because it's a concrete example of how fragile the "which AI tools can which companies legally/safely use" question is becoming globally; worth remembering if any future Orwell client work ever intersects with China-linked entities or supply chains.

## Story 2: Midjourney pushes Hollywood studios to disclose their own AI usage in ongoing IP dispute

**Source:** TechCrunch, "Midjourney wants Hollywood studios to reveal the details of their AI usage" (4 July) | https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/04/midjourney-wants-hollywood-studios-to-reveal-the-details-of-their-ai-usage/

In the ongoing legal fight between Midjourney and major Hollywood studios (who have sued the image/video generator over alleged training on copyrighted characters and footage), Midjourney is now turning the disclosure question back on the studios — seeking details of the studios' own internal AI tool usage. The move looks like a discovery/leverage tactic rather than a substantive defence: if studios are themselves using generative AI internally (for VFX, pre-production, marketing), it complicates their position as plaintiffs arguing AI training on copyrighted material is uniquely harmful.

Why it matters beyond the courtroom: this is the same fight playing out across the industry — copyright holders suing AI companies over training data, while AI companies increasingly argue "you use this stuff too." No ruling yet; this is a filing, not a decision. Nothing actionable for Orwell today, but the outcome of the broader class of AI-training-copyright cases (this one, the various music-label suits, book-author suits) is a standing watch item for anyone building products on generative media, which the [[Products]] and content-generation side of the portfolio touches indirectly.
