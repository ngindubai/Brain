## Hormuz: Iran hardens transit-fee position, talks paused for Khamenei's funeral (open-loop update, developments dated 4 July)

This is not a new story, it is a material update to the existing Hormuz open loop that had not been captured as of yesterday's report. On 4 July, Iran's ambassador to China said Tehran will "definitely" charge fees for ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz once the current free-transit window lapses, an idea Washington has already rejected, while adding that "friendly" nations may get preferential treatment. Separately, a senior Iranian official issued a pointed warning to the UK and France that the strait "is not a theatre for the military display of extra-regional powers," a direct signal against any European naval posture near the waterway. Diplomatic talks in Doha, which had reportedly made "positive progress" over two days, were then paused as the Iranian delegation returned home for the multi-day state funeral of former Supreme Leader Khamenei; the next round is scheduled "at the earliest possible time" after that concludes.

Context for why this matters: the US-Iran ceasefire framework that ended the February air war included a commitment that commercial shipping would transit Hormuz free of charge for 60 days. That window is now approaching its end, and Iran signalling it intends to monetise the strait rather than simply reopen it unconditionally is a harder line than the "cautious optimism" tone that had Brent settling near $72.30 on 4 July. A toll regime, even a modest one, keeps a geopolitical risk premium baked into freight and insurance costs through the Gulf indefinitely rather than letting it decay as the ceasefire ages. The funeral-driven pause is procedural and short-lived in itself, but it means the fee question goes unresolved for at least several more days, and the timing of "special treatment" for friendly nations is the kind of detail that could quietly reshape which flags and buyers get preferential Gulf routing. Nothing here should move Monday's oil open dramatically on its own, but it is the reason Brent has not fully round-tripped back to pre-conflict pricing despite two months of talks. Continues as an open loop.

## GPU compute: Nvidia's vendor-financing model for AI clouds keeps expanding (developments dated 4 July, carried from Friday)

Nvidia's newly launched revenue-sharing programme for AI cloud operators, under which Nvidia backstops the GPU buildout of smaller compute providers in exchange for a recurring cut of the revenue those chips eventually generate, picked up further coverage on 4 July as terms of the Sharon AI and Firmus commitments became clearer: roughly 210,000 Grace Blackwell GPUs are being deployed under the scheme. Sharon AI, which listed on Nasdaq in February via a $125 million IPO and then raised a further $1.6 billion in a June private placement, is one of the first operators using the model to fund an "AI factory" build.

This is squarely on the GPU compute venture thesis. The structure Nvidia is running here, financing the hardware itself and taking a revenue share rather than requiring the smaller operator to raise the full capital stack up front, is effectively a template for how a challenger compute provider gets into the market without needing hyperscaler-scale balance sheets. It also cuts the other way as a risk signal: analysts are already framing this as "vendor finance," meaning Nvidia is underwriting demand for its own chips by taking on the credit risk of the buyers, which is the exact dynamic that made 1990s telecom vendor financing a red flag once demand growth slowed. For a compute venture built around leasing or reselling access to GPU capacity, the read-through is twofold: this kind of financing lowers the barrier to standing up a mid-size AI cloud operation right now, but it is also a sign that the largest supplier in the market is having to manufacture its own demand at the margin, which is worth tracking rather than dismissing.
