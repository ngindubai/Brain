## Story 1: Oil Slides Despite Renewed Hormuz Attacks — the Corridor Is Holding the Market's Confidence

**Source:** HDFC Sky | https://hdfcsky.com/news/brent-crude-oil-price-today-july-1-2026-oil-prices-rise-as-iran-rejects-face-to-face-talks-with-u-s-in-fresh-middle-east-setback | Fox News | https://www.foxnews.com/politics/iran-fights-keep-grip-hormuz-us-gulf-allies-carve-new-shipping-route
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Comparison Network]]

Brent crude opened at $73.61 and traded around $73.2/barrel today, up 0.3% on the day but down sharply from Monday's $80.57 close — a fall that happened despite Iran and the US exchanging strikes over the weekend of June 27-29 (full sequence in the Geopolitics report). WTI sat at $69.7.

The explanation, per retired US Navy officials cited by Fox News: nearly half of inbound Gulf commercial traffic now runs through the new US-Oman southern shipping corridor along Oman's coastline, a route Iran cannot toll or meaningfully threaten with the same leverage it has over the northern route past its own coastline. Iran's attack on a tanker in that southern corridor last week was explicitly aimed at undermining confidence in it — "they felt it necessary to attack it," per Rear Admiral Mark Montgomery (ret.). The market's reaction — oil falling, not spiking — suggests traders currently believe the corridor plus continued supertanker throughput can absorb intermittent Iranian attacks without materially reducing supply.

**Orwell/Ventures implication:** This is the crossover point between the Geopolitics and Markets reports. If the southern corridor gets hit hard enough to force insurers to reprice again, expect oil to snap back over $100 within days and CBUAE to come under fresh pressure, dragging the peg-constrained UAE rate environment with it. Today's price action says the market isn't there yet — but the confidence is fragile and untested against a direct, sustained campaign on the Oman route itself.

**Source:** Web search aggregating Yahoo Finance/CNBC data | https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/NVDA/ | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/04/chipmaker-equities-micron-marvell-broadcom-intel.html

Micron's fiscal Q2 2026 (reported June 24, confirmed again in today's search) showed revenue up 196% year-over-year to $23.86B, with non-GAAP EPS up 682% to $12.20 — figures that update the "record quarter" framing from Monday's report with harder numbers. The June 5 selloff (Broadcom miss, $1.3T sector value wiped in a day, covered in Monday's report) is now visibly a blip on the YTD chart rather than a trend reversal. Nvidia in particular has only clawed back to +15.2% YTD, the laggard of the four — its June 5 drop was proportionally the smallest but its recovery has also been the slowest, worth watching given it's the bellwether.

**Ventures implication:** The YTD numbers confirm the compute/memory supercycle thesis is intact through H1 2026. The lesson from Monday's report — that GPU compute investments need to survive single-day sentiment swings on a bellwether miss — still holds; today's data just shows the underlying trend absorbing that shock rather than being derailed by it.

## Story 5: Qualcomm-Modular $4B Deal — Inference Software Is the New Battleground

**Source:** Crunchbase News | https://news.crunchbase.com/feed/ (GV partner Dave Munichiello interview)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Follow-up detail on Monday's reported Qualcomm acquisition of Modular (inference software) for $4B: a GV partner interview published today notes the deal reflects a structural shift toward "disaggregated inference architecture" — meaning inference now increasingly requires a software abstraction layer that runs across multiple chip vendors (Nvidia, AMD, custom silicon, Qualcomm's own), rather than chip-specific stacks. This reinforces the pattern flagged Monday: inference infrastructure (Baseten, Groq, Modular) is the hottest venture sub-category right now, ahead of the now-consolidated training layer.

**Ventures implication:** Any GPU compute venture should track the inference software layer, not just raw hardware — vendor lock-in risk is shifting up the stack.
