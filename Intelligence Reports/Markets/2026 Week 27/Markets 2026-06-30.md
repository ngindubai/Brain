## Story 1: SpaceX IPO — $2.1T Debut, Musk Becomes First Trillionaire

**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Products]]

SpaceX debuted on public markets June 12 at $135/share, raising $75 billion in the largest IPO in history. First-day close: $160.95 (+19%), market cap $2.1 trillion. Peak intraday June 16: $225.64. As of late June, trading around $153 — still above IPO price but 32% off peak, reflecting broader tech sentiment after the Broadcom shock.

Elon Musk retained 82%+ voting control. The IPO officially makes him the first trillionaire on record based on combined SpaceX, Tesla, and xAI holdings. SpaceX had previously completed the acquisition of xAI (Musk's AI venture) in February 2026, making AI a third revenue segment alongside launch services and Starlink.

Starlink: 10.3M+ active customers across 160 countries, 61% of total SpaceX revenue in 2025. The SpaceX acquisition of Cursor/Anysphere for $60B (separate story) closed around the same time — the AI coding platform is now a SpaceX subsidiary.

**Orwell/Ventures implication:** SpaceX's public float means Starlink's expansion plans are now subject to quarterly earnings scrutiny. Watch for Starlink pricing changes in key Orwell markets. More broadly: the IPO validates the thesis that AI-infrastructure companies (compute, connectivity, chip design) are the most valuable in the world right now.

## Story 2: Anthropic — $965B Valuation, IPO Filing, First-Ever Operating Profit

**Source:** Fortune | https://fortune.com/2026/06/01/anthropic-confidentially-files-ipo-965-billion-valuation/ | CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-open-ai-startup-value.html
**Related vault notes:** [[Agent Org]], [[Orwell Corp Stack Decisions]]

Anthropic completed a $65B Series H round in late May at a $965B valuation — eclipsing OpenAI's $852B to become the world's most valuable AI startup. The round was led by Altimeter Capital, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, and Sequoia. Anthropic has confidentially filed for a Nasdaq IPO, targeting an October 2026 debut that would potentially be the first $1 trillion company to list on public markets.

The financial story behind the valuation: $44B annualised run-rate revenue as of May 2026. Q2 2026 operating profit forecast at approximately $559M — the company's first-ever positive operating quarter. The path to profitability is being demonstrated ahead of the IPO, which is strategically correct: institutional investors need profit evidence before a $1T listing.

OpenAI's situation: $852B valuation from a $122B round closed in late March. OpenAI filed its confidential S-1 on June 8 — a week after Anthropic. However, Bloomberg reported on June 26 that OpenAI is now leaning toward a 2027 listing, citing market volatility and CEO Sam Altman's stated floor of a $1 trillion listing price. Altman apparently will not list below $1T, which means he needs either market recovery or more revenue growth.

Combined, SpaceX + OpenAI + Anthropic targeted valuations sum to approximately $3.6 trillion. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are advising on both OpenAI and Anthropic processes.

**Orwell/Agent Org implication:** Anthropic's IPO filing means the company is entering a period of heightened regulatory scrutiny and public-market pressure. This could affect product roadmap (prioritising profitable B2B over experimental consumer), pricing (potential increases to support margin), and the export control regime (public companies are more visible targets for government oversight). Monitor the October timeline carefully. API pricing changes before or around the IPO are plausible.

## Story 3: Broadcom Q2 Miss — The $1.3T Warning Shot

**Source:** Intellectia AI | https://intellectia.ai/blog/semiconductor-stocks-selloff-june-2026 | CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/02/broadcom-quarterly-earnings.html (web-searched)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Broadcom reported its fiscal Q2 2026 results on June 3. AI networking revenue came in at $4.1B against analyst expectations of $4.8B — a 14% miss. More damaging: CEO Hock Tan maintained rather than raised Broadcom's 2027 AI semiconductor outlook. The market had priced in an upward revision; holding guidance was interpreted as a warning.

The next trading day (June 5), the PHLX semiconductor index dropped 10% — the deepest single-day loss since March 2020. Approximate value destruction: $1.3 trillion. Individual moves: Nvidia -6% (~$740B market cap evaporated), AMD -10.86% to $466.38, Broadcom -12.6%.

The partial recovery came within 48 hours — Nvidia reclaimed roughly half its losses within two days. The speed of recovery confirms that AI infrastructure demand fundamentals are not in question; the market is simply extremely sensitive to any negative data point given the elevated multiples the sector trades at.

This matters for the [[Ventures]] GPU compute angle. The thesis — AI compute demand is real and growing — survived the June 5 shock. But the practical lesson is that GPU compute investments are denominated in a sector trading at 40-60x forward earnings, which means sentiment can swing $1T in a day. Any GPU compute venture needs to be structured to survive paper losses during sector drawdowns.

## Story 4: Micron — Biggest Quarter in History, HBM Fully Sold Out

**Source:** TechTimes | https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318825/20260622/micron-earnings-2026-record-margins-confirm-ai-memory-tax-your-next-pc.htm | TechCrunch | https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/28/why-wall-street-thinks-us-memory-maker-micron-is-the-next-nvidia/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Micron reported Q3 FY2026 earnings on June 24. Analysts forecast 998% earnings growth on ~$34.5B revenue. The actual result confirmed record margins at 81% gross — driven by HBM pricing power. Micron is the only US-headquartered HBM manufacturer, and its entire 2026 HBM capacity was sold out under binding contracts before the year began (HBM3E and HBM4 both fully allocated).

Every Nvidia AI GPU requires HBM placed physically adjacent to the compute die. Three companies produce essentially all HBM: Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron. Micron stock is up 70% in 2026, trading near its 52-week high of ~$546. Wall Street is explicitly comparing it to Nvidia's trajectory — a critical infrastructure supplier with duopoly-level pricing power.

Samsung and SK Hynix committed $550B+ to additional memory fabrication capacity (the "RAMageddon" investment), signalling that the supply crunch is expected to persist for multiple years. SK Hynix simultaneously filed for a $29B Nasdaq IPO — the largest foreign semiconductor listing in history — to fund its US expansion.

**Ventures GPU compute implication:** The memory constraint is as important as the compute constraint. GPU supply is expanding (Nvidia B200 ramp, custom silicon proliferation), but HBM supply is the binding constraint on inference throughput. Any GPU compute venture should account for HBM allocation — raw GPU count is misleading without memory bandwidth.

## Story 6: Chinese AI Stocks — Pentagon List, DoD Contracts, Retaliation

**Source:** Al Jazeera | https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/6/22/china-adds-10-us-firms-including-rare-earth-miner-to-export-control-list | CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/22/china-trade-curbs-us-companies-export-controls-procurement-exclusion-pentagon-list-
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The Pentagon added approximately 80 Chinese companies to its Chinese Military Companies list in June, including Alibaba Holdings, Baidu, and BYD. The designation bars the US Department of Defense from awarding direct contracts to listed companies starting June 30 (today), with indirect procurement restrictions following in 2027.

Both Alibaba and Baidu are suing the US over the designation, stating they are neither military companies nor MCIF (Military-Civil Integration Fusion) contributors. Alibaba stock was down 35% YTD as of late June. Baidu's AI chip unit Kunlunxin is pursuing a $50B Hong Kong IPO — a hedging move to reduce US market dependency.

China retaliated on June 22 by adding 10 US firms (including a rare-earth miner) to its export control list. China also imposed trade curbs on dozens of US firms in direct response to the Pentagon blacklist. ByteDance was separately reported in talks with Shanghai-based Iluvatar CoreX to purchase AI chips — a domestic chip sourcing move as US export controls tighten.

The US simultaneously clarified that its ban on AI chip shipments applies to Chinese firms operating outside China — closing the offshore entity loophole that had allowed some circumvention.

**Macro signal:** The bifurcation of the global AI infrastructure market into US-aligned and China-aligned supply chains is now structural, not cyclical. As of today (June 30), the DoD contract exclusion kicks in. This accelerates China's domestic semiconductor investment and creates a two-track AI development reality for the next decade.
