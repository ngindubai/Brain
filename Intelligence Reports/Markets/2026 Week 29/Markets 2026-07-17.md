## Editor's note

TSMC actually printed last night, and the market's reaction inverts the usual script: the beat was real (record fifth consecutive quarter of profit, revenue +36% YoY) but the guidance wasn't reassuring, it was alarming — capex raised from $52-56bn to $60-64bn for the year, plus a fresh $100bn US investment commitment, on top of an already-parabolic AI buildout. Read as "even more spending needed to keep up," not "demand is strong," it deepened rather than ended the chip selloff: Micron, AMD and Broadcom all down more than 5%, SoftBank off over 9% in Asia, SK Hynix's US-listed shares down another 13%. Layered on top: Alphabet sank 4% on a Bloomberg report that Gemini 3.5 Pro is months late, and Chinese AI stocks gave back some of this week's Apple-deal gains as the Wall Street rout crossed the Pacific. The options-market read is that this is a repricing of AI capex intensity, not a demand crack — genuinely useful for GPU compute venture timing, since it says the bottleneck is capital and supply, not appetite. Oil sits just off one-month highs as Hormuz tanker traffic collapses further; full military detail in today's [[Geopolitics]].

## Story 1: TSMC beats on every line — and the market sells off anyway, on the capex number

**Source:** [TradingKey](https://www.tradingkey.com/analysis/stocks/more/262034508-tsmc-q2-earnings-beat-expectations-raises-full-year-outlook-tradingkey), [SEC EDGAR 6-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001046179/000104617926000451/a2q26e_withguidancexfinal.htm), [TechPowerUp](https://www.techpowerup.com/350807/tsmc-reports-record-q2-2026-earning-results)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Priorities]]

TSMC reported Q2 revenue of NT$1,270.38 billion ($40.20bn, +33.7% YoY, +12.0% QoQ), net income NT$706.56bn (+77.4% YoY), diluted EPS $4.31 per ADR — a fifth consecutive record-profit quarter, gross margin 67.7%, operating margin 60.3%. Every headline number beat consensus. The market didn't care: TSMC simultaneously raised full-year capex guidance from $52-56bn to $60-64bn and announced an additional $100bn US investment in Arizona (bringing committed US spending to $265bn total), with 2026 USD revenue growth now guided above 40%. That combination — record results paired with an even steeper spending curve — is what tipped chip stocks further into the red rather than stabilising them: the market read "AI infrastructure still needs more capital than anyone modelled," which is a bullish-for-TSMC, bearish-for-everyone-else-financing-it signal. This resolves yesterday's open loop with the actual print; the follow-up question is whether Nvidia, Broadcom and the hyperscalers' own capex guides converge with TSMC's implied demand, or whether TSMC is simply capturing share while others get squeezed on margin.

## Story 2: Chip selloff deepens — Broadcom's cautious outlook, a memory crisis, and a smartphone demand scare compound the TSMC reaction

**Source:** [Kavout](https://www.kavout.com/market-lens/what-triggered-the-recent-semiconductor-sell-off), [Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/technology/ai/articles/nvidia-broadcom-amd-lead-ai-130643330.html), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/17/softbank-chip-stocks-asia-wall-street-ai-rout.html)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Micron and AMD are down more than 5% each, Broadcom off 5%, Arm down over 5%, and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) slid nearly 4%; in Asia today SoftBank sank more than 9%, tracking Wall Street's rout, with SK Hynix's US-listed shares down a further 13% on top of last week's slide. Three forces are compounding: a cautious AI chip demand outlook from Broadcom, a deepening HBM-driven memory shortage that's now bleeding into conventional DRAM pricing for PCs and phones, and a projected collapse in global smartphone demand as device-makers absorb higher memory costs. Analysts flag no meaningful supply relief before late 2027/2028. This is now a multi-week unwind rather than a single bad session — worth tracking against TSMC's capex signal above as the two live theories (financing squeeze vs genuine demand softening) compete for which one is actually driving price.

## Story 4: Chinese AI stocks retreat as the Wall Street rout crosses the Pacific, giving back some of this week's Apple-deal gains

**Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/17/softbank-chip-stocks-asia-wall-street-ai-rout.html), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/16/alibaba-baidu-shares-jump-apple-ai-partnership-.html)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Hong Kong-listed Tencent slipped 1.3%, Baidu eased 0.7% and Alibaba 1.3% today, tracking the Nasdaq's 1.47% Thursday drop as chip shares came under fresh pressure. The pullback follows a rally earlier this week after Apple secured Beijing's approval to roll out Apple Intelligence in mainland China using Alibaba's Qwen model (with Baidu also confirmed as a partner) — Alibaba had surged 9% and Baidu 5% on that news. Underlying fundamentals stayed strong regardless: Alibaba Cloud Intelligence posted $1.3bn quarterly revenue on triple-digit growth, Tencent grew net profit 21% on AI integration, and Baidu's AI business is now 52% of total revenue. Today's move reads as beta to the US chip selloff, not a reversal of the Apple-deal thesis.

## Story 5: NetApp acquires DataPelago — AI data-infrastructure consolidation continues

**Source:** [NetApp press coverage](https://techarena.ai/content/2026-ai-m-a-the-great-shift-from-models-to-infrastructure)
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

NetApp announced yesterday it has acquired DataPelago, a California-based AI data-infrastructure company known for eliminating processing bottlenecks in GPU-accelerated data pipelines. The deal folds GPU-accelerated data processing directly into NetApp's storage layer — part of a broader 2026 pattern of vertical integration in AI infrastructure, where incumbents and neoclouds are acquiring the plumbing needed to turn raw compute into a usable enterprise stack, rather than competing purely on model quality.
