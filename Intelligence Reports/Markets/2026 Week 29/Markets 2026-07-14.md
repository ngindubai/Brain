## Story 1: CPI, Warsh's House debut and bank earnings collide — JPMorgan beats, rest still landing

**Source:** Journal Gazette (AP wire) | https://www.journalgazette.net/business/jpmorgan-posts-strong-second-quarter-numbers-though-dimon-warns-of-tariff-geopolitical-risk/article_c69f4581-592e-51c4-9767-4a3e061c20d2.html ; TechTimes | https://www.techtimes.com/articles/320366/20260713/wall-street-q2-bank-earnings-june-cpi-collide-tuesday-rare-double-print.htm ; PBS NewsHour | https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/watch-live-fed-chair-kevin-warsh-testifies-on-monetary-policy-in-house-hearing
**Related vault notes:** [[Priorities]], [[Ventures]]

The three-way collision flagged yesterday happened. June CPI was due at 8:30am ET (consensus: headline down 0.1% month-on-month, pulling the annual rate to roughly 3.8-3.9% from May's 4.2%, driven by a ~10% June drop in pump prices tied to last month's since-collapsed Iran ceasefire; core seen holding near 2.85-2.9% year-on-year, still sticky). JPMorgan, Citigroup, Wells Fargo and Bank of America all reported before the open, with Goldman Sachs also in the mix — BlackRock reporting alongside the banks was flagged in some previews but is not itself a depository bank and typically prints on a separate day, so treat that inclusion with caution.

JPMorgan's numbers are confirmed: $14.2 billion second-quarter profit, adjusted EPS of $4.96 against a $4.48 estimate (down from $6.12 a year ago on tougher comps), markets revenue up 15% to $8.9 billion, net interest income up 2% to $23.3 billion, and total managed revenue of $45.7 billion beating forecasts. Jamie Dimon called the US economy resilient but flagged tariffs, worsening geopolitical conditions (a live one, given Hormuz) and elevated fiscal deficits as the risks that matter for the second half. Citigroup, Wells Fargo and Bank of America results had not cleared a clean confirmed source at time of writing — Citi was expected to post the group's strongest year-on-year EPS growth (~39-40%), Wells Fargo +12.3% EPS growth on +4.7% revenue.

The options market had priced this as a genuine event: implied moves of roughly ±3.6% on JPMorgan and Bank of America, ±5.5% on Wells Fargo and Citigroup, and ±6.0% on Goldman Sachs, based on at-the-money earnings straddles. That's the options/futures macro signal worth logging — this is a rare same-day pile-up of a top-tier data print, a debut Fed testimony and five-bank earnings, and implied vol was priced accordingly rather than complacently.

Warsh testifies before the House Financial Services Committee at 10am ET, his first appearance before Congress since taking the Fed chair. The CPI print lands roughly 90 minutes before he sits down, so he cannot dodge inflation questions — expect pointed exchanges on tariff pass-through, housing costs and crypto/bank-supervision policy. Outcome not yet in at time of writing; his Senate Banking testimony follows tomorrow, 15 July. For Orwell, the read-through is rates: a soft CPI print plus a hawkish Warsh would be a mixed signal on when GPU compute financing costs ease.

## Story 2: Trump's Hormuz blockade takes effect this afternoon, Brent posts biggest jump since 2020

**Source:** Axios | https://www.axios.com/2026/07/13/oil-trump-us-iran-blockade-strait-hormuz ; NPR | https://www.npr.org/2026/07/13/nx-s1-5891746/us-iran-strait-of-hormuz-updates ; Bloomberg (via web search) | https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-14
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The escalation flagged in yesterday's report has a hard deadline now: the US Navy confirmed its blockade of Iranian shipping through the Strait of Hormuz takes effect today, 14 July, at 4pm ET, with Trump also demanding a 20% reimbursement on all non-Iranian cargo transiting the strait. Brent closed above $83.30 on the move, a 9.6% one-day jump and its biggest single-day gain since May 2020; WTI closed near $78.14, up 9.4%. Kpler shipping data shows Hormuz crossings down more than 50% week-on-week. Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi has seized on Trump's reimbursement demand to justify Iran's own proposed transit fees, which risks entrenching a toll regime on roughly a fifth of global oil and gas trade rather than resolving it.

This is now the dominant macro variable feeding into today's CPI reaction and Warsh's testimony: a sustained oil spike works directly against the disinflation narrative built on June's gasoline-price drop, and post-ceasefire-collapse pricing has already partially unwound that drop. Worth flagging plainly: a blockade with a published start time and a 20% toll is a structural friction on shipping insurance and routing, not a one-day headline, and markets are already pricing it that way (see crypto story below).

## Story 3: TSMC's delayed June revenue lands — 68% growth, best month on record, beats own guidance

**Source:** CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/13/tsmc-june-revenue-rises-percent-ahead-second-quarter.html ; Digitimes | https://www.digitimes.com/news/a20260713VL213/tsmc-revenue-growth-demand-2026.html ; TSMC investor release | https://pr.tsmc.com/english/news/3323
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The delayed June revenue report flagged in yesterday's brief has now landed and it did not confirm slowdown fears — the opposite. June net revenue hit NT$442.68 billion (~$14.6 billion), up 6.2% month-on-month and 67.9% year-on-year, the best single month in the company's history. That lifts second-quarter revenue to roughly $39.6 billion, edging past the top of TSMC's own $39-40.2 billion guidance range, and first-half 2026 revenue is up 35.6% year-on-year. Full Q2 earnings, including margins and forward guidance, are due Thursday 16 July.

The signal worth sitting with: TSMC's actual order book shows no slackening in AI chip demand, even as Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom and Micron shares keep falling (see next story). That's either a lag — the equity selloff is pricing a 2027 capex pullback TSMC hasn't seen yet in bookings — or a decoupling between foundry economics and the fabless names that depend on end-customer capex announcements. Either way, it's the cleanest fundamental data point against the "AI trade is cooling" narrative that's been driving the chip stock rotation.

## Story 4: Chip stocks fall again — Nvidia, Micron, Broadcom, AMD all lower

**Source:** TradingKey | https://www.tradingkey.com/news/market-movers/262026926-market-movers-avgo-20260713 ; Yahoo Finance | https://finance.yahoo.com/technology/ai/articles/nvidia-broadcom-amd-lead-ai-130643330.html
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The AI chip selloff that started with Monday's SK Hynix slump continued into today's session. Micron fell nearly 6% in premarket trading, Broadcom was down 3.22% on the prior session and extending losses, and Nvidia and AMD both traded lower again, with Nvidia off roughly 3% intraday. The stated driver is a rotation out of high-growth semiconductor names into defensive sectors as investors weigh whether enterprise AI infrastructure spending is cooling, compounded today by the Hormuz-driven risk-off tone hitting the whole tech complex.

Set against Story 3 — TSMC's order book showing no such slowdown — this looks more like a valuation and macro-rate story than a demand story. Nvidia's forward multiple has compressed to roughly 18x earnings, below the S&P 500's ~21x and the cheapest the stock has traded since before the AI boom, per Bloomberg reporting from last week that's still the operative context. For a GPU compute venture, cheaper chip-adjacent equities and a still-strong TSMC order book is not obviously bad news — it depends whether it's compute deflation (cheaper GPUs, same demand) or genuine capex retrenchment. Today's data leans toward the former but isn't conclusive.

## Story 5: Chinese equities diverge sharply on Hormuz — mainland sells off, Hong Kong holds

**Source:** BBNTimes | https://www.bbntimes.com/global-economy/sse-composite-shanghai-benchmark-slumps-to-three-month-low-as-us-iran-tensions-rattle-chinese-equities ; Finimize | https://finimize.com/content/china-stocks-slid-as-hormuz-tensions-spooked-markets
**Related vault notes:** [[Comparison Network]]

Mainland China took the oil shock hard: the Shanghai Composite fell 2.1% to 3,913.79, a three-month low, and the Shenzhen Component dropped 3.5% to 14,522.85, both on direct exposure to China's heavy reliance on imported energy — a sustained oil spike hits input costs, inflation and corporate margins for a large net energy importer. Hong Kong's Hang Seng, by contrast, was roughly flat, up 0.1% to 24,193.10, showing far more resilience, likely reflecting its heavier weighting toward the AI/tech names that have been separately volatile but aren't as directly energy-import-exposed as mainland industrials.

This is a genuine divergence, not a uniform "China sells off" headline, and it's worth tracking because it cuts against the narrative that Chinese AI-stock strength (Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu rallies through early July) is a single unified trade — Hong Kong-listed tech and mainland-listed industrials are now trading on different macro drivers.

## Story 6: Crypto shrugs off the blockade — Bitcoin down under 2%, Fed policy now the dominant driver

**Source:** Blockhead | https://www.blockhead.co/2026/07/14/bitcoins-iran-discount/ ; CryptoSlate | https://cryptoslate.com/bitcoin-falls-below-63000-as-markets-price-a-97-chance-hormuz-stays-disrupted-through-july/
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

Bitcoin touched an intraday low near $62,500 before recovering into the $62,700-$63,100 range, a sub-2% decline on the day; Ethereum, XRP and Solana all posted similarly muted losses. That's a notably tame reaction to a live naval blockade and a fourth round of US strikes on Iran in six days. The read from crypto desks: spot Bitcoin ETF flows have made Fed policy the dominant price driver over geopolitical headlines, and the operative channel is inflation, not war risk directly — higher oil feeds CPI, reinforces a higher-for-longer rate path, tightens dollar liquidity, and that combination is what's bearish for BTC, not the strikes themselves. Markets are now pricing a 97% probability that Hormuz disruption persists through the end of July, which is itself notable: this is being treated as a structural repricing, not a headline spike to fade.
