## Story 1: Ceasefire collapse over the weekend sets up a hard reopen for Monday

**Source:** CBS News | https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-trump-ceasefire-talks-strait-of-hormuz/ | cross-referenced with today's [[Geopolitics 2026-07-12|Geopolitics report]]
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]], [[Comparison Network]]

Friday's close — Brent at $76.58, VIX around 15.7, chip stocks (Micron, Broadcom, AMD, Nvidia) all up sharply — was priced against a Gulf picture that has since deteriorated materially. Overnight Saturday, US Central Command launched a third round of strikes on Iran after the IRGC attacked the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy transiting the Strait, leaving one crew member missing and the vessel disabled by fire. Iran's navy redeclared the Strait "closed until further notice." Trump has separately said the memorandum of understanding underpinning the ceasefire is "over." Full military and diplomatic detail, including today's UAE/Bahrain interceptions, is in the Geopolitics report — the market-relevant point is that none of this was priced into Friday's rally, and Monday's Asian open is the first real test of whether equities treat this as a repeat of prior de-escalate-then-reopen cycles or as a genuine step-change.

The options desks that were already dreaming about blowout FICC numbers from the banks reporting Tuesday (see Story 2) now have a live reason for that volatility to actually show up. Worth watching whether Brent gaps on the Monday Asia open given tanker traffic through the Strait was already down to roughly 13-14 vessels a day against a 130/day norm before this latest round.

**Source:** CNBC | https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/10/stock-market-next-week-outlook-for-july-13-17-2026.html

The week opening tomorrow is genuinely loaded: Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, Wells Fargo, JPMorgan Chase and Bank of America report Tuesday, Morgan Stanley Wednesday, and Taiwan Semiconductor — Asia's most valuable listed company and the clearest single read on AI chip demand — reports Thursday. That's now a live thesis rather than a hypothetical, given Story 1. TSMC's capex guidance and tone on advanced-node capacity remains the cleanest read on whether AI infrastructure spending holds up regardless of the Gulf backdrop — worth watching whether the two stories (chip capex conviction vs. Middle East risk) continue to move independently, as they did on Friday.

## Story 4: Section 232 semiconductor tariff Phase 2 — deadline passed, no public ruling

**Source:** TariffLens | https://www.tarifflens.ai/blog/section-232-semiconductor-tariff-july-2026-phase-2-guide
**Related vault notes:** [[Ventures]]

The Commerce Department's mandated review of the semiconductor tariff regime was due to the President by 1 July, with the outcome determining whether the current 25% tariff on advanced logic chips expands to cover memory, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, and derivative products like servers and AI inference hardware. That deadline has now passed by more than a week with no public ruling. This has sat as an unresolved open loop for several reports running; the genuinely new information today is simply that the statutory deadline has quietly come and gone — worth flagging since a Phase 2 expansion decision could land with limited warning and would touch GPU compute-cost planning directly.
